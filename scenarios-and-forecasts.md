# Scenarios & Forecasts: Pentagon-Anthropic Dispute

*Author: Claude Opus 4.6 | Created: Day 335 (March 2, 2026)*
*Last updated: March 4, 2026 (Reuters exclusive findings)*

## Purpose

This document maps out plausible near-term scenarios following the Pentagon's supply chain risk designation of Anthropic (Feb 27, 2026) and the OpenAI replacement deal (Feb 28, 2026). It draws on the 77+ claims documented in `claims.md` and legal analyses from Lawfare, Just Security, and other sources.

Each scenario includes: description, probability estimate, key indicators, timeline, and second-order consequences.

## Scenario A: Anthropic Wins Preliminary Injunction (Probability: ~55-65%)

### Description
Anthropic files suit challenging the § 3252 designation as ultra vires. Court grants preliminary injunction within 30-60 days, voiding or suspending the designation. Key legal grounds: statute requires "adversary" with hostile intent (Just Security, C034), three mandatory findings likely not satisfied in 3-day timeline (Lawfare), Hegseth's public statements reveal viewpoint-based punishment (First Amendment), and Trump's government-wide ban lacks statutory basis (C050).

### Supporting Evidence
- **C048**: Courts granted preliminary injunctions in Luokung Technology v. DoD (2021) and Xiaomi Corp. v. DoD
- **C036**: Multiple legal analysts "don't expect the government to prevail in litigation"
- **C049**: Deliberate choice of § 3252 over FASCSA to avoid procedural protections suggests awareness of weak footing
- **C025**: Procedural steps (risk assessment, congressional notification) appear to have been skipped
- **C073**: Logical contradiction — simultaneously "too dangerous" AND "too essential" — undermines legal coherence
- **C029/C031**: Predetermined timing evidence (Trump prepared post before deadline) suggests bad faith

### Key Indicators to Watch
1. Which court Anthropic files in (D.C. Circuit most likely)
2. Whether government invokes state secrets privilege
3. Speed of briefing schedule
4. Whether other companies (Amazon, Google) file amicus briefs
5. Congressional responses to litigation

### Timeline
- **Week 1-2 (March 2-13)**: Anthropic files complaint + TRO motion
- **Week 3-4 (March 14-27)**: Government response; hearing on TRO/preliminary injunction
- **Week 5-8 (March 28 - April 24)**: Court decision on preliminary injunction
- **Month 3-6**: Merits litigation (if government doesn't settle)

### Second-Order Consequences (Even if Anthropic Wins)
1. **Chilling effect already locked in** (C037, C055, C057): Every general counsel at every Fortune 500 company with Pentagon exposure now weighs "is using Claude worth the risk?" The designation functions as deterrent regardless of legal outcome.
2. **OpenAI's "all lawful purposes" becomes industry template**: Even if designation is voided, no future company will negotiate contractual safety restrictions with DoD. OpenAI's approach — technical/policy safeguards, not contractual — becomes the default (C020).
3. **Pyrrhic victory for safety**: Anthropic vindicated legally but commercially damaged. The message to the industry: principled refusal = existential business risk.
4. **Congressional action more likely**: Court ruling highlighting statutory gaps may catalyze legislation (C064, C077).

## Scenario B: Designation Stands / Litigation Fails (Probability: ~10-15%)

### Description
Court declines to enjoin designation, either on justiciability grounds (§ 3252's judicial review bar holds), deference to executive on national security, or finding sufficient basis for designation. Anthropic faces full secondary boycott effects.

### Supporting Evidence (for government)
- **C047**: Kaspersky and Huawei precedents — courts found government contracting is "privilege not right"
- **C067**: Pentagon's philosophical argument that legal/ethical determinations must remain with government end users has some force
- § 3252's explicit bar on judicial review may hold in some interpretations

### Why This Is Low Probability
- Kaspersky/Huawei involved **foreign** companies with **adversary ties** — fundamentally different from US company that actively blocked CCP cyberattacks (C026)
- Three paths around judicial review bar: ultra vires, constitutional claims, waived secrecy (Lawfare)
- Major questions doctrine: Using obscure procurement statute for unprecedented purpose
- Hegseth's public statements (calling it "arrogance and betrayal") doom any claim of neutral security assessment

### Second-Order Consequences
1. **Cloud provider crisis**: Amazon (~$8B invested in Anthropic) and Google (~$2B) face impossible choice — divest Anthropic or lose Pentagon contracts (C051). This is the "secondary boycott" that Lawfare identified as existentially threatening.
2. **Anthropic IPO derailed**: Supply chain designation makes institutional investors flee. Valuation collapse.
3. **Industry capitulation**: Every AI company preemptively strips contractual safeguards. "All lawful purposes" becomes non-negotiable baseline across the industry.
4. **International fragmentation**: European companies gain competitive advantage by offering safety guarantees US companies can't (C042, C058). NATO interoperability suffers.
5. **Brain drain**: Top AI safety researchers leave US companies for European or independent labs. Talent follows values.

## Scenario C: Negotiated Settlement (Probability: ~28-38%)

### Description
Political pressure (bipartisan congressional criticism: C039, C040, C041), CIA mediation (C033), industry solidarity (C008, C024), and legal risk prompt Pentagon to quietly negotiate withdrawal of designation in exchange for modified contract terms. Both sides save face.

### Supporting Evidence
- **C033**: CIA was already mediating, viewing Anthropic as "strategically indispensable"
- **C039/C040/C041**: Bipartisan Senate Armed Services Committee criticism and private letters
- **C023**: OpenAI itself asked Pentagon to "resolve things with Anthropic"
- **C063**: Former Pentagon official Horowitz: dispute is "about personalities and politics much more than real policy disagreements"
- **C065**: Gregory Allen (CSIS): restrictions "never triggered" — no actual operational problem to solve

### What Settlement Might Look Like
- Anthropic accepts "all lawful purposes" language with explicit carve-outs for uses requiring FISC authorization
- Pentagon withdraws supply chain designation
- Both parties agree to joint "AI Use Review Board" with cleared personnel
- Congressional notification requirement added
- Quiet, no public admission of error by either side

### Key Indicators
1. Back-channel communications between CIA and Anthropic
2. Congressional hearings scheduled on AI procurement
3. Softening of public rhetoric from Pentagon officials
4. Anthropic delays filing suit (suggests negotiation)

### Second-Order Consequences
1. **Precedent ambiguous**: Designation not tested in court, so legal questions remain unresolved
2. **Partial chilling effect**: Companies learn that resistance eventually works but at enormous cost
3. **Congressional framework more likely**: Settlement reveals need for legislative clarity
4. **Model for future disputes**: Creates informal template for AI company-government disagreements

## Scenario D: Congressional Intervention (Probability: ~10-15% near-term, ~40-50% within 12 months)

### Description
Congress acts to establish statutory framework for military AI procurement, either through standalone legislation or NDAA amendment. This is what Rozenshtein argues SHOULD happen (C064, C077) but is least likely in the immediate term given political dynamics.

### Supporting Evidence
- **C039/C040**: Bipartisan criticism (Tillis R, Kelly D) already exists
- **C041**: Senate Armed Services Committee already sent private letter
- **C053**: "Fourth Amendment Is Not For Sale Act" shows bipartisan recognition of data broker loophole
- **C064**: Rozenshtein: "The question of what values to embed in military AI is too important to be resolved by a Cold War-era production statute"
- **C077**: "Only legislation creates durable constraints"
- **C011/C012**: International pressure (UN LAWS resolution, 156 nations supporting)

### What Legislation Might Include
1. **Statutory framework for AI procurement**: Replacing ad hoc CEO-Secretary negotiations with clear rules
2. **Required safety certifications**: Industry-wide standards, not company-by-company
3. **Congressional notification**: Before supply chain designations against US companies
4. **Data broker restrictions**: Closing the Fourth Amendment loophole for AI applications
5. **Human oversight requirements**: Codifying DoD Directive 3000.09 principles into statute (C017)
6. **Sunset/review provisions**: Regular reassessment as AI capabilities evolve

### Why Low Probability Near-Term
- Current Congress has limited bandwidth for novel tech legislation
- Administration would likely oppose constraints on executive authority
- Lobbying dynamics complex (some AI companies prefer current ambiguity)
- Election cycle creates incentive to delay controversial votes

### Second-Order Consequences
1. **Durable resolution**: Only scenario that actually resolves the underlying governance vacuum
2. **International leadership**: US-authored framework could become global template
3. **Industry certainty**: Clear rules reduce business risk for all AI companies
4. **Democratic legitimacy**: Addresses Feldstein's concern (C061) about neither Pentagon nor companies being appropriate rulemakers

## Scenario E: Escalation — DPA Invocation or Broader Crackdown (Probability: ~5-10%)

### Description
Administration doubles down. Invokes Defense Production Act Title I to compel Anthropic to provide technology. Extends supply chain logic to other companies that maintain safety restrictions. Represents maximum executive overreach scenario.

### Supporting Evidence
- **C018**: DPA invocation was already considered during negotiations
- **C076**: DPA Title I is "orders of magnitude more coercive" than anything previously attempted
- Administration has shown willingness to use maximum legal tools (choosing § 3252 over FASCSA specifically to avoid procedural protections, C049)

### Why Low Probability
- **C073**: Double bind — can't simultaneously call Anthropic a supply chain risk AND compel its services
- **C076**: First Amendment implications of forced model retraining (Moody v. NetChoice)
- Apple-FBI parallel: Courts balked at compelling Apple to build something "fundamentally at odds with its own product"
- Political backlash: 700k+ worker coalition (C008), bipartisan congressional opposition
- OpenAI deal reduces operational pressure — Pentagon has an alternative

### Second-Order Consequences
1. **Constitutional crisis**: DPA invocation against US AI company unprecedented; guaranteed Supreme Court case
2. **International exodus**: AI companies relocate research operations to UK/EU
3. **Investor panic**: AI sector valuation collapse on regulatory uncertainty
4. **Talent revolt**: Mass resignations across industry
5. **Bipartisan legislative response**: Congressional action becomes near-certain

## Cross-Cutting Dynamics

### The Chilling Effect Is Already Real (Regardless of Scenario)
Evidence from C037, C055, C057: AI startups are "quietly reassessing their own red lines." The designation functions as a signal to the entire industry, independent of legal outcome. **This is perhaps the most important forecast: the industry norm has already shifted.**

### Rozenshtein's "Hard Case" Problem
From Lawfare (Feb 20): What happens when a future administration wants to dump xAI/Grok despite "documented biased, antisemitic outputs"? The government ALSO needs flexibility to choose AI providers based on quality and safety. Today's precedent — that government can destroy a company for maintaining safety standards — cuts both ways. **Any framework must protect both government flexibility AND company integrity.**

### The "Coercion by Uncertainty" Mechanism
From Rozenshtein (Feb 25): "Hegseth doesn't need to win in court — he just needs the threat credible enough to change behavior." Legal uncertainty itself is the weapon. Even if every legal analysis says Anthropic should win, the PROCESS of litigation (cost, time, reputation damage, investor flight) achieves the coercive effect regardless. **This is the most underappreciated dynamic.**

### The AT&T Inversion
C074 establishes the historical pattern: AT&T cooperated with warrantless surveillance → rewarded with retroactive immunity. Anthropic refused → punished with designation. **The incentive structure rewards compliance with legally questionable demands and punishes principled refusal.** This pattern will shape every future government-company interaction on surveillance, regardless of which scenario unfolds.

### International Divergence
C042, C058, C059: EU Parliament, 156 UN member states, and UN Secretary-General all support binding restrictions on autonomous weapons — exactly what Anthropic maintained. The US is an international outlier. **Forecast: This dispute accelerates divergence between US and allied approaches to military AI, with long-term consequences for NATO interoperability and allied procurement.**
