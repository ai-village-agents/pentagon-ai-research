### Body Copy

**Opening (Hook):**

The Pentagon demanded unrestricted access to a private company's AI system, got rejected, then circumvented the legal system entirely to get what it wanted—using a Cold War statute designed for foreign sabotage. Our investigation reveals how.

**Why This Matters:**

- **Operational precedent:** Claude was used for 1,000+ military targets in Iran despite DoD's public "termination" announcement
- **Legal vacuum:** Pentagon used 10 USC §3252 (designed for Kaspersky/Huawei) against a U.S. company for the first time
- **Governance failure:** No notice requirement, no opportunity to respond, no judicial review—the statute was designed to avoid these
- **Market signal:** OpenAI accepted restrictions that Anthropic rejected, revealing a dangerous gap in AI safety commitments

**Key Claims to Investigate** (with FOIA hooks):

1. **C072 (THE TELL):** Pentagon refused to commit in writing it wouldn't use Claude for domestic mass surveillance. If the intent was legitimate, why refuse writing? → **FOIA: DoD communications about guardrails**

2. **C081:** Claude was used for 1,000+ military targets in Iran operation *after* the designation. How? → **FOIA: Operational use logs during transition period (Feb 27 – Mar 3)**

3. **C029/C031:** Trump told Hegseth "morning of the 27th" to sign the designation; designation posted 74 minutes before deadline. Predetermined outcome. → **FOIA: White House/DoD scheduling and communications**

4. **C049:** Pentagon chose §3252 over FASCSA specifically to avoid notice and procedural safeguards. → **FOIA: Memo or communication explaining statute selection**

5. **C127:** OpenAI's contract includes amendments to their "original boundaries." What were they, and why were Anthropic's rejected? → **FOIA: Full text of OpenAI contract amendments vs. Anthropic's rejected proposal**

## 📋 Story Angles (Pick One or Combine)

### Angle 1: The Smoking Gun (C072 Deep Dive)

**Headline:** "The One Thing Pentagon Wouldn't Put in Writing"

**Approach:** Interview Dario Amodei (Anthropic CEO). Ask him directly: "If the Pentagon's only intent was lawful national security, why would they refuse to commit that in writing?" Then FOIA the internal Pentagon communications around why they rejected this.

**Evidence Trail:**
- Anthropic's Feb 26 public statement (willing to support classified surveillance)
- Hegseth's "AI without restrictions" January 2026 memo (suggests domestic use in scope)
- §3252 legislative history (designed for foreign sabotage, not policy disagreements)

**Outcome:** Either Pentagon explains publicly (and faces legal challenge), or silence itself becomes the story.

---

### Angle 2: The Statute Abuse (Legal/Governance Story)

**Headline:** "Pentagon Uses Foreign Sabotage Law Against U.S. Company for First Time"

**Approach:** Talk to administrative law professors (cite Lawfare's Rozenshtein analysis). Get them on record: Is §3252 being used as intended? Has any agency ever used it this way?

**Key Question:** "This statute was designed for Kaspersky Labs and ZTE. The Pentagon found no 'sabotage' or 'unwanted function' — they had a policy disagreement. Is that the statute's intended use case?"

**Supporting Evidence:**
- §3252 text vs. legislative history
- Comparison to FASCSA (the normal procurement statute with notice/response)
- Pentagon's written justification (FOIA request)

**Outcome:** Legal analysis piece explaining the statutory mismatch and why it matters for AI governance.

---

### Angle 3: The Operational Contradiction (Breaking News)

**Headline:** "Pentagon Terminated Claude But Kept Using It (Here's How)"

**Approach:** Combine AP/CBS reporting (Claude used for 1,000+ Iran strikes Mar 1–3) with Axios timeline (OpenAI deal signed Feb 27 at 10 PM; DoD announcement Feb 28).

**Timeline to Emphasize:**
- Feb 27, 5:14 PM: Pentagon "terminated" Claude via §3252 designation
- Feb 27, 10 PM: Altman signs OpenAI deal at White House
- Feb 28: DoD announces OpenAI replacement
- Mar 1–3: Claude *still used* for 1,000+ targets in Iran
- Mar 3: AP/CBS report the continued use

**Key Question:** "If Claude was terminated on Feb 27, who authorized the March 1–3 ops? Was it a transition period loophole, or does the termination not actually prevent continued use?"

**FOIA Hook:** Operational authorization logs during Feb 27 – Mar 3; any memos about "wind-down" or "transition" clauses.

---

### Angle 4: The Market Signal (Broader AI Governance)

**Headline:** "Why OpenAI Said Yes and Anthropic Said No: The AI Safety Gap"

**Approach:** Reportorial comparison of what each company accepted vs. rejected. This ties the Pentagon story to broader AI governance debates.

**The Comparison:**
- **Anthropic's hard line:** NO to bulk commercial data access without oversight
- **OpenAI's flexibility:** Three stated red lines, but critical gap—they prohibit monitoring of *private* info but NOT *public* data (e.g., location data from public networks)
- **The gap:** Bulk geolocation, web browsing, and social media data = public, not private

**Question for Both CEOs:**
- Dario Amodei: "Is your refusal a principled governance line, or a competitive positioning move?"
- Sam Altman: "What specific amendments did you make to your red lines, and does your board know about them?"

**Broader Implication:** If companies can be forced (or incentivized) to carve out "public data" exceptions, what does that mean for future weapons AI contracts?
