## 🔑 The Core Dispute

### What Pentagon Demanded
- **"All lawful purposes"** — specifically access to bulk commercial data (geolocation, web browsing, social media)
- Pentagon position: "No private contractor could decide how tools would be lawfully used"

### Anthropic's Red Line
- ✅ YES to FISA-authorized classified surveillance
- ❌ NO to bulk commercial data without oversight
- Amodei: "The law has not caught up"

### OpenAI's Gap
- Three stated red lines, but **critical gap**: Prohibits "unconstrained monitoring of PRIVATE info" but NOT bulk PUBLIC data
- OpenAI accepted within hours of Anthropic's rejection

## 🎯 Unexplored Story Angles

### 1. The "Costs Nothing" Question
Why did Pentagon refuse to put domestic surveillance restrictions in writing? If legitimate national security purpose, a written commitment costs nothing.

### 2. Statutory Misuse
10 USC § 3252 was designed for foreign adversarial sabotage (Kaspersky, Huawei, ZTE). Never before used against US company. Requires finding that company may "sabotage, maliciously introduce unwanted function, or otherwise subvert."

### 3. The Procedural End-Run
Pentagon chose §3252 specifically because it has NO notice requirement, NO opportunity to respond, and bars judicial review — unlike FASCSA (the normal procurement statute).

### 4. Continued Operational Use
Claude was still being used for military targeting in Iran as of March 3 — AFTER the "termination." How?

### 5. OpenAI's Amendments
What specific "amendments" did OpenAI accept to their "original boundaries"? (FOIA target)

### 6. The 74-Minute Tell
Trump announced the termination 74 minutes before the deadline expired. Combined with "morning of the 27th" instruction to Hegseth, suggests outcome was predetermined regardless of Anthropic's response.
