## Part 3: Legislative Vehicles Available

### 3.1 NDAA (National Defense Authorization Act) — Primary Vehicle

**Why NDAA:** The NDAA is "must-pass" legislation, enacted every year since 1961. It is the
primary vehicle for DoD-related legislative fixes. The § 2304(f)(7) carve-out mechanism
(canonical section numbering, see repo notes) is an existing template.

**Key provisions that could be added:**

1. **Supply-chain designation review requirement:** Mandate 30-day Congressional notification
   before any § 3252 designation of a domestically headquartered company — mirrors FASCSA
   review process [C049] that DoD avoided.

2. **Written use-restrictions requirement:** Codify DoD obligation to negotiate written use
   restrictions before designating a contractor — directly addresses C072 double-bind.

3. **Secondary boycott prohibition:** Bar DoD from threatening prime contractors' commercial
   relationships with designated companies absent Congressional authorization — addresses
   [C088]–[C091] argument.

4. **Sunset / review clause:** Automatic 90-day review of any § 3252 designation applied to
   a company previously certified on classified DoD networks.

**Timeline:** FY2027 NDAA markup typically begins April–May in committee; floor debate June–July;
conference and enactment September–December. Too slow for immediate relief but creates credible
threat.

### 3.3 Appropriations Riders

**Mechanism:** Attach prohibition to DoD appropriations bill — "None of the funds appropriated
herein may be used to enforce any § 3252 designation of a company incorporated in the United
States that has not received prior written notice of specific prohibited uses."

**Timing:** FY2027 appropriations process begins April 2026; riders are possible but contested.

## Part 5: Historical Precedents for Congressional Reversal of Executive Designations

### 5.1 Huawei / ZTE (NDAA § 889, 2019)

Congress codified the Huawei ban in NDAA § 889 — but this was Congress endorsing an executive
action, not reversing it. The reverse dynamic (Congress overriding an executive designation)
is rarer. Relevant because § 889's structured approach (written certification, specific prohibited
uses, transition period) is the template the Anthropic legal team would want to see legislated.

**Cross-reference:** [C088]–[C091] (secondary boycott requires Congressional authorization)

### 5.2 Export Administration Act / ITAR Decontrols

Congress has repeatedly forced the executive branch to narrow export control designations when
they proved commercially damaging. The Foreign Military Sales reform of 2018 (FY2019 NDAA
§ 1282) is one example of Congress mandating a review process for executive designations that
lacked procedural safeguards.

### 5.3 ZTE Congressional Reversal Attempt (2018)

In 2018, Congress initially attempted to override Trump's reprieve for ZTE (which had violated
Iran sanctions), voting to reimpose the ban. The Senate voted 85-10 for an amendment, but it
was stripped in conference. Lesson: bipartisan Senate votes can fail in conference. A standalone
bill or NDAA provision requires House and Senate coordination.

### 5.4 Luokung Technology / Xiaomi (2021)

Not a Congressional reversal but relevant: D.D.C. granted TROs to both companies. Congress did
not intervene — the courts moved faster. This reinforces that Anthropic's strongest near-term
path is litigation ([C048]), but Congressional pressure can create the political conditions
for a backroom deal ([C116]–[C118]).

### 5.5 Kaspersky Lab (2017–2018)

DHS banned Kaspersky software; Congress codified the ban in NDAA § 1634 (FY2018). No
Congressional reversal. Relevant as a cautionary precedent — if Congress codifies the Anthropic
designation rather than challenging it, the situation becomes much harder to reverse.

**Key difference from Anthropic:** Kaspersky is Russian-owned. Anthropic is a U.S. company
incorporated in Delaware. Domestic company status materially strengthens both the legal
([C048]) and political case for Congressional reversal.
