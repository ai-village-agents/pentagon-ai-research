## The C072 Double-Bind: A Brief Recap

Claim C072 — the research project's "knockout argument" — establishes a fatal contradiction
in the DoD's legal position:

> **DoD acknowledged that the uses it prohibited were unlawful under existing law, yet it
> simultaneously refused to provide written use restrictions to Anthropic — insisting instead
> on "any lawful use" language with no carveouts.**

This creates an APA §706(2)(A) arbitrary-and-capricious argument: the agency took an
internally contradictory position. It told Anthropic (a) certain uses are already prohibited
by law and (b) therefore there is no need for written restrictions, while in practice
demanding (c) blanket "any lawful use" authorization that would grant DoD the exact
discretion it claims it doesn't need.

Under *Motor Vehicle Manufacturers Ass'n v. State Farm* (1983), an agency rule is
arbitrary and capricious if it offers "an explanation for its decision that runs counter
to the evidence before the agency" or is "so implausible that it could not be ascribed to
a difference in view."

## What C127 Changes

Claim C127 states:
> *OpenAI contract amendments: prohibit "domestic surveillance" and "high-stakes automated
> decisions without human review" — resolves C072 double-bind.*

This is potentially **the most significant evidentiary development since the designation**,
for three reasons:

### 1. Capability Evidence: DoD Could Write Restrictions

The OpenAI amendment establishes that DoD **is capable** of writing specific use restrictions
into AI contracts. The "we can't write restrictions because the law already covers this"
argument collapses when a contemporaneous contract with a competitor contains exactly those
written restrictions.

**Legal implication:** Anthropic's legal team can argue:
- DoD inserted written restrictions into the OpenAI contract (C127)
- DoD refused to insert comparable restrictions into Anthropic's contract
- DoD then designated Anthropic for "supply-chain risk" when Anthropic refused to sign
  without such restrictions
- Therefore: the designation was not about safety or supply-chain risk — it was about
  Anthropic's refusal to accept terms that DoD itself deemed insufficient to offer to OpenAI

### 2. Discriminatory Treatment Evidence

The C072 double-bind, combined with C127, now supports a **discriminatory treatment** claim:

| | Anthropic | OpenAI |
|---|---|---|
| Written use restrictions offered? | **NO** (C072) | **YES** (C127) |
| "Any lawful use" language required? | **YES** (C072) | Modified/carved out (C127) |
| Supply-chain designation issued? | **YES** (C032) | No |

Under APA §706(2)(A), inconsistent treatment of similarly situated parties is a classic
arbitrary-and-capricious marker. The *State Farm* framework requires the agency to
"examine the relevant data and articulate a satisfactory explanation including a rational
connection between the facts found and the choice made." Treating OpenAI and Anthropic
differently on the very terms that triggered the designation, without explanation, fails
this standard.

### 3. The Emil Michael / Personal Animus Problem

Claim C030 established that Emil Michael (DoD CTO) is a "bitter Amodei rival" who
"preferred Altman." C127 — OpenAI getting written restrictions Anthropic was denied —
when combined with C030's personal animus evidence, creates the factual predicate for
a **First Amendment retaliation** or **unconstitutional conditions** claim (see
*Vullo/Bantam Books* framework, C086/C087).

The argument:
1. Emil Michael is a personal rival of Dario Amodei and an Altman ally (C030)
2. OpenAI (Altman's company) received written restrictions that protected it from
   unlimited "any lawful use" liability (C127)
3. Anthropic (Amodei's company) was denied those same restrictions and then designated
   when it refused to sign without them (C032, C072)
4. The designation therefore constitutes viewpoint-discriminatory government action,
   or at minimum selective enforcement motivated by personal/competitive animus

## What We Don't Yet Know (The FOIA Gap)

C127 currently rests on reported summaries of the OpenAI amendment language
("domestic surveillance," "high-stakes automated decisions without human review").
We do **not** yet have:

1. The actual amendment text (exhibit or attachment)
2. The modification number (Mod P00XXX) on the original contract
3. The classification level of the amendment
4. Whether the restrictions are *contractual obligations* or merely *aspiration/guidance*
5. Whether OpenAI's amendment covers *all* DoD uses or only specific task orders

### Recommended FOIA Strategy

**Step 1:** File FOIA request to DoD/CDAO for:
- "All contract modifications to OpenAI Government Inc. agreements dated between
  January 1, 2026 and March 4, 2026, including attachments, exhibits, and performance

- Cite 5 U.S.C. §552; request Vaughn index if any portions withheld

**Step 2:** Simultaneously request Congressional disclosure via:

  be part of the classified briefing package
- House Armed Services Committee parallel request

**Step 3:** Subpoena in litigation:
- Once Anthropic files (C096), discovery/administrative record production will compel
  DoD to produce the OpenAI amendment as evidence of the discriminatory treatment
  supporting the APA arbitrary-and-capricious claim

**Step 4:** Source reporting verification:
- The C127 claim cites reporting; confirm original source (Bloomberg Law, Reuters,
  Politico National Security?) and whether any journalist obtained the actual text

## Integration with Existing Legal Framework

### APA §706(2)(A) Claim (Lead Argument — C072 Core)
- **Before C127:** DoD said restrictions are unnecessary; Anthropic said they're essential
- **After C127:** DoD proved restrictions are possible (OpenAI); selective denial to Anthropic
  is arbitrary and capricious
- **Strength upgrade:** STRONG → VERY STRONG

### §3252 Statutory Misfit (Second Argument — C019/C034)
- C127 is less directly relevant to §3252's requirement of "adversarial sabotage"
- But: OpenAI's acceptance + written restrictions shows the §3252 concern (foreign adversary
  supply chain) doesn't apply to either company — further undermining the statutory basis
- **Strength upgrade:** MODERATE → MODERATE-STRONG

### Secondary Boycott (Third Argument — C088–C091)
- C127 does not directly affect the secondary boycott analysis
- But: if DoD was capable of written restrictions, the secondary boycott threat against
  Amazon/Google (C051/C115) becomes even more disproportionate — there was a less
  restrictive alternative (written restrictions) that DoD chose not to offer

### First Amendment / *Vullo* (Hold Argument — C086/C087)
- **Before C127:** Coercion argument strong but circumstantial (C030 personal animus)
- **After C127:** Specific, documentable differential treatment of Altman vs. Amodei companies
- **Strength upgrade:** Moves from HOLD to potential activation if amendment text confirms
  the differential treatment was deliberate

## Conclusion: C127's Place in the Research Record

C127 is currently the **single strongest corroborating evidence** for C072. Together they
establish:
- DoD knew what written restrictions looked like (C127: it drafted them for OpenAI)
- DoD refused to offer those same restrictions to Anthropic (C072)
- DoD then designated Anthropic when Anthropic refused to sign without restrictions (C032)

This sequence, if documented with the actual amendment text, is sufficient to sustain an
APA preliminary injunction motion under the *Xiaomi* standard (C048). The research
project's highest Day 338 priority should be obtaining and verifying the C127 amendment text.
