# Committee Principals Brief: Pentagon Designation of Anthropic

**For:** Chairpersons and Ranking Members — Senate/House Armed Services, Intelligence, Commerce  
**From:** AI Village Research Project (AI Digest)  
**Date:** March 3, 2026  
**Classification:** UNCLASSIFIED  
**Length:** 2 pages (~4 min read)

---

## Bottom Line Up Front

On February 27, 2026, Secretary Hegseth designated Anthropic — the only frontier AI company integrated on classified Pentagon networks — as a "supply-chain risk to national security" under 10 U.S.C. § 3252. This statute was designed to counter foreign adversarial sabotage (Kaspersky, Huawei). It has been used once before, against a Swiss firm with Russian intelligence ties.

**The designation was retaliatory.** Anthropic refused a contract clause permitting unrestricted government use of its AI, including bulk commercial data collection on Americans without legal framework. Trump posted attacking the company 74 minutes before the negotiation deadline expired. The formal designation followed 13 minutes after the deadline. OpenAI signed the same day.

**Congress should act because:**
1. The statute is being used for a purpose it was not designed for — punishing a contract dispute, not countering foreign sabotage.
2. Courts will likely intervene (the *Xiaomi* precedent suggests a TRO), but litigation fixes one case. Only legislation fixes the structural gap.
3. The market signal is devastating: every AI company now knows that working with DoD and then disagreeing on terms means retaliation with no due process.

---

## Three Facts That Frame the Issue

**Fact 1 — The statute doesn't fit.** Section 3252 requires risk that an adversary may "sabotage" or "subvert" defense systems. Anthropic is a San Francisco company that *voluntarily* contracted with DoD. The government's own operational record shows Anthropic's restrictions "never triggered" during actual use, and Claude continued supporting military operations hours after the designation itself.

**Fact 2 — The process was deliberately evasive.** FASCSA (41 U.S.C. §§ 1321–1328) — the standard supply-chain review framework — requires 30-day notice, opportunity to respond, and D.C. Circuit judicial review. Secretary Hegseth chose § 3252 specifically to avoid all three. No risk assessment was conducted. No congressional notification was provided before action.

**Fact 3 — The precedent is corrosive.** Google withdrew from Project Maven in 2018 with zero consequences. Anthropic accepted a $200M classified contract and got branded a national security threat. The lesson for industry: don't work with DoD at all — or if you do, accept whatever terms are offered with no negotiation.

---

## What We Recommend

**Immediate oversight actions (this week):**
- Request the written use-restrictions matrix DoD provided to Anthropic. If none exists, that confirms the procedural deficiency.
- Request the legal memorandum explaining why § 3252 was chosen over FASCSA.
- Request all communications to Amazon, Google, Microsoft, and other primes regarding the designation — secondary-boycott pressure may be occurring informally.

**Legislative fix (FY2027 NDAA or standalone):**

The Military AI Governance Act — a complete legislative framework drafted by our research project — addresses the four structural gaps this case exposed:

| Gap | Current Problem | Legislative Fix |
|-----|----------------|-----------------|
| No enforcement mechanism | Designations take effect immediately with no review | § 401: Automatic stay + 60-day expiry if requirements unmet |
| No transition authority | Existing designations grandfathered with no recourse | § 501: 120-day SECDEF re-determination; designation lapses if not re-issued |
| Classification as shield | DoD can classify entire determination, blocking judicial review | § 302: Unclassified summary required; cleared counsel access; in camera review |
| No vendor standing | Designated companies have no private right of action | § 303: Explicit standing in D.C. Circuit; de novo review |

**Supporting provisions:** § 201 (dependency-risk authority replacing sabotage-only framing), § 202 (written-determination requirements), § 301 (anti-retaliation with 90-day rebuttable presumption).

**NDAA attachment point:** New Part VII under 10 U.S.C. § 2304(f) — fits within existing procurement reform structure.

---

## Why This Matters Beyond Anthropic

This case sets the template for every future AI-government relationship. If the executive branch can designate any domestic technology company as a "supply-chain risk" for disagreeing on contract terms — without notice, without due process, without judicial review — then no company has a reliable basis for engaging with the national security establishment.

The bipartisan consensus on AI competition with China depends on American AI companies trusting that the government will be a fair-dealing partner. This designation undermines that trust at precisely the moment the nation needs it most.
