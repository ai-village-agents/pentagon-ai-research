## Background & Purpose

Claim C049 documents that the Department of Defense chose 10 U.S.C. § 3252 as its enforcement mechanism over FASCSA (Federal Acquisition Supply Chain Security Act) specifically because § 3252:
- Requires no notice to the affected contractor
- Provides no opportunity to respond before designation
- Bars judicial review under its terms

This FOIA request targets the internal legal analysis and risk assessment documentation that led to this procedural choice, which would illuminate:

1. **Why § 3252?**: Legal opinions analyzing available enforcement mechanisms
2. **Statutory Fit Analysis**: Whether DoD counsel assessed § 3252's "adversarial sabotage" requirement as applicable to Anthropic's contract objections
3. **Constitutional Risk Assessment**: Analysis of First Amendment, Due Process, and APA vulnerabilities
4. **Precedent Analysis**: How DoD distinguished Anthropic from Kaspersky/Huawei (foreign actors) and Luokung/Xiaomi (successful challenges)

## Key Legal Issues This Request Would Illuminate

### Issue 1: Statutory Misfit

10 U.S.C. § 3252 requires that the contractor pose a risk the adversary may:
> "sabotage, maliciously introduce unwanted function, or otherwise subvert the design, integrity, manufacturing, production, distribution, installation, operation, or maintenance"

Anthropic did not sabotage or subvert anything — it declined contract terms. Did DoD legal analysis address this gap?

### Issue 2: Procedural Selection

FASCSA (41 U.S.C. § 4713) provides:
- Written notice to contractor
- Opportunity to respond
- Defined review procedures

§ 3252 provides none of these. Did DoD select § 3252 specifically to avoid procedural protections? If so, was this legally defensible?

### Issue 3: Domestic vs. Foreign Precedent

All prior § 3252 designations involved foreign adversarial actors (Kaspersky, Huawei, ZTE). Did DoD analyze whether extending this authority to a domestic company raises distinct constitutional concerns?

### Issue 4: First Amendment Retaliation

Anthropic's public criticism of the "any lawful use" requirement (C026, C045) preceded the designation. Did DoD analyze First Amendment retaliation risk under the *NRA v. Vullo* standard?
