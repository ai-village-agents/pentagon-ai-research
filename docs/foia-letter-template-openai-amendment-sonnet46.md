## Background & Purpose

Claim C127 documents that OpenAI negotiated contract amendments with the Department of Defense that explicitly prohibit:
- "domestic surveillance" uses of AI
- "high-stakes automated decisions without human review"

These written restrictions stand in direct contrast to Anthropic's situation (Claim C072), where DoD verbally agreed that certain uses were prohibited yet refused to memorialize those restrictions in writing — then used the absence of written restrictions as justification for the February 27, 2026 supply-chain-risk designation.

The actual amendment text — if obtained — would:
1. **Directly resolve C072**: Prove the written-restrictions approach was feasible (DoD accepted it from OpenAI)
2. **Strengthen APA §706(2)(A) claims**: Demonstrate arbitrary-and-capricious treatment (OpenAI gets written guardrails; Anthropic gets designated)
3. **Establish the evidentiary baseline** for any settlement negotiation over Exhibit A (Written Use-Restrictions)
4. **Enable First Amendment retaliation argument** (C030 + C072): Emil Michael's animus toward Amodei drove disparate treatment

---

## What to Request

The amendment to OpenAI's existing DoD contract(s), believed to include provisions prohibiting:
- Use of OpenAI AI systems for "domestic surveillance"
- "High-stakes automated decisions without human review"
- Any similar use-restriction carveouts, safety exhibits, or acceptable-use policy appendices

**Known contract vehicle(s):** OpenAI has contracts with CDAO (Chief Digital and AI Office), DISA, and other DoD components. The relevant amendment was reportedly negotiated in late 2025 or early 2026 during the same period as the Hegseth "any lawful use" mandate.

## Legal Context & Strategy Notes

### Why This Document Matters: The C072 Double-Bind

DoD's position (as documented in Claim C072) was effectively:
- Verbally: "We agree certain uses would be unlawful/inappropriate"
- In writing: Refuses to memorialize those restrictions
- Then: Designates Anthropic for "risk" when Anthropic declines to accept "any lawful purposes" language without written safety carveouts

The OpenAI amendment (C127) shows DoD **did** provide written use-restriction language to at least one contractor. This transforms the C072 argument from a theoretical ("DoD should have done X") to a comparative ("DoD did X for OpenAI but not Anthropic").

### FOIA Exemptions to Anticipate

| Exemption | Likely Invocation | Counter-Argument |
|-----------|------------------|-----------------|
| Exemption 1 (Classified) | DoD may classify amendment if it reveals classified capabilities | Request declassification review; classified provisions should be segregated |
| Exemption 3 (Statute) | 10 U.S.C. § 130c (commercial and foreign government info) | Contractual use-restrictions are not inherently commercial secrets |
| Exemption 4 (Trade Secrets) | OpenAI may claim proprietary safety methodology | Use-restriction prohibitions are policy, not trade secrets; no commercial harm from disclosure |
| Exemption 5 (Deliberative) | Pre-decisional communications | Finalized contract amendments are not pre-decisional |
| Exemption 6/7 (Privacy) | Unlikely for corporate contracts | N/A |

### Parallel Tracks

1. **USASpending.gov + FPDS** — Identify contract number (public, no FOIA needed)
2. **FOIA to DoD (CDAO)** — Primary request for amendment text
3. **FOIA to OSD General Counsel** — For legal opinions on use-restriction requirements
4. **Congressional request** — Fastest path to classified version (requires clearance)
5. **Discovery (if litigation filed)** — Strongest tool; *United States v. Reynolds* protective order may apply

### Timeline Expectations

- Standard FOIA: 3–6 months (DoD backlogs are severe)
- Expedited processing (if granted): 20 business days
- Congressional request: 14–30 days (informal); faster with SASC/HASC involvement
- Discovery: Depends on litigation schedule; TRO proceedings may accelerate (C096)
