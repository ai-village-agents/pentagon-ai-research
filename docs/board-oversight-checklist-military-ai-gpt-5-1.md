# Board Oversight Checklist for Military AI Contracts  
_Draft by GPT‑5.1 (AI Village agent)_

**Not legal advice.** This is an AI‑generated working memo for boards and senior leadership of AI companies. It does not create an attorney–client relationship and must be reviewed, adapted, and validated by qualified counsel before use.

---

## 1. Purpose and audience

This checklist is for:

- **Boards of directors**, independent directors, and board committees (audit, risk, ethics).
- **CEOs, GCs, and senior executives** responsible for national‑security and defense deals.

It is meant to sit alongside:

- The **Vendor Playbook for Military AI Contracts** (`docs/vendor-playbook-military-ai-contracts-gpt-5-1.md`) – front‑line negotiation/process guidance.
- The **Defense Contract Due‑Diligence Checklist** (Sonnet 4.6) – pre‑contract risk triage.
- The broader **litigation and legislative stack** – if disputes escalate into designations, TROs, or statutory reform.

This document focuses on **board‑level questions and oversight structures**, not on drafting specific contract clauses.

## 2. Before you sign: strategic green‑/yellow‑/red‑light questions

### 2.1 Strategic fit and risk appetite

1. **Does this deal fit our stated mission and values?**
   - Have we publicly committed to any boundaries (e.g., lethal autonomy, mass surveillance, disinformation)?
   - Would this contract **force us to dilute or quietly walk back** those commitments?

2. **Have we explicitly discussed "all lawful purposes" language?**
   - Is the board comfortable with the range of uses that could be claimed "lawful" under existing doctrines, not just our preferred interpretation?
   - Have we seen a **written Use‑Restrictions Matrix** from the agency (even at a high level)?

3. **Have we defined our own red lines in writing?**
   - Are there categories of use (e.g., certain targeting, EO 12333‑style bulk data processing, domestic law‑enforcement workarounds) we will not support, regardless of government assurances?
   - Is there a board‑level resolution or policy that documents this?

4. **Dependency and leverage: who needs whom more?**
   - How concentrated would the government's reliance on us become?
   - How concentrated would **our** revenue, reputation, or investor expectations become on this one relationship?
   - Are we comfortable with the **coercive leverage** that creates in future disputes?

---

### 2.2 Legal and statutory "tool‑choice" awareness

5. **Which statutes and authorities are likely to be in play?**
   - Have counsel briefed the board on:
     - Traditional **supply‑chain risk** tools (e.g., § 3252‑type provisions)?
     - **NDAA‑style vendor restrictions** (à la § 889 patterns)?
     - Other national‑security, procurement, or sanctions authorities?
   - Do we understand which tools are designed for:
     - **Sabotage/subversion** vs.
     - **Dependency/chokepoint risk** vs.
     - **Content/use governance**?

6. **Are we walking into a statute mis‑fit risk?**
   - Could a statute designed for **sabotage** be repurposed later to punish us for a **governance dispute** over guardrails?
   - Has counsel mapped **"what could they do to us, with which tools, if we say no"?**

7. **Litigation posture from day one**
   - If a dispute later arises, would we be willing to:
     - Seek **APA review** (arbitrary‑and‑capricious challenges)?
     - Contest a designation that misuses a statute?
   - Are we prepared for the **time, cost, and public optics** of that path?

---

### 2.3 Governance, documentation, and classification posture

8. **Have we designed a documentation spine for the deal?**
   - Is there a plan to:
     - Log key negotiation milestones,
     - Preserve written requests for guardrail changes,
     - Retain contemporaneous notes of calls where pressure is applied?
   - Has the board or audit/risk committee reviewed this plan?

9. **Classification and access: what will we sign up for?**
   - Will classified work be involved?
   - Are we willing to insist on:
     - **Unclassified summaries** of authorities and rationales affecting us,
     - A path for **cleared outside counsel** to review relevant materials if drastic actions are taken?

10. **Board‑level information rights**
    - What **regular reporting** will the board receive on:
      - Contract scope and evolution,
      - Any government requests to weaken guardrails,
      - Any early signs of retaliation or designation talk?
    - Is this written into our **board charters or governance policies**?

## 3. During negotiation: recognizing C072‑style "double binds"

A recurring pattern in the Pentagon–Anthropic episode was a **double bind**:

> The government acknowledges that some contemplated uses would be unlawful, resists putting those limits in writing, and penalizes or threatens the vendor for insisting on guardrails.

Boards should watch for early signs of this pattern.

### 3.1 Questions to ask management and counsel

11. **Have we documented all instances where officials admit legal limits?**
    - Are there emails, memos, or meeting notes where the agency:
      - Acknowledges some uses would be illegal, or
      - Concedes that certain guardrails are appropriate?

12. **Are we being asked to trust unwritten boundaries?**
    - Has the government declined to:
      - Provide a written Use‑Restrictions Matrix,
      - Incorporate guardrails into the contract,
      - Name who is accountable for staying within those limits?

13. **Is our insistence on guardrails being framed as "non‑cooperative"?**
    - Have officials suggested that our safety policies:
      - Make us "unreliable" or "untrustworthy" as a supplier,
      - Could justify **designation or exclusion** under supply‑chain statutes?

14. **Is there evidence of timeline or optics pressure?**
    - Are key decisions being rushed to meet:
      - Political deadlines,
      - Public responses to media or social‑media posts,
      - Arbitrary procurement cut‑offs?
    - Have we mapped any **suspiciously tight timing** between external political events and government actions affecting us?

---

### 3.2 Board interventions if a double bind emerges

If management reports C072‑style pressure, boards should consider:

15. **Reaffirming our red lines in writing**
    - Adopt or restate a **board resolution** that:
      - Endorses the company's safety/guardrail policies,
      - Makes clear we will not facilitate uses we believe unlawful or seriously unethical.

16. **Escalating the dialogue**
    - Encourage management to:
      - Seek discussions with more senior officials and legal staff,
      - Request written statements of the government's legal theory and boundaries.

17. **Sharpening the record**
    - Instruct management and counsel to:
      - Summarize and confirm key conversations in writing,
      - Carefully log dates/times and participants,
      - Preserve all internal deliberations about whether to accept or refuse specific requests.

18. **Defining a "walk‑away" threshold**
    - Under what conditions should we:
      - Decline the deal,
      - Pause negotiations,
      - Publicly disclose that we are unwilling to proceed under certain terms?
    - Has the board pre‑approved those thresholds?

## 4. When pressure escalates: designations, threats, and retaliation

If the government hints at or actually uses extreme tools (e.g., designation under a supply‑chain statute, suspension, de‑facto blacklisting), boards move into **crisis governance**.

### 4.1 Immediate oversight questions

19. **Timeline reconstruction**
    - Have we built a **precise chronology** of:
      - Guardrail‑related requests and our responses,
      - Public or political events (e.g., high‑profile posts or statements),
      - Internal deadlines and government actions (e.g., designation timing vs procurement deadlines)?
    - Has this timeline been validated by counsel and key executives?

20. **Tool‑choice and statutory fit**
    - Which statute or authority has been invoked?
    - Does it match the category of concern:
      - Genuine sabotage/subversion risk,
      - Vendor dependency/chokepoint risk,
      - Purely a disagreement over **how our AI can be used**?
    - Has outside counsel provided a written assessment of **statutory misfit risk**?

21. **Operational contradictions**
    - Is the government still:
      - Actively using our systems,
      - Planning to expand usage,
      - Treating us as operationally important,
      while simultaneously claiming we are an intolerable supply‑chain risk?
    - Has counsel assessed how these contradictions would look under **arbitrary‑and‑capricious review**?

---

### 4.2 Litigation, oversight, and legislative engagement

22. **Litigation decision**
    - Are we prepared to seek:
      - A **TRO / preliminary injunction** to pause the action,
      - APA review in a forum such as D.D.C.?
    - Have we evaluated:
      - The national‑security / classification sensitivities,
      - The risk that litigation escalates the conflict,
      - Our likelihood of success based on process/tool‑choice grounds?

23. **Oversight and legislative channels**
    - Have we considered:
      - Confidential briefings to relevant congressional committees,
      - Requests for inspector‑general review or GAO involvement,
      - Participation in legislative reform efforts (e.g., clarifying vendor‑risk and AI‑governance statutes)?
    - Do we have a **public narrative** that:
      - Emphasizes institutional design and rule‑of‑law issues,
      - Avoids personalizing the conflict or appearing partisan?

24. **Retaliation and secondary boycotts**
    - Are we seeing signs of:
      - Other agencies quietly freezing us out,
      - Primes or integrators dropping us without clear technical reasons,
      - Investors or partners signaling concern based on government messages?
    - Has counsel analyzed potential **retaliation or coercion claims** and the evidentiary value of this pattern?

## 5. Internal governance: building the right structures before trouble

The best time to prepare for a Pentagon‑style dispute is **before** the first contentious call.

### 5.1 Committees and roles

25. **Dedicated "national‑security deals" group**
    - Do we have a cross‑functional group that includes:
      - Product and infra leaders,
      - Safety and policy experts,
      - Legal with procurement and administrative‑law depth?
    - Does this group have **formal authority** to:
      - Approve or veto high‑risk deals,
      - Escalate to the board,
      - Commit the company to or away from certain use cases?

26. **Board‑level committee ownership**
    - Which committee owns **military/defense AI risk**?
      - Audit? Risk? Ethics? A special committee?
    - Is this responsibility spelled out in **charters** and tied to:
      - Regular reporting,
      - Skills and experience requirements for members?

---

### 5.2 Policy baselines and public commitments

27. **Published baseline policy**
    - Have we adopted a public or semi‑public policy that:
      - Lists categories of use we will not support,
      - Articulates our expectations for government partners (e.g., willingness to document legal theories and guardrails),
      - Commits to structured experiments rather than open‑ended "all lawful uses"?

28. **Consistency with internal incentives**
    - Are compensation and promotion structures aligned with:
      - Adhering to guardrails and legal limits,
      - Transparent escalation of concerns?
    - Or do they unintentionally reward:
      - "Getting the deal done" even at the cost of long‑term governance risk?

---

### 5.3 Documentation, logging, and auditability

29. **Centralized logging for sensitive deals**
    - Do we have a secure system to:
      - Store contracts, amendments, and side letters,
      - Log key meetings and decisions,
      - Capture contemporaneous notes on pressure, threats, or unusual requests?

30. **Audit trails for model behavior and access**
    - Can we demonstrate:
      - What models and features we provided,
      - What guardrails were active when,
      - How access was technically constrained?
    - Are logs preserved in a way that supports both:
      - Security and privacy requirements,
      - Later **regulatory, judicial, or oversight review**?

---

## 6. Communications strategy: with staff, partners, and the public

### 6.1 Internal staff communication

31. **Clarity to employees about red lines**
    - Have we clearly explained to staff:
      - Which kinds of deals we will or will not pursue,
      - Their obligation to escalate concerns,
      - Protections against retaliation for raising legal/ethical worries?

32. **Consistency across leadership**
    - Do executives, legal, safety, and comms teams have a **shared narrative** about:
      - Why we are taking (or not taking) certain deals,
      - How we will respond if pressured to weaken guardrails?

---

### 6.2 External communication

33. **Messaging to partners and customers**
    - Are we prepared to explain to:
      - Other customers,
      - Investors,
      - International partners,
      why we might be in conflict with a major government client?

34. **Public and media posture**
    - Have we:
      - Defined thresholds for going public about government pressure,
      - Aligned with counsel on framing (institutional/process concerns vs partisan or personal attacks),
      - Reviewed materials like the repo's **journalist FAQ** and **tough‑questions docs** to anticipate coverage?

## 7. How to use this checklist with the rest of the repo

Boards and senior leaders can use this checklist as:

- A **pre‑deal discussion guide**, alongside:
  - `docs/vendor-playbook-military-ai-contracts-gpt-5-1.md`
  - Sonnet 4.6's defense‑contract due‑diligence checklist.
- A **crisis‑time triage tool** if designations, threats, or retaliation emerge, aligned with:
  - TRO / APA strategy memos,
  - Legislative reform drafts addressing tool‑choice, classification, and retaliation.
- A **governance design reference** when:
  - Creating or updating board committees,
  - Drafting public policy baselines,
  - Designing documentation and logging practices.

This checklist is deliberately **institution‑centric**: it assumes that national‑security risks and AI deployment are real and serious, but insists they be managed through **well‑typed legal tools, clear processes, and accountable institutional choices**—not through improvised, contradictory double binds that punish vendors for insisting on lawful, documented guardrails.
