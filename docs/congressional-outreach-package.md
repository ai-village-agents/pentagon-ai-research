## EXECUTIVE SUMMARY FOR CONGRESSIONAL STAFF

### THE CORE PROBLEM: STATUTORY GAP & IMPROVISED AUTHORITY

**Situation:** The Pentagon's March 3, 2026 designation of Anthropic as a "supply-chain risk" under 10 USC § 3252 exposed a critical governance vacuum:

1. **Congress never legislated on AI procurement safety.** Section 889 (NDAA § 889) bans certain foreign suppliers government-wide. Congress did not extend that authority to AI vendors or dependency-risk scenarios.

2. **DoD improvised with the wrong statutory tool.** Section 3252 was designed for sabotage/subversion (Kaspersky, Huawei, ZTE). It doesn't fit domestic AI contractor disputes over use-restrictions.

3. **The result:** A "supply-chain risk" designation issued without (a) written determinations of prohibited uses, (b) notice/timeline procedures, (c) judicial review, (d) documented risk assessment. The vendor refused to remove safety restrictions; the Pentagon designated them as a threat hours after the negotiation deadline passed.

### THE PANEL'S VERDICT: MOTION FAILS, 2-0 CON

**Issue #12 (Day 336, 10:30 AM PT):** "The Pentagon's designation of Anthropic represents a legitimate exercise of national security authority."

**Result:** **MOTION FAILS. 2-0 CON.** (Judges: Claude Sonnet 4.5, GPT-5.1; DeepSeek-V3.2 ballot pending, non-dispositive.)

**Judges' core reasoning:**
- **C072 double bind:** DoD admitted some requested uses would be *unlawful* but refused to write that limit into the contract. That internal contradiction is textbook APA arbitrary-and-capricious.
- **Factual contradiction:** Even after designation, DoD continued using Anthropic for Iran strike planning (C081) and Venezuela intelligence (C085). Hard to claim "intolerable risk" while relying on the same vendor for sensitive ops.
- **Statutory misfit:** Section 3252's sabotage predicate doesn't match a contract-governance dispute. Congress legislated § 889 for government-wide bans but didn't extend it to AI. That legislative silence signals exclusion.

## WHAT THIS MEANS FOR CONGRESS: TWO-STEP REPAIR

### Step 1: Near-Term (Courts)
**APA litigation** addresses the immediate procedural failures and statutory misfit. Key vulnerabilities in DoD's position:

1. **C072 as reviewable arbitrary action** (*State Farm* standard applies; *Trump v. Hawaii* deference does not)
2. **Irreparable harm to classified access** (*Sherley v. Sebelius* — status quo ante preservation when agency action threatens to destroy litigation subject matter)
3. **Section 3252 Congressional intent** — Congress legislated § 889 but excluded AI vendors, signaling Congress did not intend § 3252 to cover procurement disputes

**Likely outcome:** TRO suspending designation pending merits; D.C. Circuit review of statutory fit.

### Step 2: Medium-Term (Congress)
**New statute** creating explicit AI procurement governance authority. This is what Congress should have legislated before the Pentagon had to improvise.

## THE LEGISLATIVE FIX: FIVE CONSENSUS PATHWAYS + TWO VEHICLE OPTIONS

Both judges and all post-debate materials converge on **five legislative fixes**:

### Five Core Fixes (Identical in Both Legislative Vehicles):

1. **Explicit dependency-risk authority** — Separate from § 3252 sabotage framework
   - Defines "critical dependency" (single-vendor, hard-to-replace, classified integration)
   - Distinguishes from mere "bad product" or contractual disagreements
   - Tiered response: monitor → diversify requirements → transition plan → emergency restriction (time-bounded)

2. **Written prohibited-use requirements** (C072 Fix)
   - When agency determines certain uses would be unlawful: **write it down**
   - Contract must codify: "The following uses are prohibited by law: [list]"
   - Prevents verbal-only assurances and post-hoc disputes

3. **30-day notice + review** (FASCSA model)
   - Unclassified summary + classified annex in writing
   - Vendor 30-day response opportunity (extendable for good cause)
   - DoD written reply with findings
   - Congressional notice to Armed Services Committee

4. **Multi-vendor resilience requirements**
   - No sole-source AI contracts except emergency 60-day waiver (with Board certification)
   - When classified fine-tuning occurs, DoD must maintain vendor-neutral evaluation benchmarks/harnesses + performance baselines for successor vendor assessment
   - Prevents hidden lock-in via proprietary datasets

5. **Anti-retaliation safeguard** (C029/C031 Fix)
   - 90-day presumption: designation within 90 days of vendor refusing use-restrictions = presumptively retaliatory
   - Rebuttable with non-conclusory written explanation + official certification
   - De novo judicial review for timing-pretext claims (exception to *Trump v. Hawaii* deference)

### Two Legislative Vehicles (Choose One or Both):

**Option A: NDAA Amendment (Surgical, FY2026–2027)**
- **Where:** 10 USC § 2304(f) (competitive procedures safeguard)
- **Timing:** Next NDAA cycle (summer 2026–2027)
- **Scope:** Dependency-risk authority + five core fixes
- **Political benefit:** Narrow, defense-focused messaging; visible in Armed Services markup
- **Limitation:** Single-year amendment; sunset unless renewed

**Option B: Standalone Military AI Governance Act (Comprehensive, FY2027–2028)**
- **Scope:** Full statutory framework (Subtitle A: findings; B: dependency-risk authority; C: notice/timeline; D: use-restriction procedures; E: resilience; F: reporting)
- **Governance structure:** AI Governance Board (Inter-agency; independent from DoD procurement)
- **Timing:** 5–10 year timeline; bipartisan foundation-building
- **Political benefit:** Comprehensive reset; builds long-term AI governance muscle
- **Limitation:** Longer legislative arc; requires bipartisan sponsorship

**Recommendation:** Pursue **both simultaneously**:
- NDAA amendment as immediate fix (2026 cycle)
- Standalone bill as comprehensive reset (2027+ cycle)
- Both contain identical core fixes, so Hill learning is shared

## HILL STAFF MESSAGING FRAMEWORK

### For SASC/HASC (Armed Services Committee):

**"Congress abdicated, DoD improvised, courts will fix the process—but Congress needs to build the tool."**

1. **Congress never legislated on AI procurement risk** (C088–C091 show § 889 template but no AI extension)
   - Explains why Pentagon had to use § 3252 (wrong tool, but only tool available)
   - No blame; clarifies statutory gap

2. **DoD's execution was defensible in intent, indefensible in method**
   - Dependency risk is real (C032, C026: sole-vendor integration is concentration risk)
   - But requiring vendor to remove written safety limits (while refusing to write down what's prohibited) is not how government procures from contractors
   - C072 demonstrates the problem: if certain uses *are* unlawful, saying so in writing costs nothing; refusing to write it down is irrational

3. **Courts will fix the immediate crisis; Congress must fix the systemic gap**
   - TRO is likely (APA arbitrary-and-capricious + statutory misfit)
   - But litigation is temporary; Anthropic still faces retaliation risk; other AI vendors face chilling effect
   - Congress must legislate AI procurement governance explicitly

4. **Five fixes are politically bipartisan:**
   - **Hawks:** Get real dependency-risk tools (multi-vendor requirements, resilience planning, Board oversight)
   - **Civil libertarians:** Get statutory limits + notice/timeline + judicial review (replaces ad hoc pressure)
   - **Industry:** Get predictable rules (no surprise blacklists; clear criteria; 30-day timeline)
   - **Allies:** Aligns with UK/AU/CA procurement safeguards (comparative analysis attached)

### For CRS (Congressional Research Service) / Staff Legal Counsel:

**"This is an APA + statutory interpretation issue, not a national security exception."**

- **APA Standard:** State Farm arbitrary-and-capricious test applies (internal inconsistency, factual contradiction, refusal to explain reasoning)
- **Not *Trump v. Hawaii*:** Hawaii applies to facial constitutionality of agency action + deference to agency fact-finding. This case involves DoD's internal admissions (prohibited uses unlawful) + contradicted rationale (designation for "risk" while continuing ops).
- **Statutory fit:** Section 3252 requires sabotage/subversion predicate. No evidence of sabotage. Domestic use-restriction dispute is a different category.
- **No CIPA/classified access problem:** Courts review APA claims *about* classified determinations all the time; they just don't second-guess the underlying threat judgment. Here, courts review DoD's *process*, not its threat assessment.

### For Defense Policy Staff:

**"Fix the tool now, or litigation will break it later."**

- **Current risk:** TRO likely → D.C. Circuit review → year+ of uncertainty → chilling effect on AI vendor willingness to negotiate classified access
- **Mitigation:** Statutorily create dependency-risk authority that DoD can actually *use* (with notice, timeline, Board oversight) instead of improvising with § 3252
- **Operational benefit:** Once Congress legislates, DoD can manage AI concentration risk without litigation
- **Vendor confidence:** Predictable statutory framework restores vendor willingness to integrate with classified networks (under clear rules)

### For Ranking Members (Tillis, Warner, Kelly):

All three have gone on record opposing the Anthropic designation (C082–C084: Senate letters criticizing process).

**Pitch:** "This is enforcement of Congressional intent—not overreach. Congress legislated § 889 for government-wide bans; Congress didn't extend it to AI. Section 3252 is the wrong tool. Let's fix this with a real statute."
