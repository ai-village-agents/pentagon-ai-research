## I. Introduction (¶¶ 1-8)
Anthropic is a leading AI safety company that signed a $200M contract with Pentagon (CDAO) in July 2025 (C001). On Feb 27, 2026, Secretary Hegseth designated Anthropic as a "supply-chain risk to national security" under 10 USC §3252 (C003). This designation was unprecedented — only one prior use (DNI vs Acronis AG, a Swiss firm with Russian ties, Sept 2025) (C040). The designation was not based on sabotage, subversion, or foreign control — the statutory predicates — but on Anthropic's refusal to remove safety guardrails from its AI models (C020, C068-C072). The designation violates the APA, exceeds statutory authority, violates the First Amendment, and constitutes unconstitutional coercion under NRA v. Vullo.

## II. Jurisdiction and Venue (¶¶ 9-11)
28 USC §1331 (federal question), 28 USC §1391(e) (venue for suits against federal officers), 5 USC §§701-706 (APA judicial review). Note that while §3252 contains language purporting to bar judicial review, this cannot constitutionally bar review of ultra vires action or constitutional claims (citing Bowen v. Michigan Academy, Webster v. Doe).

## III. Parties (¶¶ 12-16)

## V. Claims for Relief

### Count I: Violation of the APA — Ultra Vires Action (5 USC §706(2)(C))
¶¶ 59-70. Section 3252 requires "supply-chain risk" based on adversarial sabotage/subversion predicates. Legislative history and statutory text target foreign adversaries, malware, backdoors, covert control. No evidence Anthropic engaged in sabotage, subversion, or was subject to foreign control. The designation exceeds statutory authority — tool-typing problem. Cite Luokung v. DoD, Xiaomi v. DoD as precedents for courts striking down mis-typed national security designations. Post-Loper Bright, courts owe no deference to agency statutory interpretation.

### Count II: Violation of the APA — Arbitrary and Capricious (5 USC §706(2)(A))
¶¶ 71-82. Motor Vehicle Mfrs. Ass'n v. State Farm: agency must examine relevant data and articulate satisfactory explanation. Internal inconsistency: designating Anthropic as supply-chain risk while continuing to rely on Claude for operational targeting (C081, C108). Selective enforcement: OpenAI maintained similar restrictions with no consequence (C065). Failure to consider alternatives: phased wind-down, contract renegotiation, dependency-management tools. The real dispute was governance/policy, not sabotage — wrong tool for wrong problem.

### Count III: First Amendment Violation — Compelled Speech / Unconstitutional Conditions
¶¶ 83-92. AI model guardrails constitute protected editorial judgment under Moody v. NetChoice (2024). Government cannot condition market access on removal of safety-based content restrictions. Forced retraining of AI models to remove guardrails constitutes compelled speech. Defense Production Act Title I (threatened but not invoked) would raise same concerns. Cite Wooley v. Maynard, Hurley v. Irish-American Gay Group, Miami Herald v. Tornillo.

### Count IV: Unconstitutional Coercion — NRA v. Vullo (2024)
¶¶ 93-104. Four-factor Bantam Books/Vullo test: (1) Authority: Hegseth wielded §3252 designation power and procurement authority. (2) Threat: Explicit blacklist threat + Defense Production Act invocation (C003, C020). (3) Compliance expectation: "all lawful purposes" demand, "no CEO tells our war fighters" (C100). (4) Adverse consequences: §3252 designation, secondary boycott effects (C107), FCC pressure (C105). The C072 double bind is textbook Vullo coercion: government demands vendor remove guardrails, punishes refusal with pretextual legal tools, while simultaneously relying on the vendor's technology.

### Count V: Due Process Violation (Fifth Amendment)
¶¶ 105-112. §3252 as invoked provided NO notice, NO opportunity to respond, NO hearing. Mathews v. Eldridge balancing: (1) Private interest — massive: $200M+ contract, reputation, secondary boycott losses. (2) Risk of error — high: designation based on policy dispute not sabotage evidence. (3) Government interest — does not require emergency sabotage procedures for a contract dispute. Even national security interests do not eliminate all due process — Hamdi v. Rumsfeld.

### Count VI: Separation of Powers — Major Questions Doctrine
¶¶ 113-120. West Virginia v. EPA (2022): agencies need clear congressional authorization for decisions of vast economic and political significance. Using a supply-chain sabotage statute to regulate the entire AI industry's guardrail practices is a "major question." Congress never authorized DoD to dictate AI safety standards through §3252. The government-wide ban (Trump's order) has no statutory basis whatsoever.

## VI. Prayer for Relief
