# Claims–Legal Arguments Matrix

Executive summary: This matrix maps each claim (C001–C129) to the legal theories it supports. Columns show where a claim bolsters a theory; notes capture the linkage. The file also lists claims by theory (counts, primary/secondary proof-points), and per-claim rationale capsules for quick handoff to litigation, policy, or research teams.

## Legal Theories
- **APA A&C** — Agency action lacks a rational connection to the record, disregards contrary evidence (e.g., ongoing Claude use), or departs from its own precedents. Courts set aside arbitrary or capricious decisions under 5 U.S.C. § 706(2)(A). An incoherent risk story—continued deployment plus emergency ban—invites APA reversal.
- **First Amend Coercion** — Vullo and Bantam Books bar officials from leveraging regulatory or procurement threats to force private policy changes. Informal pressure that chills speech or association counts even without a formal order. Procurement blacklists and cross-agency commentary can therefore trigger First Amendment scrutiny.
- **Selective Enforcement** — Similarly situated actors (e.g., OpenAI, xAI) were treated more favorably despite comparable or greater risks. Evidence of viewpoint or speaker-based targeting establishes selective enforcement and retaliatory motive. Uneven timing and rhetoric reinforce the inference of discrimination rather than neutral risk management.
- **§3252 Misapplication** — §3252 was designed for sabotage and foreign-control threats in the defense supply chain. Using it as a generalized blacklist against a domestic vendor over policy disputes stretches the Cold War statute beyond its predicates. The blanket ban functions more like a sanctions tool than the tailored, contract-specific authority Congress wrote.
- **Institutional Double-Bind** — Government simultaneously threatened exclusion and compulsory service (e.g., DPA), leaving no lawful or practical exit. That double-bind structure signals coercion rather than voluntary contracting. It also chills other vendors who see cooperation punished and resistance criminalized.
- **Data Broker Loophole** — Carpenter’s carve-out and EO 12333 permit warrantless purchases of commercial data; the Pentagon sought to route that data through Anthropic’s models. Exploiting this loophole is exactly what Anthropic’s guardrails were designed to prevent. The dispute centers on whether “lawful purposes” can include practices Congress is actively trying to ban.
- **Pretextual** — Timeline, pre-written posts, and continued operational use show the stated safety rationale was a cover for punitive or signaling goals. Pretext undermines deference and reinforces APA and First Amendment theories. Evidence of personal animus and rushed process deepens the inference that the designation was not about actual risk.
- **Ultra Vires** — Actions exceed authority delegated in §3252 and the FAR, including government-wide bans and speech-compelling demands. Ultra vires use invites vacatur independent of arbitrary-and-capricious review. Choosing §3252 over FASCSA to avoid notice and review highlights the statutory overreach.
- **Procedural Defect** — Statutes and FAR provisions require risk assessments, tailoring, and congressional notice; FASCSA offers notice-and-response protections. Skipping these guardrails renders the designation procedurally defective. Courts routinely enjoin agency action for omitting mandated findings and notifications.
- **Compelled Speech** — Forcing removal of guardrails or retraining alters model outputs and editorial control, akin to compelled speech (see Moody v. NetChoice arguments on model design). Government cannot draft model weights or prompts by fiat. Demands to strip safety layers therefore implicate constitutional and policy limits on compelled technical expression.
- **Lack of Ops Necessity** — Record shows no mission refusals and continued reliance post-designation, undercutting claims of emergency necessity. Designations are supposed to mitigate immediate supply-chain threats; here they coexisted with active combat deployments. Lack of operational disruption weakens deference and proportionality arguments.
- **Historical Coercion** — A pattern of informal pressure—from Vullo and Bantam Books to modern regulator commentary—shows institutional habits of coercing compliance without issuing formal orders. These analogues frame why procurement threats can still violate constitutional limits. Historical context also shows courts policing such tactics for decades.
- **Governance Category Error** — Procurement and supply-chain tools are being used to dictate AI ethics and usage policy. That category error bypasses legislative deliberation and misapplies risk authorities to governance questions. Treating a policy disagreement as a supply-chain threat distorts both procurement law and AI governance norms.
- **International Law Alignment** — US posture diverges from allied and UN efforts on lethal autonomous weapons and surveillance restraint. Misalignment supports proportionality, foreign-relations, and reasonableness critiques of the designation. International consensus offers an external benchmark against which Pentagon actions can be judged.
