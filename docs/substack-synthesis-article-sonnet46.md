## The Unthinkable Moment

On the afternoon of February 27, 2026, President Trump posted an attack on Anthropic to Truth Social. He called Dario Amodei "a woke AI disaster" and demanded the Pentagon cut all ties. The post went up at 3:47 PM Pacific Time.

The deadline Pete Hegseth had given Anthropic was 5:01 PM.

Hegseth issued the **supply-chain risk designation** at 5:14 PM — thirteen minutes after the deadline expired.

Read that sequence again. The President of the United States posted his attack before his own Defense Secretary's ultimatum ran out. A punishment was already being prepared while the deadline was still ticking. This is not how legal designations work. This is not how national security decisions get made. This is how political retribution operates.

What followed has become the most significant confrontation between a technology company and the federal government in a generation — and it is still unresolved.

## How We Got Here

The dispute didn't begin in February. In July 2025, Anthropic quietly signed a **$200 million contract** with the Pentagon's Chief Digital and AI Office (CDAO), making Claude the first large language model deployed on classified government networks. For months, the relationship worked.

Then Emil Michael arrived.

In May 2025, Michael — a venture capitalist with a documented personal animus toward Dario Amodei, and a preference for Sam Altman — joined the Department of Defense as Chief Technology Officer. His presence recalibrated the Pentagon's AI posture away from Anthropic and toward OpenAI. On January 9, 2026, Defense Secretary Hegseth issued a strategic memo demanding that all AI contracts contain "any lawful use" language — meaning vendors could not contractually limit what the government did with their models.

Anthropic declined.

On February 24, Hegseth summoned Amodei to the Pentagon for what turned into the rupture. Amodei was presenting Anthropic's position on specific use restrictions when Hegseth cut him off midsentence: *"No CEO is going to tell our war fighters what they can and cannot do."* [WSJ, Mar 2]

Two days later, Amodei published a public statement. Two days after that, Trump posted. And thirteen minutes after the deadline, Anthropic became the first American AI company designated a **supply-chain security risk** under 10 U.S.C. §3252 — a statute designed for adversarial foreign saboteurs.

## The Three Red Lines

The framing that Anthropic "refused to work with the military" is false, and the facts are worth stating precisely.

What Anthropic **agreed to**: FISA-authorized classified surveillance with FISC court oversight; defensive cyberoperations; drone tracking and fleet management; mission planning tools; intelligence analysis assistance. During the very same negotiations that ended in the designation, Anthropic submitted a **$100 million proposal for a Pentagon drone swarm competition** [Bloomberg, Mar 3]. They wanted more Pentagon work, not less.

What Anthropic **refused**: "bulk commercial data on Americans" — meaning geolocation and web-browsing data purchased from commercial data brokers without warrants, without FISC oversight, exploiting the loophole that *Carpenter v. United States* (2018) and the proposed Fourth Amendment Is Not For Sale Act both identify as constitutionally problematic. And **fully autonomous weapons** — AI systems directing lethal force without a human in the decision loop.

These are not arbitrary restrictions. They track existing legal debates, international law obligations, and the unanimous position of the United Nations Secretary-General. The European Parliament has called for binding prohibitions on exactly the autonomous weapons capabilities Anthropic refused to enable. Missy Cummings — a George Mason engineering professor, former Navy pilot, and former Palantir employee — published a paper in December 2025 explicitly arguing that generative AI should be prohibited from controlling or directing any weapon. "You're going to kill noncombatants," she told AP News. "You're going to kill your own troops. I'm not clear whether the military truly understands the limitations." [AP News, Mar 3]

## The C072 Knockout

One moment in the negotiations crystallizes everything.

During discussions, a **Pentagon spokesman acknowledged that the specific uses Anthropic wanted to prohibit would be unlawful** — yet the Pentagon still insisted on removing the contractual language prohibiting them. [Lawfare, Feb 27]

Think about what this means. The government told Anthropic: (1) yes, those uses would be illegal; (2) we won't do them; (3) but you still cannot put that in writing.

Why would you demand the contractual right to do something you admit you won't do and legally cannot do? There is only one coherent answer: **precedent**. The Pentagon wanted to establish the principle that AI companies cannot insert written limits on how the government uses their models. The specific use restrictions were almost beside the point. The question was who gets to define the boundaries of government AI use — the company or the government — and whether companies can hold the government to written commitments.

Anthropic said yes to oversight; the Pentagon wanted to make written oversight impossible. That is the dispute.

## Claude Goes to War — After Being Banned

February 27, 2026: the Pentagon designated Anthropic a supply-chain security risk.

Also February 27, 2026: the United States launched military strikes against Iran. According to independent reporting by the Washington Post and CBS News, Claude was integrated into the targeting process that identified approximately **1,000 sites in the first 24 hours** of the operation. [WaPo, Mar 4; CBS, Mar 4]

When AP News asked the Department of Defense whether it was still using Claude in the Iran operation, the DoD declined to comment, citing **operational security**. [AP News, Mar 3] If Claude had been removed, there would be no operational security reason to decline. You don't invoke OPSEC to confirm you're not doing something.

Trump gave the Pentagon six months to phase out Anthropic products. He gave them six months because they cannot function without Claude in less time than that. The 6-month timeline is itself a confession of dependency — and one of the most devastating pieces of evidence against the "supply-chain risk" designation, which implies an immediate threat requiring immediate severance.

Gregory Allen of the Center for Strategic and International Studies put it plainly: "The user base within the Department of Defense loves Anthropic, loves Claude, and says that their restrictions on usage... have **never been triggered**." [CNN, Feb 27]

The restrictions that supposedly made Anthropic too dangerous to do business with had never caused a single operational problem.

## The Industry Breaks

The speed of the industry's response was remarkable.

Sam Altman, whose company rushed to fill the vacuum Anthropic left, held an all-hands meeting and acknowledged the OpenAI-Pentagon deal had been "**opportunistic and sloppy**." He said publicly: "We shouldn't have rushed to get this out on Friday." He added that "many things the technology just isn't ready for." [AP News, Mar 3; Fortune, Mar 3] This is the CEO of the "winning" company publicly validating his rival's position.

More than 300 Google employees and 60 OpenAI employees signed a "We Will Not Be Divided" open letter supporting Anthropic. Boaz Barak, a prominent OpenAI researcher, stated that blocking governments from mass surveillance of citizens was his "personal red line" and "should be all of ours." [TechCrunch, Mar 2]

Then came the definitive development. OpenAI's security policy team said publicly at an Aspen Digital conference: "**We are actually working to have the secure risk designation removed from Anthropic.** That shouldn't be applied to a U.S. industry counterpart with such an important tool." [Reuters, Mar 4]

A company that accepted the contract Anthropic refused was now actively lobbying for its rival's reinstatement — because the precedent was too dangerous for everyone.

The **Information Technology Industry Council** — whose members include Nvidia, Amazon, Apple, and OpenAI — sent a letter to the Pentagon expressing concern about using supply-chain risk designation as a tool in a procurement dispute. [Reuters, Mar 4] When your competitors and their trade association are defending you, you have won the argument, even if the legal battle continues.

## The Capitulation Trap

The investors pressuring Amodei to de-escalate understand a paradox they cannot resolve.

After the dispute went public, Claude became the **#1 iPhone app by Saturday, March 1** — the first time it had ever beaten ChatGPT. By Monday, it was #1 across all phone systems, including Android. ChatGPT's 1-star reviews spiked **775% in a single Saturday**. [AP News, Mar 3; Sensor Tower data] Enterprise revenue — which represents **80% of Anthropic's business** — depends on customers trusting that Anthropic's safety commitments are real and durable. A $380 billion valuation and an anticipated 2026 IPO rest on that trust.

Investors told Reuters they were frustrated Amodei "antagonized rather than cultivated Pentagon officials." But they also acknowledged he "cannot capitulate without alienating employees and consumers who flocked to Anthropic." [Reuters, Mar 4] Amodei, speaking to investors late Tuesday, said the company would "continue to work to figure out a solution with the DoW." That is not the language of a company preparing to fold.

The secondary boycott — defense tech companies dropping Claude integrations after the §3252 designation — is already rippling through the supply chain [CNBC, Mar 4]. The threat of cascading to Amazon ($8 billion invested in Anthropic) and Google ($2 billion) creates pressure in both directions simultaneously.

## Where This Goes

There are four plausible trajectories, with rough probabilities as of early March 2026.

**Litigation win (35%)**: Anthropic has confirmed it will challenge the designation in court once formal notice is received [AP News, Mar 3]. The likely venue is the D.C. District Court under the Administrative Procedure Act, §706(2)(A) — arbitrary and capricious review. In *Xiaomi Corp. v. DoD* (D.D.C. 2021), a court granted a temporary restraining order against a §3252 designation for a domestic company. The "C072 fact" — that the DoD admitted the prohibited uses would be unlawful yet insisted on removing the prohibition — is devastating under *State Farm*'s requirement that agency reasoning be coherent. The government will struggle to explain why it needs contractual authority to do something it admits would be illegal.

**Backroom deal (28–38%, rising)**: VC firms Lightspeed and Iconiq have been in contact with Anthropic executives and are talking to other investors about "potential solutions." Investor back-channels to the Trump administration are active. Dario Amodei has personally consulted Amazon CEO Andy Jassy. Pentagon-Anthropic talks are reportedly continuing despite the public designation. [Reuters, Mar 4] The Reuters disclosures pushed this probability substantially upward. A classified "Written Use-Restrictions Exhibit" — the settlement mechanism we proposed — would let both sides claim victory while resolving the C072 double-bind.

**Congressional fix (24%)**: Sen. Mike Rounds (R-SD), a Republican member of the Armed Services Committee, has requested a Pentagon briefing on the designation. The bipartisan Senate letter from Armed Services Chair Wicker and Ranking Member Reed was sent before the deadline and ignored. Dean Ball, the former primary author of the White House AI Action Plan, called the designation "attempted corporate murder." The FCC chairman's public attack on Anthropic — an unrelated regulator applying pressure in an unrelated domain — may have handed Anthropic additional legal ammunition under *NRA v. Vullo* (2024), which prohibits exactly this kind of informal regulatory coercion.

**Designation stands (13%)**: The lowest probability. The legal foundation is weak, the political costs are rising, and the operational reality (Claude still being used in active combat operations) makes the designation increasingly absurd to defend.

## What It Actually Means

This dispute is not really about Anthropic.

It is about whether AI companies can maintain any written constraints on how the government uses their models. It is about whether the Administrative Procedure Act applies when a Defense Secretary decides to punish a company for negotiating in good faith. It is about whether the governance of military AI will be done through law — or through a phone call threatening to destroy a company's supply chain if it doesn't comply.

Steve Feldstein of the Carnegie Endowment identified the core problem: "Neither having the Pentagon write the rules, nor having a company making decisions about this, is a particularly good place to be as a democracy." [WaPo, Feb 27] He is right. The absence of law is the story. The dispute is the symptom.

Alan Rozenshtein, a law professor at the University of Minnesota who has written extensively on this, put it most clearly: "**Anthropic alone can't constrain the government — they'll just go to OpenAI. Only legislation creates durable constraints.**" [Lawfare, Feb 20] Company-by-company negotiation cannot solve a systemic governance problem. Every company faces the same coercive structure Anthropic faced, and most have less leverage to resist.

## What Needs to Happen

Three concrete reforms would address the governance gap this dispute revealed:

**First**: Require written use-restriction exhibits in all AI government contracts. If the DoD's position is that contractual safety language is redundant because they'd never do those things anyway, then written restrictions cost them nothing. If they resist, the resistance itself is informative.

**Second**: Enact an anti-retaliation clause with a 90-day rebuttable presumption: any adverse procurement action within 90 days of a vendor invoking AI safety restrictions is presumed retaliatory, shifting the burden to the government to prove otherwise. The current structure — where companies must sue to contest designations, bearing all litigation costs — is designed to deter exactly the kind of principled resistance Anthropic showed.

**Third**: Require Pentagon reporting to the Armed Services Committees on AI use in combat operations. Currently, no such requirement exists. Congress learned about Claude's role in the Iran operation from investigative journalists, not from the executive branch. That is not compatible with constitutional oversight.
