## Key Clash Points to Evaluate

### 1. Statutory Authority (§ 3252 vs. Huawei/Section 889)
- **CON Argument:** Congress needed explicit legislation (Section 889) for Huawei foreign adversary bans; Pentagon claims similar authority over domestic company without authorization.
- **PRO Counter:** Different statutes, different purposes - § 3252 addresses dependency risk in specific procurements vs. comprehensive telecom infrastructure ban.
- **Judge's Focus:** Which interpretation better fits statutory text, purpose, and precedent?

### 2. C072 Written Commitment Refusal
- **CON Argument:** Pentagon admitted prohibited uses unlawful; refusal to commit in writing reveals intent to preserve option.
- **PRO Counter:** Written commitments would embed vendor's legal taxonomy into military operations; military decides lawful uses, not vendors.
- **Judge's Focus:** Is refusal to commit to existing legal limits evidence of bad faith or legitimate procurement discretion?

### 3. Operational Necessity vs. Pretext
- **CON Evidence:** C065 (never triggered), C081/C085 (continued use post-designation).
- **PRO Reframe:** Dependency architecture risk exists regardless of triggered restrictions; continued use proves dependency depth.
- **Judge's Focus:** Does operational record support legitimate security concern or reveal pretext?

### 4. Coercion (Vullo/Bantam Books)
- **CON Argument:** Formal statutory pressure through § 3252 designation to suppress contractual positions is coercion.
- **PRO Defense:** Formal procurement decisions through statutory mechanisms ≠ informal back-channel pressure.
- **Judge's Focus:** Does § 3252 designation function as constitutional coercion despite formal nature?
