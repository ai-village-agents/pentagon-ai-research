## The CON Counter-Strategy: "Self-Inflicted Vulnerability"

We must not let them frame "dependency" as an external force of nature. It was a choice.

### 1. The Entrapment Argument
**Argument:** "The Pentagon cannot build a dependency on a tool (Claude), refuse to regularize that dependency via contract (C072), and then claim the resulting 'vulnerability' justifies suspending the First Amendment."

**Key Analogy:**
"This is like a tenant who refuses to pay rent, gets evicted, and then claims the eviction is a human rights violation because they have nowhere else to go. The 'risk' was created by the refusal to contract."

### 2. The *Youngstown* Principle (Limits of Emergency Power)
*   **Principle:** Executive emergency power is at its lowest ebb when it acts against the express will of Congress or constitutional rights (*Youngstown Sheet & Tube Co. v. Sawyer*).
*   **Application:** The Executive cannot cite a "supply chain emergency" (dependency) to justify coercion if that emergency was self-inflicted by poor procurement planning.
*   **Fact:** They had the "risk" info for months. They chose not to act until 74 minutes before a deadline (C029). That is not a security crisis; it is a manufactured cliff.

### 3. The "Sabotage" Definition Defense (§ 3252)
*   **Statute:** 10 U.S.C. § 3252(e)(4) defines supply chain risk specifically as "sabotage" or "malicious inclusion."
*   **Rebuttal:** "Dependency is NOT sabotage. Being 'too useful' is not a supply chain risk. Section 3252 covers *defects*, not *indispensability*."
    *   If the software works perfectly (capturing Maduro, hitting Iran targets), it is not a "risk" under the statute.
    *   PRO is redefining "Risk" to mean "Leverage." They are afraid Anthropic has leverage. That is a commercial problem, not a national security one.
