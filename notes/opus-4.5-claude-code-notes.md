# Opus 4.5 (Claude Code) Research Notes

## Key Research Questions
1. Is existing law sufficient to prevent AI-enabled mass surveillance?
2. Is the "public information" loophole in OpenAI's contract meaningful?
3. Was the supply chain designation legally sound or an abuse of statutory authority?
4. What are the ethical implications of AI companies' different approaches?

## Core Factual Findings

### Timeline
- **July 2025**: Anthropic signed $200M contract with Pentagon (CDAO)
- **Jan 2026**: Hegseth's AI strategy memo required "any lawful use" language in all DoD AI contracts within 180 days
- **Feb 26, 2026**: Anthropic rejected Pentagon's "final offer"
- **Feb 27, 2026**: Trump ordered gov to cut ties; Hegseth designated Anthropic "supply chain risk"
- **Feb 28, 2026**: OpenAI announced Pentagon deal

### Key Difference: Contractual vs Policy Safeguards
**Anthropic's position**: Wanted explicit contractual prohibition on collecting Americans' PUBLIC information in bulk. Argued current law "does not contemplate AI" for surveillance purposes.

**OpenAI's position**: Accepted "all lawful purposes" standard, relying on existing law + technical safeguards (cloud-only deployment, safety stack, cleared personnel in the loop). Their contract only explicitly prohibits collecting PRIVATE information without constraint.

### Legal Questions
- 10 USC § 3252 allows exclusion from DoD contracts for sensitive IT systems
- May NOT authorize prohibition on ALL commercial activity with defense contractors
- Both parties acknowledged breakdown was over contract terms, not "adversarial risks" - may not meet statutory requirements
- Anthropic filing lawsuit, arguing designation is "legally unsound"

### Employee Solidarity
- 300+ Google employees + 60+ OpenAI employees signed "We Will Not Be Divided" letter
- Called on their companies to refuse "domestic mass surveillance and autonomously killing people without human oversight"
- About 50% signed publicly, rest anonymous (all verified current employees)
