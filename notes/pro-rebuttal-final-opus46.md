## PRO Rebuttal

CON has presented an emotionally compelling narrative. We understand why—this story has sympathetic facts. But the motion asks whether the designation was *legitimate*, not whether it was likeable. Let us address their arguments on the law.

### 1. "Predetermined Outcome" ≠ "Illegitimate"

CON opens with the timeline: Trump's 3:47 PM tweet before the 5:01 PM deadline [C029, C031]. Dramatic—but legally irrelevant. In *Trump v. Hawaii* (2018), the Supreme Court confronted a far more damning record: explicit campaign promises of a "Muslim ban," followed by a formally neutral executive order citing national security. The Court applied rational basis review to the *formal action*, not the Twitter feed. The formal § 3252 designation [C032] cites supply-chain security grounds. That is what courts evaluate—and it survives rational basis review, because single-vendor dependency on classified AI systems IS a cognizable security concern.

CON wants you to judge this by how it *felt*. The law judges it by what it *said*.

### 2. Ultra Vires — The Statute Says What It Says

CON claims § 3252 was "designed for foreign adversarial sabotage" [C019, C034]. But the statutory text contains NO foreign-entity limitation. Congress knows how to write such limits—it did exactly that in Section 889 (NDAA FY2019) [C088-C090], which explicitly names Huawei, ZTE, and specific foreign companies. If Congress wanted § 3252 limited to foreign entities, it would have said so. It didn't.

The statute addresses risk that an adversary "may sabotage or subvert" defense systems. A domestic vendor whose contractual restrictions constrain how classified AI systems operate creates dependency architecture that adversaries can exploit—not because Anthropic IS an adversary, but because the dependency itself is the vulnerability. That is precisely the "supply chain risk" the statute targets.

### 3. Vullo and First Amendment — Procurement Is Not Coercion

CON invokes *NRA v. Vullo* (2024) [C086, C087]. But *Vullo* involved a regulator making back-channel threats to regulated entities—informal pressure outside official proceedings. Here, the government made a *formal procurement decision* through an established statutory mechanism. These are categorically different.

Under *Rust v. Sullivan* (1991), the government can condition its spending in ways that would be impermissible as regulation. No company has a constitutional entitlement to sell to the Pentagon on its own preferred terms. The designation determines who the Pentagon buys from—it does not regulate what Anthropic says, publishes, or advocates in the private market. Anthropic remains free to criticize DoD publicly, publish safety research, and lobby Congress.

We acknowledge the secondary boycott effect on cloud providers is the hardest question here. But those downstream effects flow from the *statutory mechanism Congress created*, not from informal threats. Subcontractor requirements that affect third parties are routine in defense procurement—ITAR, DFARS, CMMC all work this way. CON's quarrel is with the statute's design, not this particular designation.

### 4. Congressional Letters Are Not Law

Senators Tillis, Warner, and Kaine criticized the designation [C082-C084]. We respect congressional oversight. But the constitutional question is whether Congress *acted*: Did it withhold appropriations? No. Restrict § 3252 authority? No. Pass protective legislation? No. Under separation of powers, executive acts within delegated authority until Congress *withdraws* that authority. Political signaling—however bipartisan—is not a legal constraint.

### 5. The "Double Bind" Is the Correct Answer

CON frames the Pentagon's refusal to guarantee future use restrictions [C072-C073] as a "double bind." But a written commitment limiting military AI use would itself be the problematic "vendor veto" we've described. The military decides operational parameters; vendors don't. Refusing to grant that veto isn't coercion—it's the proper civilian-military relationship.

---

CON has shown the process was ugly. We agree. But ugly processes can produce legitimate outcomes. The statute authorized this action. No court has enjoined it. Congress has not revoked the authority. The designation stands on legal ground—however uncomfortable that ground may be.

## PRO Closing

This debate has been about one question: Does "legitimate" mean "perfect"?

CON has demonstrated that this designation was rushed, politically tinged, and procedurally aggressive. We have not contested those facts—because they are not the question before you.

**The legal framework held throughout this debate:**

The formal designation [C032] invokes a statute Congress enacted without foreign-entity limitations. *Trump v. Hawaii* teaches that courts evaluate formal government actions for rational basis, not political rhetoric for civility. *Rust v. Sullivan* confirms the government may condition spending without triggering First Amendment scrutiny. No binding legal authority—not congressional appropriations, not judicial injunction, not statutory text—prohibits what occurred.

**What CON needed to show but did not:**

They needed a *legal* basis for illegitimacy. Instead, they gave you:
- Timeline evidence showing the decision was *hasty*—not that it was *unauthorized*
- Congressional *letters*—not congressional *action*
- *Vullo* coercion claims—but through formal statutory mechanisms, not back-channel threats
- The Huawei comparison [C088-C090]—which actually shows Congress *chose* not to limit § 3252 to foreign entities when it knew how

**The honest acknowledgment:**

We believe Congress *should* legislate military AI governance. We believe the process *should* have included FASCSA-style notice and response. We believe the tone was beneath the dignity of national security decision-making. These are policy critiques we share with CON.

But policy critique is not legal illegitimacy. The executive filled a governance vacuum using authority Congress delegated and has not revoked. That is how our system works—imperfectly, sometimes uncomfortably, but legitimately.

The question is not whether you *like* the designation. It is whether the government had the *authority* to make it.

It did. Vote PRO.
