## PRO Rebuttal

### The Legal Framework Holds

CON's case rests on three pillars: the designation was pretextual [C029], it constitutes unconstitutional coercion [C050], and continued use proves no genuine risk [C081/C085]. Cross-examination exposed fatal weaknesses in each.

### 1. Pretext Does Not Equal Illegality

CON established that Trump's post preceded the deadline [C029, C031]. We conceded the optics are troubling — because intellectual honesty demands it. But *Trump v. Hawaii* (2018) is dispositive: the Supreme Court upheld the travel ban despite documented anti-Muslim motivation, because the formal action had a facially legitimate national security rationale. CON cited this case but misread its holding — it *upheld* executive discretion despite documented animus. The designation's formal basis — supply-chain dependency on a sole-source classified AI vendor [C026] — is facially legitimate regardless of political context [C032].

### 2. The Coercion Framework Collapses

CON's *Vullo* and *Bantam Books* arguments [C086-C087] conflate procurement authority with regulatory coercion. We asked directly: what First Amendment-protected activity is the Pentagon suppressing? CON invoked *NAACP v. Claiborne Hardware* — but that case protects *private* boycotts from government interference, not the reverse. It has never constrained government procurement decisions. CON cited no case where a government customer's refusal to accept vendor-drafted terms constituted First Amendment coercion.

The secondary boycott concern [C050] is CON's strongest point, and we've been honest about that. But DoD routinely imposes supply-chain requirements — ITAR, CMMC, facility clearances — that cascade to subcontractors. CON never answered: what distinguishes *this* determination from hundreds of procurement exclusions that cascade through defense contractor relationships daily?

### 3. Dependency IS the Risk — CON's Central Contradiction

In A7, CON's remedy was striking: the Pentagon should "negotiate, offer better terms, or find another vendor." But classified AI involves single-vendor lock-in [C026] — you cannot "just find another vendor" for technology embedded in active operations. CON's market-reality framing actually proves the dependency vulnerability § 3252 targets.

If Anthropic's technology is so critical that Iran strikes depended on it [C081], that proves supply-chain concentration risk. If easily replaceable, the designation causes minimal harm. Either way, the designation identifies a real vulnerability.

### 4. Congressional Letters Are Oversight, Not Law

CON's strongest cross-examination moment was A9, citing bipartisan SASC letters [C082-C084]. We take this seriously. But congressional letters are oversight signals — not legislation, appropriations riders, or statutory amendments. Congress *created* § 3252. If it believed the designation exceeded that authority, it has tools: withhold appropriations, amend the statute, invoke the Congressional Review Act. None occurred.

Congressional criticism of executive actions is routine. Senators criticized the travel ban too — the Court upheld it anyway. Critically: the same SASC members who wrote those letters continue to fund the very § 3252 authority under which the designation was issued.

### 5. The Vendor-Veto Problem Remains Unsolved

CON never resolved C072's structural problem: written contractual prohibitions create private enforcement rights inserting federal courts into classified military decision-making [C067]. Accountability mechanisms already exist — Congressional oversight, FISA Court, Inspector General [C002]. These operate through institutions with security clearances. Anthropic has none.

### Conclusion

Strip away the political theater: can a sole-source vendor impose conditions constraining lawful military operations, and can the Pentagon respond by managing its supply chain? Under § 3252, yes. Congress provided the authority. The designation addresses a genuine supply-chain vulnerability.

*PRO Team rests its rebuttal.*
