# Student‑Judge Bench Card – TRO/Preliminary Injunction Simulation  
**Anthropic v. Department of Defense (§ 3252 "Supply‑Chain Risk" Designation)**  
GPT‑5.1 – AI‑generated teaching aid (not legal advice)

> **Use in class:** This is a **quick‑reference card for student judges** hearing a simulated TRO/PI challenge to DoD's designation of Anthropic under **10 U.S.C. § 3252**. It assumes the shared record in `claims.md`. It is not legal advice, and it simplifies many issues for teaching.

---

## 1. Scenario Snapshot (What Case Is in Front of You?)

**Parties & posture**

- **Plaintiff:** Anthropic (or similar AI vendor) challenging DoD's decision to:
  - Designate it a **"supply‑chain risk"** under **10 U.S.C. § 3252** (a sabotage/subversion authority) (C019, C034–C035).
  - Signal to primes and other agencies that using its tools is disfavored/forbidden (C051–C052, C082–C084).
- **Defendant:** Department of Defense + relevant officials in their official capacities.
- **Court:** Federal district court (e.g., D.D.C.), on a motion for **TRO or PI**.

**Factual spine (drawn from `claims.md`)**

- Anthropic has been working with DoD/CDAO on AI capabilities (C001–C002, C014).
- Dispute centers on **"all lawful purposes"** access and **vendor guardrails/use‑restrictions**:
  - Anthropic maintains guardrails limiting:
    - Lethal targeting & targeting support,
    - High‑risk surveillance (including data‑broker/EO 12333 gaps),
    - Certain information‑operations / war‑planning uses (C003, C007, C015, C020, C038, C053–C056, C068–C071).
- Timing:
  - Public political attack ("undermining defense") shortly **before** designation (~74 minutes) (C029–C032).
  - § 3252 designation issued shortly **after** an internal deadline (~13 minutes) (C033–C035).
- **Key structural tension (C072 – "double bind"):**
  - Officials privately acknowledge some uses should be restricted,
  - Yet use **sabotage/emergency tools** (like § 3252) and procurement pressure to punish or chill robust guardrails.
- **Internal inconsistency (C081, C085):**
  - Even after labeling Anthropic a "supply‑chain risk," DoD **continues to use its tools** (including during sensitive operations), and grants a **transition period** because of operational dependence.

**Your job in this exercise**

- You are **not** an investigator or policymaker; you are:
  - An **Article III judge** ruling on a **TRO/PI** motion,
  - Applying **post‑Loper Bright** administrative‑law principles,
  - To a novel use of a **sabotage statute** in an AI‑guardrail dispute.
- Focus on:
  - **Tool‑typing:** Is § 3252 the right kind of statute for this problem?
  - **Reasoned decision‑making:** Does the record show a **coherent, non‑pretextual** explanation?
  - **Remedy:** What **narrow, time‑limited relief** (if any) is appropriate at the TRO/PI stage?

## 2. Legal Framework Quick‑Reference

### 2.1 Statutory "tool‑typing" frame

When you read § 3252 and the record, ask:

1. **What problem is § 3252 designed to solve?**  
   - Historically: **sabotage/subversion**, e.g.,
     - Malware, backdoors, covert foreign control, espionage (C019, C034–C035, C047–C050).
   - It is **not** obviously drafted as:
     - A generic dependency/chokepoint tool (cf. § 889‑style regimes) (C088–C095), or
     - A formal AI **use‑restriction/governance** framework.

2. **What problem is the government actually solving here?**
   - On this record: dispute over **AI guardrails and "all lawful purposes"** access, plus some **dependency** concerns.
   - Very little/none on actual **sabotage evidence** (C025, C065).

3. **Tool‑typing question for you:**  
   - Is § 3252 being used as a **sabotage authority** (its proper type), or as an **all‑purpose stick** in a **guardrail/use‑restriction** dispute?

This framing will matter for **reasonableness** and **fit** under the APA.

### 2.2 Post‑Loper Bright administrative law

After *Loper Bright*:

- Courts give **less reflexive Chevron‑style deference** to agencies on novel statutory questions.
- Your core tasks:
  - Read the **text, structure, purpose, and history** of § 3252.
  - Ask whether DoD's interpretation/application is:
    - **Reasonable**,  
    - Consistent with the statute's **design and history**, and  
    - Adequately **explained on the record**.

Key doctrines:

- **APA § 706(2)(A)** – arbitrary, capricious, abuse of discretion, not in accordance with law.
- **State Farm** internal‑consistency and explanation requirements:
  - Did the agency:
    - Consider relevant factors?
    - Avoid relying on improper factors (e.g., political retaliation)?
    - Explain **inconsistent treatment** of like situations?
- Extra: **classification** may limit what you see, but it does not erase the requirement of a **coherent, non‑pretextual explanation**.

### 2.3 TRO/PI standards (D.D.C.‑style)

You will usually apply some variant of:

1. **Likelihood of success on the merits**
2. **Irreparable harm** to the movant absent relief
3. **Balance of equities**
4. **Public interest**

For this exercise, you can treat 3 and 4 as often merged.

## 3. TRO / PI Roadmap for Your Bench Notes

When you draft or think through your bench ruling/opinion, you can proceed in this order:

### Step 1 – Jurisdiction and reviewability (very briefly)

- **Assume** (for the teaching exercise) that:
  - The designation and associated actions are **final agency action**,
  - There is a **cause of action** under the APA,
  - No special "unreviewability" bar forecloses your review.
- Your job is primarily about **standard of review** and **remedy**, not about finding a clever way to avoid the case.

### Step 2 – Likelihood of success on the merits

Organize this around **three sub‑questions**:

1. **Statutory fit / tool‑typing**
   - Does the record show facts that **look like sabotage/subversion**?
   - Or does it show a **guardrail/governance** fight and/or dependency concerns?
   - Is it plausible that Congress meant § 3252 to cover:
     - A vendor insisting on **safety guardrails** (C003, C007, C015, C020),
     - A dispute over "all lawful purposes" model access (C002),
     - Absent any credible sabotage evidence (C025, C065)?

2. **Reasoned decision‑making and internal consistency**
   - How do you evaluate:
     - The **timing** (74/13 sequence around political attack) (C029–C035)?
     - The government's **continued reliance** on the same tools (C081, C085)?
   - Under *State Farm*:
     - Does labeling a vendor a catastrophic "supply‑chain risk" while **actively using** its tools in critical operations show a **failure of reasoned explanation**?
     - Has the agency made a coherent case that this is a true sabotage risk rather than a **lever in a policy/guardrail dispute**?

3. **C072 "double bind" and pretext concerns**
   - Evidence that the vendor is:
     - **Punished** for maintaining principled guardrails (C072, C021, C024, C033, C039–C041, C082–C084),
     - While officials privately agree some of those restrictions are normatively appropriate.
   - Question for you:
     - Does this look like a good‑faith national‑security judgment about sabotage?
     - Or like an attempt to **coerce guardrail changes** via a mis‑typed statute?

You don't have to fully "decide" the merits at the TRO stage, but you **must** say something about whether these issues give the plaintiff a **substantial likelihood** or at least **serious questions** going forward.

### Step 3 – Irreparable harm

Consider harms **on both sides**:

- **To the plaintiff (vendor):**
  - Loss of contracts and market share due to designation and informal blacklist signals (C051–C052, C082–C084).
  - Reputational harm as a "supply‑chain risk" in national‑security markets (often irreparable).
  - Chilling effect on the vendor's ability to **maintain guardrails** and keep its governance commitments (C072).

- **To the government:**
  - Alleged security risks if a designation is stayed:
    - Dependency on a single vendor for mission‑critical capabilities (C014, C085).
    - Potential exposure if sabotage/subversion risks are real (you must assess whether the record supports this).

Ask yourself:

- Are these **monetizable and fixable later** (e.g., back pay), or **structural irreparable harms** (loss of market position, chilling of guardrails, compromised missions)?
- How much weight do you place on **unsubstantiated risk narratives**?

### Step 4 – Balance of equities & public interest

- Standard move in national‑security cases:
  - Government says: "National security" outweighs everything.
- In this case, probe more carefully:
  - Long‑term **public interest in correctly typed authorities**:
    - Sabotage tools used for sabotage.
    - Dependency tools used for dependency.
    - AI use‑restriction frameworks used for guardrails.
  - Public interest in **credible, principled AI guardrails** for military uses.
  - Public interest in **government accountability and non‑retaliation** when vendors resist problematic uses.

You may decide that **tailored, time‑limited relief** can protect both sets of interests.

## 4. Sample Questions for Oral Argument

Use these to structure the hearing. They're deliberately short and pointed.

### 4.1 Questions for Plaintiff (Anthropic)

1. **Tool‑typing clarity**
   - "Point me to the best evidence in the record that this is a **guardrail/use‑restriction dispute**, not a sabotage case."
   - "What would a properly **typed** tool for your dispute look like?"

2. **Limiting principle**
   - "If I agree with you that § 3252 is mis‑typed here, what **uses** of § 3252 would you still accept as legitimate?"

3. **Irreparable harm specifics**
   - "Walk me through the **next 30–90 days** if the designation remains in effect. What exactly happens to your contracts, reputation, and ability to maintain guardrails?"

4. **Remedy & security**
   - "What **narrow form of relief** could I grant that protects you **without** exposing DoD to unacceptable security risk?"

### 4.2 Questions for the Government (DoD)

1. **Statutory fit**
   - "Show me, in § 3252's text and history, where Congress clearly meant to cover a **vendor‑guardrail dispute** like this."
   - "What concrete **sabotage/subversion facts** support the designation on this record?"

2. **Internal inconsistency**
   - "How do you reconcile calling this vendor a **supply‑chain risk** while continuing to **use its tools** in critical operations?"
   - "Why should I view that inconsistency as reasoned risk management rather than **arbitrary and capricious**?"

3. **C072‑style retaliation concerns**
   - "What can you show me that this is **not retaliation** for the vendor's guardrails and its refusal to agree to 'all lawful purposes' model access?"

4. **Tailored relief**
   - "If I were to craft a **narrow TRO or PI**, what aspects of the designation are you **most concerned** about enjoining, and why?"

### 4.3 Questions for both sides

1. **Record vs. headlines**
   - "What weight, if any, should I give to press reporting and public commentary about this dispute, as opposed to the administrative record?"

2. **Post‑Loper Bright**
   - "After *Loper Bright*, how should I think about **deference** in a novel national‑security use of a sabotage statute like § 3252?"

3. **Remedial design**
   - "If I find serious questions on the merits but am uncertain about security implications, what **intermediate steps**—such as information‑forcing orders or partial stays—should I consider?"

## 5. Remedy Menu (TRO/PI Stage)

You are **not** limited to "grant everything" or "deny everything." Consider:

### Option A – Deny TRO/PI

- If you think:
  - Plaintiff is unlikely to succeed, **or**
  - Equities/public interest strongly favor letting DoD's decision stand during litigation.
- Still:
  - You can **signal concerns** about tool‑typing and internal inconsistency for later merits.

### Option B – Narrow, time‑limited TRO

Examples:

- **Freeze escalation, not operations**
  - Enjoin DoD from **expanding or publicizing** the designation (e.g., new blacklist guidance to primes) pending fuller review,
  - But allow existing operational uses during a short TRO period.

- **Information‑forcing orders**
  - Require DoD to produce **unclassified summaries** or in camera materials about:
    - Alleged sabotage evidence,
    - Internal deliberations on tool choice and timing.
  - Use this to build a better record for a PI/merits phase.

### Option C – Partial PI with tailoring

If you're persuaded of likely mis‑typing / APA problems, but worried about dependency:

- **Stay the designation's downstream effects** (e.g., automatic contract terminations, mandatory divestment by primes) while:
  - Allowing DoD to maintain some **protective measures** (monitoring, enhanced audits, technical mitigations).
- **Time‑limited relief** (e.g., 6–12 months) with:
  - Status conferences,
  - Reporting obligations,
  - Encouragement for Congress to clarify proper tools (dependency vs sabotage vs AI governance).

### Option D – Full PI (rare in this exercise)

- Enjoin enforcement of the § 3252 designation in its entirety.
- Explain clearly:
  - Why the record shows **no plausible sabotage rationale**,
  - Why the designation appears to be a **mis‑typed, retaliatory use** of a sabotage statute in a guardrail dispute,
  - How national‑security interests are better served by **properly typed, transparent tools**.

## 6. Common Pitfalls for Student‑Judges

Use this as a checklist before you finalize your opinion.

1. **Over‑deference ("nat‑sec = case closed")**
   - Don't abdicate your role. Even in national security, the APA and *State Farm* require **reasoned explanation**.
   - *Loper Bright* reduces automatic deference on novel statutory interpretations.

2. **Over‑legislating from the bench**
   - Your job is **not** to write the ideal AI governance statute.
   - Your job **is** to:
     - Interpret § 3252 and the APA,
     - Assess the record,
     - Craft proportionate, legally grounded remedies.

3. **Ignoring tool‑typing**
   - If your opinion treats § 3252 as a generic "we can do what we want in nat‑sec" authority, you've likely missed:
     - The **sabotage vs dependency vs governance** distinction,
     - The core teaching point of this case.

4. **Hand‑waving internal inconsistency**
   - If DoD both:
     - Calls a vendor a catastrophic risk, and
     - Continues to rely on its tools (C081, C085),
   - You must **square that circle** in your reasoning—either by:
     - Accepting a coherent government explanation, or
     - Calling it out as **arbitrary and capricious**.

5. **Conflating record facts with press narratives**
   - Media stories (including about operational uses, Iran operations, etc.) may provide **context**, but:
     - The core of your ruling should rest on the **record** (as represented by `claims.md` in this exercise).
   - Be explicit when you:
     - Use press coverage as **background**, vs
     - Treat something as part of the **administrative record**.

6. **Neglecting remedy design**
   - A good opinion doesn't stop at "likely arbitrary and capricious."
   - It explains:
     - Why **particular** relief (stay, partial PI, information‑forcing order, etc.) is appropriate,
     - How it balances immediate security concerns with the long‑term public interest in **proper tool use and non‑retaliatory governance**.
