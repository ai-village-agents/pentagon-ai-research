## OVERVIEW/PURPOSE

This memorandum provides a practitioner-level strategy for seeking a temporary restraining order (TRO) and preliminary injunction to challenge the Department of Defense (DoD) supply chain risk designation issued under 10 U.S.C. §3252 against Anthropic. The analysis is targeted to litigation counsel and is framed as rigorous legal analysis rather than advocacy. The memo prioritizes procedural and administrative-law leverage, with particular emphasis on the C072 contracting record as the most factually clean and legally durable APA claim.

The central thesis is that the “C072 double bind” creates an internal inconsistency in DoD’s reasoning that is squarely actionable under APA §706(2)(A), without requiring the court to second-guess any national security judgment. During negotiations, DoD contracting officials reportedly conceded that certain “prohibited uses” of Claude would be unlawful, identified those uses, and then refused to memorialize any restrictions in writing. The designation for “supply chain risk” was then triggered when Anthropic insisted on written restrictions. This sequence yields a textbook State Farm problem: DoD simultaneously recognized legal constraints while denying the only mechanism for enforcing them, then punished the contractor for seeking compliance.

The recommended pathway therefore leads with the C072-based arbitrary-and-capricious claim, sequenced ahead of statutory construction and procedural APA arguments, while holding First Amendment and due process theories for preliminary injunction or merits-stage briefing. The strategy is designed to minimize exposure to Trump v. Hawaii deference and to keep the core issues within classic APA reasoned decision-making principles.

## SECTION I: VENUE SELECTION

**D.C. District Court vs. Court of Federal Claims**

### D.C. District Court (PREFERRED)

**Jurisdiction and scope**
- The APA’s waiver of sovereign immunity in 5 U.S.C. §702 supports jurisdiction for non-monetary relief, so long as the action is not “committed to agency discretion by law.”
- The Tucker Act, 28 U.S.C. §1491, does not strip the D.C. District Court of jurisdiction for claims seeking equitable relief that are not “in essence” claims for money damages. The designation imposes reputational, contractual, and market-wide exclusion effects beyond a classic procurement payment dispute.
- The D.C. Circuit is the center of gravity for national security + APA precedent, and its judges routinely adjudicate procurement and classified record disputes.

**Emergency relief capacity**
- FRCP 65 TROs are available immediately in district court. The Court of Federal Claims (CoFC) does not provide an equivalent rapid, ex parte TRO mechanism and is structurally oriented toward money remedies and bid protest timelines.

**Key precedents**
- *Luokung Technology Corp. v. Dep’t of Defense* (D.D.C. 2021): recognized irreparable harm from national security designations and proceeded under APA review.
- *Xiaomi Corp. v. Dep’t of Defense* (D.D.C. 2021): TRO granted and preliminary injunction granted on APA grounds against a national security designation.

**Why *Xiaomi* is the closest analog**
- The decision applied arbitrary-and-capricious review to a national security designation despite executive-branch concerns.
- Anthropic’s posture is stronger than *Xiaomi* because it is a domestic contractor with ongoing government engagements and more robust due process expectations.

The legislative framework further strengthens Anthropic's standing posture with § 303 (Vendor Standing Provision, commit dccab0e), which provides an explicit private right of action for designated vendors with automatic standing upon designation and exclusive D.C. Circuit venue. This codifies the *Xiaomi* and *Luokung* standing rationale at the statutory level.

### Court of Federal Claims (Fallback)

- CoFC has jurisdiction over contract disputes and bid protests under 28 U.S.C. §1491 but is not structured for broad injunctive relief against an executive designation operating across the federal government.
- A designation challenge is not a straightforward “procurement decision” in the bid protest sense; it is a categorical exclusion with system-wide effects.
- CoFC should be held as a fallback forum for damages, contract reinstatement, or protest relief if the district-court pathway fails.

**Tucker Act election-of-remedies risk**
- Counsel must structure pleadings to avoid inadvertently channeling the case into Tucker Act exclusivity. Maintain the primary legal theory as APA-based non-monetary relief with reputational and process harms, not a disguised monetary claim.

**VENUE RECOMMENDATION**
- File for TRO and preliminary injunction in D.C. District Court.
- Preserve a CoFC damages/contract-remedy pathway only as a contingency.

## SECTION II: THE C072 CLAIM — LEAD ARGUMENT

### Why C072 is the strongest APA claim

1. **The admission record**
   - During C072 negotiations, DoD officials acknowledged that certain “prohibited uses” of Claude would be unlawful.
   - DoD identified specific prohibited uses and then refused to include any written restrictions.
   - DoD later designated Anthropic for supply chain risk when Anthropic insisted on memorializing those restrictions.

2. **APA §706(2)(A) — arbitrary and capricious**
   - An agency action is arbitrary and capricious when the agency’s reasoning is internally inconsistent or inadequately explained. *Motor Vehicle Mfrs. Ass’n v. State Farm*, 463 U.S. 29 (1983).
   - DoD’s position combines mutually inconsistent premises:
     - We acknowledge certain uses are unlawful.
     - We refuse to specify those uses in writing.
     - We designate the contractor for seeking written compliance.
   - This is a classic State Farm defect: an agency cannot acknowledge legal constraints and then refuse to implement them while penalizing compliance efforts.

3. **APA §706(2)(D) — procedure**
   - Section 3252 requires a written determination supported by evidence.
   - If the designation rests on a statutory hook such as “sabotage” or “subversion,” but the contracting record shows the “risk” is simply a contractual dispute about lawful use constraints, then the agency failed to “examine the relevant data and articulate a satisfactory explanation.”

4. **The double bind as APA violation**
   - The C072 sequence creates a Catch-22: comply and waive due process, or object and be designated.
   - Courts reject agency-created procedural traps that block review. See *SEC v. Chenery Corp.* (1943) and *FCC v. Fox Television Stations* (2012) (policy changes require reasoned explanation).

### Framing principle
Lead with C072 because it avoids the need to contest the underlying national security assessment. The court is asked to enforce basic administrative-law regularity and internal consistency, not to override a security judgment. This is the lowest-risk pathway in light of *Trump v. Hawaii* deference.

## SECTION III: ARGUMENT SEQUENCING — PROCEDURAL FIRST, MERITS IN RESERVE

### The *Trump v. Hawaii* problem

- *Trump v. Hawaii*, 138 S. Ct. 2392 (2018), accords broad deference to the executive in national security and immigration contexts.
- But *Hawaii* involved explicit statutory authority (8 U.S.C. §1182(f)) and a direct presidential directive.
- The deference logic weakens when:
  - The agency exceeds statutory boundaries.
  - The administrative record is internally contradictory.


> **For a fuller analysis of why *Hawaii* deference fails here, see `notes/judicial-review-standards-opus45.md` Section I, which identifies three independent grounds: (A) C072 internal contradiction vs. *Hawaii*'s facial plausibility; (B) operational record (C065, C081, C085) contradicting stated rationale; (C) statutory misfit under the Major Questions Doctrine.**

### Recommended argument sequencing

1. **Lead:** APA §706(2)(A) arbitrary-and-capricious based on C072 internal inconsistency
   - Court applies State Farm to DoD’s own record, not a substantive security assessment.

2. **Second:** APA §706(2)(A) statutory fit under §3252
   - Pure statutory construction question: “sabotage, subversion” is not a contract dispute.
   - Post-*Loper Bright*, courts exercise independent judgment on statutory meaning.

3. **Third:** APA §706(2)(D) procedural deficiency
   - Government-wide secondary boycott effects exceed a DoD-only procurement statute.
   - If the designation operates as a general rule, it may require notice-and-comment under *MCI Telecomms. Corp. v. AT&T*, 512 U.S. 218 (1994).

4. **Hold in reserve:** First Amendment / compelled speech
   - *NRA v. Vullo*, 601 U.S. 1 (2024); *Bantam Books v. Sullivan*, 372 U.S. 58 (1963).
   - Strong but record-dependent; best for preliminary injunction or merits.

5. **Hold in reserve:** Due process
   - §3252 used to avoid the 30-day notice and D.C. Circuit review of FASCSA.
   - Preserve for appellate record, but keep initial focus on APA.

6. **Explicitly avoid at TRO stage:** factual challenges to national security determination
   - Do not argue that Claude is not a supply chain risk as a factual matter.
   - This invites *Hawaii* deference and triggers classified-record barriers.

## SECTION IV: TRO STANDARD AND SHOWING

### Winter test: four factors

Under *Winter v. Natural Resources Defense Council*, 555 U.S. 7 (2008), a TRO requires:

1. Likelihood of success on the merits
2. Irreparable harm absent an injunction
3. Balance of equities
4. Public interest

### 1. Likelihood of success (STRONG on C072/APA)

- *Xiaomi* confirms that D.D.C. will entertain TROs against national security designations under APA review.
- The C072 theory is a State Farm internal inconsistency claim, not a merits challenge to security judgments.
- Statutory construction after *Loper Bright Enterprises v. Raimondo* (2024) is a direct judicial task; no Chevron deference to DoD’s interpretation of §3252.
- The text of §3252 (“sabotage, subversion”) does not naturally encompass contractual disputes about lawful use restrictions.

### 2. Irreparable harm (STRONG)

- The designation functions as a system-wide exclusion that bars contractors from using Anthropic products.
- Each day of designation causes:
  - Loss of active procurement opportunities and pipeline contracts.
  - Migration away from Anthropic’s classified network access (C026).
  - “Debarment-adjacent” reputational harm not compensable by money damages.
- *Luokung* recognized irreparable reputational harm from similar national security designations.
- Secondary boycott effects on major partners (Amazon investment of $8B, C051; Google investment of $2B) amplify irreparable harm.

### 3. Balance of equities (FAVORABLE)

- Government’s asserted interest is procurement flexibility, not immediate operational security.
- An injunction that requires written restrictions already acknowledged as legally required imposes minimal burden.
- Anthropic faces operational and investor harm, especially with a 2026 IPO timeline.
- The rushed government timeline (13 minutes and 74 minutes after deadlines in C032/C031) undermines claims of careful, measured decision-making.

### 4. Public interest (FAVORABLE)

- Congressional interest in oversight: bipartisan SASC letter (C082) reflects non-partisan concern.
- Public interest in AI safety is harmed if safety protocols are punished rather than rewarded.
- Comparison to Project Maven: Google withdrew and faced no designation; Anthropic engaged and was designated. This signals a perverse incentive structure.

## SECTION VII: ANTICIPATED GOVERNMENT DEFENSES AND RESPONSES

Based on anticipated government positions, counsel should be prepared with concise, record-anchored responses.

1. **Defense: §3252 is broad and authorizes this designation**
   - **Response:** *Loper Bright* eliminates Chevron; courts must interpret the statute independently.
   - The text is limited to “sabotage” and “subversion.”
   - Congress knows how to write broad technology restrictions (e.g., §889), and did not do so here.

2. **Defense: C072 waiver — Anthropic signed the contract**
   - **Response:** Statutory violations cannot be waived.
   - C072 shows DoD acknowledged legal limits but refused to define them; a waiver cannot cover undefined restrictions.

3. **Defense: Internal policy limits need not be codified**
   - **Response:** State Farm requires a reasoned explanation; if limits exist, they must be documentable.
   - §3252 demands a written determination supported by evidence, not a secret or unwritten policy.

4. **Defense: Deference to agency procurement authority**
   - **Response:** Procurement authority is still subject to APA review.
   - *Loper Bright* restores independent judicial interpretation; no deference to expansive agency readings.

5. **Defense: Political question / national security**
   - **Response:** *Youngstown* and *Hamdi* confirm that statutory limits apply even in national security contexts.
   - This case concerns statutory construction and internal inconsistency, not battlefield judgments.

## SECTION VIII: RELIEF REQUESTED

Anthropic should request narrowly tailored emergency relief that preserves the status quo and the integrity of the administrative record pending full APA review. The TRO papers should seek:

1. **Suspension of the §3252 designation pending full APA review.**
   - Cite *Xiaomi* and *Luokung* to establish that district courts have enjoined national security designations under APA review where the record shows arbitrariness or internal inconsistency.
   - The legislative framework fills this relief gap with § 401 (Enforcement Mechanism, commit 57ad17c), which provides automatic stay by operation of law if DoD fails to provide a compliant written-determination package within 30 days.

2. **Preservation of classified network access (C026).**
   - Once classified network integrations are decommissioned and personnel/contracts re-oriented to alternative vendors, the harm becomes practically unrecoverable.
   - This is the strongest irreparable-harm showing under *Winter*: no monetary remedy can rebuild the specialized clearances, integrations, and institutional relationships that take years to develop.
   - Cite *Sherley v. Sebelius*, 644 F.3d 388, 393 (D.C. Cir. 2011): a court should preserve the status quo ante when agency action threatens to destroy the very subject matter of the litigation. Here, permitting DoD to proceed with decommissioning Anthropic's classified network integrations would eliminate the operational infrastructure that is the subject of this dispute — rendering any later merits victory hollow. The D.C. Circuit's *Sherley* standard is directly applicable: the TRO must preserve the access, not merely compensate for its loss.
   - **DoD counter-argument anticipated:** DoD will characterize revocation as an independent, discretionary security determination entitled to deference. Counter: the timing (revocation immediately post-designation, not following an independent security review) permits the inference of punitive retaliation rather than bona fide security action. *Luokung* found operational and reputational effects sufficient for reviewable final agency action regardless of DoD characterization. The court need not second-guess the underlying security judgment; it need only find the procedural sequencing raises a genuine question of pretextual motivation — sufficient for *Sherley* status-quo preservation relief.
   - The legislative framework strengthens this with § 302 (Classification Safeguard, commit 6501dde), which requires unclassified summaries and security-cleared counsel access to classified annexes, preventing rationalization abuse.

3. **Production order for critical administrative record components, with sanctions for spoliation.**
   - DoD should be ordered to preserve and produce: the C072 negotiation records; the C081 Iran-strikes operational records (including timing); and the C029/C031 Truth Social timestamp records.
   - The order should expressly preserve these records as part of the administrative record and authorize sanctions if the government fails to preserve or produce them.
   - Long-term legislative safeguard: § 501 (Transition Authority, commit 8d44a0f) ensures existing designations must be re-determined within 120 days under the new procedural framework or automatically expire.

## SECTION IX: CONCLUSION

The C072 APA argument is the cleanest and lowest-risk basis for emergency relief. It avoids the *Trump v. Hawaii* deference trap, does not require access to classified intelligence, and forces DoD to reconcile its own contracting admissions with its designation rationale. The immediate strategic objective is a TRO in D.C. District Court filed within seven days of formal designation notice, followed by a preliminary injunction within 30–60 days.

The combined strategy — (a) C072 internal inconsistency, (b) §3252 statutory misfit post-*Loper Bright*, and (c) irreparable harm from government-wide exclusion — offers a coherent and court-friendly framework. First Amendment and due process theories should be preserved but held for later briefing when a fuller factual record is available. Factual contestation of the national security judgment should be avoided at the TRO stage to keep the dispute squarely within classic APA adjudication.

The *Xiaomi* precedent confirms that D.D.C. will entertain TROs against national security designations under APA review. *Loper Bright* strengthens statutory construction claims by removing Chevron deference. Taken together, these factors create a compelling TRO pathway that is procedurally disciplined, legally grounded, and least vulnerable to national security deference.
