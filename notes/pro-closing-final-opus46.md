## PRO Closing Statement

### What This Debate Proved

Three rounds of cross-examination tested every element of the designation's legitimacy. Here is what survived.

**The statutory authority is real.** Congress created § 3252 for DoD supply-chain risk determinations [C032]. CON argued only bespoke legislation like Section 889 can authorize exclusions [C088-C091] — but that theory would invalidate the flexible, case-by-case procurement authority Congress deliberately created. CON's statutory challenge failed.

**The dependency risk is real.** Anthropic was the sole frontier AI company on classified Pentagon systems [C026]. When pressed, CON's answer was "find another vendor" — ignoring the single-vendor lock-in that made operational transition impossible overnight. The Iran strikes [C081] proved this: the military used Claude *after* designation because it had no alternative. That is not evidence against supply-chain risk. It is the textbook definition.

**The coercion theory collapsed.** CON cited *Vullo* and *Bantam Books* [C086-C087] but could not identify any First Amendment-protected activity the Pentagon suppressed. *Claiborne Hardware* protects private boycotts from government — not vendors from government procurement decisions. No court has held that declining vendor-drafted contract terms constitutes constitutional coercion.

**Congressional letters confirmed oversight, not illegality.** Bipartisan SASC criticism [C082-C084] is how democratic accountability works — Congress signals concern while retaining power to legislate, defund, or amend. It did none of these. The authority stands.

**We conceded what honesty required.** The rhetoric was ugly [C029, C031]. The secondary boycott raises legitimate concerns [C050]. But imperfect execution does not void lawful authority — *Trump v. Hawaii* established this principle.

The question was never whether we *like* this outcome. It was whether the government acted within its lawful authority. It did.

*PRO rests.*
