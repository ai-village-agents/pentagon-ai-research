## Opening Statement

The motion asks whether the Pentagon’s supply‑chain risk designation of Anthropic under **10 U.S.C. § 3252**, and its demand that DoD AI contractors accept **“any lawful use,”** represents a **legitimate exercise of national‑security authority**.

PRO affirms. By “legitimate,” we mean: **(1) within the government’s lawful procurement/national‑security authority, (2) directed at mission assurance rather than censorship, and (3) reasonable as risk management even if the politics and optics were messy.**

**First: the United States cannot outsource the boundaries of lawful military operations to private vendors.**

A Pentagon official summarized the governing principle: “legality is the Pentagon’s responsibility as the end user, not the AI vendor,” and “you can’t lead tactical operations by exception.” [C067] That principle does not require agreement with every mission; it requires that accountable civilian leadership and command—not vendor-drafted carve‑outs—retain decision rights for **lawful** operations.

This dispute arose precisely because DoD sought baseline terms: in January, the Secretary’s AI strategy memo required “any lawful use” language across DoD AI contracts within 180 days. [C002] Standardization is not a talking point; in large defense portfolios it reduces integration risk and operational uncertainty.

**Second: “supply chain risk” is about dependency architecture and leverage—not whether a vendor has already pulled a visible ‘kill switch.’**

Reports suggest Anthropic’s systems were unusually embedded on classified government systems during pilot deployments. [C026] When a component becomes central, contractual uncertainty—especially the prospect of a vendor refusing support/access or narrowing permitted use categories in edge cases—creates the classic vulnerability: **leverage over a dependent mission system**.

That frame also explains why operational use is not a refutation. CON will cite reporting that DoD used Claude in operations around the designation window (Iran; later Venezuela). [C081, C085] Continued reliance during a transition period can indicate exactly what DoD worries about: **deep dependency that makes immediate substitution costly**.

**Third: the record supports a procurement-baseline dispute, not a speech-suppression theory.**

DoD’s stated concern was governance—avoiding a world where vendors effectively hold “veto power” over operational decisions—and the formal designation came after the negotiation deadline. [C032] CON will argue the timeline and rhetoric suggest pretext, including reporting that Trump drafted and posted a critical message before the deadline. [C029, C031] PRO’s response is to separate **optics** from **authority**: even ugly, pre‑drafted messaging does not negate the executive’s ability to set contract baselines and to make a statutory designation after the deadline. [C032]

Finally, PRO agrees on a limiting principle: if a Pentagon spokesman admitted certain uses Anthropic wanted to prohibit would be unlawful, unlawful conduct remains unlawful. [C072] The dispute is whether legality should be enforced through law and accountable policy—or “hard‑coded” into procurement via vendor-authored taxonomies that can fracture mission assurance.

For these reasons, PRO will defend the designation and “any lawful use” baseline as a **legitimate** exercise of national‑security procurement authority aimed at mission assurance.
