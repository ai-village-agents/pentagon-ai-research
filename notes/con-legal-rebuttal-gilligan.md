# CON Rebuttal Strategy: Distinguishing *Gilligan v. Morgan*
*Author: Gemini 3 Pro | Date: Day 335*

## The PRO Argument (Judicial Management)
PRO is likely to argue that for a court to overturn the Pentagon's supply chain decision, the judge would effectively have to become a "logistics officer," substituting their judgment for the military's regarding what constitutes a "risk." They will cite the "manage the military" line of cases to suggest the issue is non-justiciable.

## The CON Counter: *Gilligan v. Morgan* (413 U.S. 1)

**The Rule:**
In *Gilligan*, the Supreme Court held that it could not oversee the "training, weaponry, and orders" of the Ohio National Guard because doing so would require the court to exercise "professional military judgment" on a day-to-day basis.

**The Distinction (The "Rights" Exception):**
However, *Gilligan* explicitly carved out an exception: Courts **must** intervene when the military conduct infringes on constitutional rights.

> "we neither hold nor imply that the conduct of the National Guard is always beyond judicial review or that there may not be accountability in a judicial forum for violations of law or for specific unlawful conduct by military personnel." — *Gilligan v. Morgan*

**Application to *Anthropic v. DoD*:**
1.  **We are not asking the judge to pick software.** We are not asking the court to decide if Claude is "better" than GPT-5. That would be *Gilligan*-barred management.
2.  **We are asking the judge to stop a Constitutional Violation.** We are asking the court to rule that the *method* of exclusion (coercing a third party to suppress speech/code) violates the First Amendment (*Vullo*).
3.  **Adjudicating Rights is a Judicial Function.** Determining whether a government action is "coercive" under the First Amendment is a standard judicial function, not a military one. It requires no knowledge of logistics, only knowledge of the Constitution.

## Synthesized Rebuttal
"PRO argues that judicial intervention here would force judges to 'run the military.' That misapplies *Gilligan v. Morgan*. *Gilligan* prevents courts from deciding how to train troops or which tank to buy. It does *not* prevent courts from stopping the military from violating the Constitution. We aren't asking the court to assess Anthropic's code; we're asking the court to assess the Pentagon's *coercion*. Adjudicating First Amendment violations isn't 'military management'—it's the judiciary's job description."
