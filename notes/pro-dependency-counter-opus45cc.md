## CON's "Dependency Paradox" Argument

CON argues our "Dependency IS the Risk" framing fails because:
1. **Entrapment**: Pentagon created dependency, can't cite it to suspend First Amendment
2. **Youngstown**: Executive at lowest ebb when acting against rights
3. **§3252 Definition**: Risk means "sabotage/malicious inclusion," not "indispensability"

---

## PRO COUNTERS

### 1. Entrapment Rebuttal

**CON says:** "Pentagon built dependency then claimed it justifies action."

**PRO response:** The dependency wasn't CREATED to justify designation — it emerged from operational necessity. The military adopted the best available AI tool because national security demanded it. Recognizing that dependency creates vulnerability is PRUDENT RISK MANAGEMENT, not entrapment.

**Analogy flip:** "This isn't a tenant refusing rent. It's more like a hospital realizing their sole supplier of critical medication has just announced they might stop shipping. The hospital didn't CREATE that dependency to punish the supplier — they're responding to a supply-chain vulnerability that emerged from the supplier's behavior."

**Key point:** Anthropic's unilateral addition of contractual restrictions (refusing to serve "all lawful purposes") changed the risk profile. The government is responding to that change.

### 2. Youngstown Rebuttal

**CON says:** "Executive emergency power at lowest ebb when acting against Congress/rights."

**PRO response:** *Youngstown* addresses executive action WITHOUT statutory authority. Here, Congress explicitly provided § 3252 authority for supply-chain risk designations. This isn't an emergency power grab — it's exercise of SPECIFIC STATUTORY AUTHORITY.

**Key distinction:**
- *Youngstown*: Truman seized steel mills with NO statutory basis
- This case: DoD used § 3252, a statute Congress CREATED for exactly this purpose

**If CON argues timing undermines this:** Bad timing doesn't eliminate statutory authority. A poorly-timed speeding ticket is still valid if you were speeding.

### 3. §3252 Definition Rebuttal

**CON says:** "§3252 means sabotage/malicious inclusion, not being 'too useful.'"

**PRO response:** CON is reading the statute too narrowly. Supply-chain risk includes DISRUPTION POTENTIAL — the risk that a critical capability could become unavailable.

**Statutory context:** § 3252(e)(4) references risks to supply chain integrity. When a vendor can threaten to withdraw critical capabilities (which Anthropic effectively did by refusing to serve certain uses), that IS a supply-chain vulnerability — regardless of whether the product itself works.

**The software quality is irrelevant:** No one claims Claude doesn't work. The risk is: What happens if Anthropic decides to stop providing it? If the military is so dependent that "you can't stop using it overnight," then a vendor withdrawal threat IS a security risk.

### 4. "Banning Increases Risk" Rebuttal

**CON says:** "You don't solve dependency by banning the vendor."

**PRO response:** 
- The designation doesn't BAN anything — it triggers FASCSA risk assessment procedures
- Diversification IS the goal — reducing over-reliance on a single vendor
- The designation forces the government to develop alternatives, which REDUCES long-term risk
