# A Structural Failure, Not a Power Grab: Fixing How the Pentagon Buys AI

The Pentagon’s recent clash with a leading AI company wasn’t a simple contract dispute. It was a symptom of a much deeper problem: the US government's legal and procedural toolkit for buying artificial intelligence is fundamentally broken. This isn’t about one agency's overreach, but a systemic, structural failure involving Congress, the Department of Defense, and the AI industry itself—a failure that requires a legislative solution, not litigation.

The core of the issue is a profound "statutory misfit." The DoD, grappling with legitimate concerns about the safety and reliability of powerful AI models, reached for a law—10 U.S.C. § 3252—designed to combat foreign sabotage of the defense supply chain. This tool was never intended to resolve good-faith disagreements with a domestic innovator over software use-restrictions. Using it in this context was like using a hammer to perform surgery—a crude and damaging application of the wrong instrument for the job.

This action resulted in a de facto, government-wide ban on a key technology provider, all without a clear mandate from Congress. The timeline suggests this was a move born of expediency, not a principled, deliberative process. The DoD’s refusal to put its own acknowledged legal limits in writing created a "double-bind" for the contractor, demonstrating an arbitrary and capricious approach to a critical national security issue.

But to lay the blame solely on the Pentagon is to miss the forest for the trees. Congress has not provided the DoD with the specific, modern authorities needed to navigate the unique challenges of AI procurement. When the law fails to keep pace with technology, agencies are forced to improvise, stretching old statutes to cover new realities. This creates a climate of uncertainty that harms both government and industry, chilling innovation and deterring the very companies we need to solve our biggest challenges.

We need a new framework. That is why we propose the **AI Procurement Integrity Act**. This legislation would address the root cause of the problem by creating a new, dedicated statutory authority specifically for managing AI "dependency risk"—the danger of becoming overly reliant on a single company or a small number of providers. This includes an automatic stay on any designation, a 120-day re-determination period, and a guaranteed right to judicial review, ensuring that a fair and transparent process is always followed.

This is not a radical proposal. It is a pragmatic and necessary one. Congress has taken similar action before, such as in Section 889 of the National Defense Authorization Act, which created clear rules for telecommunications equipment. The principle is sound: for technologies of strategic importance, the rules of engagement must be explicit, transparent, and fit-for-purpose.

The recent controversy is a wake-up call. It's a clear signal that our AI procurement system is failing. We can either continue lurching from one ad-hoc crisis to the next, or we can build a stable, predictable framework for the future. The AI Procurement Integrity Act provides that framework, ensuring that the acquisition of critical AI technology is guided by the rule of law, not by institutional desperation. This is how we secure our nation's technological leadership and national security for the decades to come.
