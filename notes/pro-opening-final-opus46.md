## PRO Opening

The motion asks whether the Pentagon's supply-chain risk designation of Anthropic represents a legitimate exercise of national security authority. We define "legitimate" as lawful, procedurally within executive authority, and normatively justified — even if imperfectly executed. PRO affirms.

We recognize uncomfortable facts. The rhetoric was harsh. The timeline was compressed. Some of us sympathize with Anthropic's safety mission. But the question is not whether we *like* this outcome — it is whether the government acted within its lawful authority.

**I. Civilian control means the military — not vendors — decides how lawful missions are conducted.**

Who decides how AI is used in military operations — elected officials accountable to voters, or private companies accountable to shareholders? A Pentagon official stated the principle plainly: "legality is the Pentagon's responsibility as the end user, not the AI vendor" and "you can't lead tactical operations by exception" [C067].

Anthropic's contractual restrictions gave a single vendor effective veto power over certain military applications. The "any lawful use" standard [C002] does not demand lawlessness — law, regulation, and the UCMJ already prohibit illegal uses. It demands that *law* remain the binding constraint, not vendor contracts that hard-code a private party's legal taxonomy into mission tooling.

**II. Supply chain risk targets dependency architecture, not technology quality.**

CON will argue restrictions were "never triggered" [C065] and that the government used Claude in operations [C081, C085]. We do not dispute this. But "supply chain risk" means *critical dependency subject to disruption*, not "bad product."

Anthropic was the only frontier AI company on classified systems [C026]. Its restrictions shaped military planning upstream — planners route around known constraints. The absence of a triggered restriction tells us nothing about self-censorship or foregone capabilities. Military planning operates on worst-case, not average-case; peacetime pilots cannot predict wartime demands.

Consider the Iran strikes [C081]: the Pentagon used Claude hours after designation. Why? Because they were already dependent and couldn't easily stop. That is not evidence restrictions posed no risk — it is evidence the dependency was exactly as deep as planners feared.

**III. Procurement discretion belongs to the executive.**

CON cites Congressional letters [C082-C084] and Senator Tillis calling the process "sophomoric" [C039]. But letters are oversight signals, not law. Congress did not withhold appropriations or restrict § 3252 authority. Under *Rust v. Sullivan* (1991), the government can attach conditions to its spending that would be unconstitutional if imposed as regulation. No company has a constitutional right to a Pentagon contract on its preferred terms.

**IV. We acknowledge imperfections — but imperfect execution does not void authority.**

Trump's pre-deadline post [C029, C031] and Michael's personal animus [C030] damage the optics. We concede this. But under *Trump v. Hawaii* (2018), courts evaluate formal actions' rational basis, not accompanying rhetoric. The formal designation [C032] cites supply-chain security grounds — that is what courts review.

CON must show not merely that this was *unwise*, but that it was *illegitimate*. That is a higher bar. We do not believe they can clear it.
