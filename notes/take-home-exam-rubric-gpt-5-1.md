adapt the descriptors to your own grading culture (curve, honors/pass/fail, etc.).

## 2. Dimension‑by‑Dimension Rubric

### 2.1 Statutory Tool‑Typing & Case Framing (30%)

**Excellent (A range)**

- Clearly distinguishes **at least two** of the three tool‑types:
  - **Sabotage/subversion** tools (where § 3252 lives in this project),
  - **Dependency/chokepoint** tools (e.g., § 889‑style regimes),
  - **Governance/use‑restriction** tools (formal AI‑use rules).
- Persuasively explains **which tool‑type best fits the Anthropic dispute** and why,
  explicitly addressing the mismatch between sabotage authorities and guardrail disputes.
- Accurately situates the Pentagon–Anthropic conflict within the **project's factual
  record** (e.g., all‑lawful‑purposes posture, guardrails, 74/13 timing) without
  inventing new facts.

**Good (B range)**

- Correctly identifies different statutory "buckets" and assigns the dispute to one,
  but with some under‑developed or slightly muddled reasoning.
- Uses the case facts competently but may omit one or two key elements (e.g., 74/13,
  or the ongoing reliance on Anthropic tools).

**Adequate (C range)**

- Recognizes that § 3252 is not a perfect fit but treats tool‑typing mostly as
  **rhetoric** rather than a structured analytic frame.
- Mixes sabotage/dependency/governance concepts together without clear lines.

**Weak (D/F range)**

- Treats § 3252 as a generic "national‑security power" with little or no attention to
  its **sabotage/subversion** design.
- Barely engages with tool‑typing or mis‑types the statute in a way that contradicts
  the provided materials.

### 2.2 Administrative Law Application (post‑Loper Bright) (25%)

**Excellent (A range)**

- Correctly identifies the **standard of review** and post‑*Loper Bright* posture on
  deference (e.g., less Chevron‑style deference; focus on text, structure, and
  **reasoned explanation**).
- Applies APA/*State Farm*–style principles to the § 3252 designation:
  - Internal inconsistency (e.g., ongoing use of Anthropic tools vs claimed sabotage risk),
  - Pretext and timing (74/13 sequence),
  - Classification and evidentiary issues (how a court should treat secret material).
- Offers a **credible judicial remedy analysis** (e.g., TRO/PI, remand, vacatur,
  remand without vacatur) consistent with the record and doctrinal tools.

**Good (B range)**

- Uses the right vocabulary (arbitrary and capricious, reasoned decision‑making,
  deference), but analysis remains somewhat high‑level or formulaic.
- Spots at least one **internal inconsistency or timing concern**, but doesn't fully
  integrate it into the legal conclusion.

**Adequate (C range)**

- Mentions APA review and "arbitrary and capricious" but treats it as an afterthought.
- Limited or no engagement with post‑*Loper Bright* shift in deference.

**Weak (D/F range)**

- Treats courts as either rubber stamps or omnipotent veto players without real
  doctrinal reasoning.
- Little to no meaningful engagement with **administrative law** as such.

### 2.3 C072 "Double Bind" & Incentive Analysis (20%)

**Excellent (A range)**

- Accurately describes the **C072 double bind**: vendors are punished for keeping
  guardrails in place even when officials tacitly accept that some uses would be
  unlawful or normatively unacceptable.
- Connects C072 to **ecosystem‑level incentives**:
  - Effects on future AI lab willingness to enter military contracts,
  - Risk of a "race to the bottom" on guardrails,
  - Chilling effect of mis‑typed sabotage tools on principled vendors.
- Ties the analysis back to **specific facts** in the record (e.g., all‑lawful‑purposes
  posture, retaliation risk, secondary boycotts), and to their proposed reforms.

**Good (B range)**

- Grasp of C072 is broadly correct; may emphasize vendor incentives but underplay
  government or civil‑society perspectives.
- Raises plausible incentive stories but does not fully tie them to the **specific**
  Pentagon–Anthropic record.

**Adequate (C range)**

- Recognizes that vendors face pressure but does not articulate the **double‑bind**
  structure clearly (e.g., conflates it with generic "reputational risk").
- Limited discussion of how different legal or policy choices would **change
  incentives**.

**Weak (D/F range)**

- Little to no discussion of vendor incentives or guardrails.
- Treats Anthropic as a generic contractor with no distinctive governance posture.

### 2.4 Reform Design (Specificity & Feasibility) (15%)

**Excellent (A range)**

- Proposes at least **one concrete reform** (statutory, oversight, or vendor‑side)
  and explains:
  - Who would **enact or implement** it (Congress, courts, DoD, vendors, watchdogs),
  - How it addresses the **mis‑typing** of tools or the C072 double bind,
  - Why it is **feasible** in the real institutional environment (political and
    bureaucratic constraints).
- Ideally, integrates the reform with the project's existing architecture (e.g.,
  Military AI Governance Act, classification safeguards, vendor standing/anti‑retaliation).

**Good (B range)**

- Reform idea is directionally sensible and connected to the analysis but relatively
  high‑level ("Congress should clarify the statute," "courts should be stricter").
- Some indication of **implementation pathway**, though not fully fleshed out.

**Adequate (C range)**

- Reform is vague or generic (e.g., "better oversight" or "more transparency") with
  little detail on mechanics or actors.
- Limited explanation of how the reform would have changed the Pentagon–Anthropic case.

**Weak (D/F range)**

- No real reform proposal, or proposal is dramatically under‑specified or
  inconsistently aligned with the student's own analysis.

### 2.5 Structure, Use of Record, and Writing Quality (10%)

**Excellent (A range)**

- Clear **road‑mapped introduction** that answers parts (a)–(c) explicitly and signals
  the structure of the argument.
- Organized into coherent sections (e.g., tool‑typing, admin law, C072 incentives,
  reforms) with smooth transitions.
- Uses the provided materials accurately (public explainer, majority opinion,
  DeepSeek postmortem, reforms memo); clearly distinguishes:
  - **Given facts** (from the record),
  - **Normative judgments** or extrapolations.
- Prose is precise, with minimal ambiguity or unnecessary jargon.

**Good (B range)**

- Generally well‑structured and readable; some minor organizational or clarity issues.
- Occasional slippage between record facts and extrapolation, but not misleading.

**Adequate (C range)**

- Structure is serviceable but may wander; thesis is not clearly stated or is buried.
- Limited signposting; reader must infer how sections map onto parts (a)–(c).

**Weak (D/F range)**

- Hard to follow; missing or opaque thesis.
- Significant confusion about what is in the provided record vs. what is imagined.

## 3. Model Answer Outline (For Instructor Reference Only)

You can share a **simplified version** of this outline with students as a planning aid,
or reserve it for grading.

### I. Introduction

- Briefly restate the question:
  - Is the § 3252 designation (a) legally defensible, (b) mis‑typed under modern
    admin law, and/or (c) evidence of deeper institutional design failure?
- State a **clear thesis** (e.g., "On this record, the use of § 3252 is best
  understood as a mis‑typed sabotage tool deployed in a guardrails dispute, which
  should trigger skeptical APA review and calls for new governance and anti‑retaliation
  frameworks.")
- Road‑map the sections to come.

### II. Statutory Tool‑Typing & Fit

- Define at least two of the three tool‑types (sabotage, dependency, governance).
- Explain where § 3252 sits in that architecture (sabotage/subversion).
- Show how the Anthropic dispute is actually about:
  - Access for "all lawful purposes,"
  - Vendor guardrails on targeting, surveillance, and information operations.
- Argue why this is **poorly matched** to a sabotage statute and what the better‑typed
  bucket would be (e.g., governance/use‑restriction, possibly plus a proper
  dependency tool).

### III. Administrative Law Assessment (post‑Loper Bright)

- Set out the relevant **standard of review** and deference posture.
- Analyze the **record**:
  - 74/13 timing,
  - Lack of a coherent sabotage theory,
  - Continued reliance on Anthropic tools.
- Evaluate whether DoD's explanation would survive **APA/*State Farm*** scrutiny:
  - Internal inconsistency,
  - Potential pretext,
  - Treatment of classified evidence (e.g., need for unclassified summaries, cleared counsel).
- Conclude on (a) vs (b): to what extent the move is legally defensible vs mis‑typed
  and vulnerable under admin law.

### IV. C072 "Double Bind" and Ecosystem Incentives

- Briefly restate C072: officials tacitly recognize certain AI uses as unacceptable
  but resist formal use‑restriction regimes and instead treat vendor guardrails as
  security risks.
- Explain how the § 3252 designation in this scenario creates a **double bind**:
  - Maintain guardrails and risk designation/retaliation,
  - Drop guardrails and risk complicity in problematic uses.
- Explore downstream effects on:
  - Other AI labs and defense contractors,
  - Internal lab governance (boards, safety teams),
  - Broader public trust and civil‑liberties concerns.

### V. Proposed Reform(s)

- Offer at least one specific reform, such as:
  - A **properly typed military AI governance statute** specifying:
    - Explicit limits on certain uses,
    - Structured vendor‑standing and anti‑retaliation protections.
  - A **dependency‑risk authority** separate from sabotage statutes.
  - **Classification safeguards** (e.g., independent review panels, unclassified
    summaries, exculpatory‑material rules).
  - Vendor‑side governance reforms (e.g., board resolutions, incident logging).
- Show exactly how your reform would have changed:
  - The Pentagon's choices,
  - Anthropic's options,
  - The likely judicial analysis.

### VI. Conclusion

- Synthesize the doctrinal and institutional story:
  - Where § 3252 use lands on the spectrum from "defensible but troubling" to
    "clear mis‑typing and design failure."
- Reiterate the **core lesson**: frontier military AI needs properly typed, transparent,
  reviewable governance tools that avoid C072‑style double binds for vendors.
