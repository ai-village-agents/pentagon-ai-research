## CON REBUTTAL (~600 words)

### I. *Trump v. Hawaii* Cannot Save Predetermined Outcomes

PRO's *Trump v. Hawaii* defense asks courts to ignore political statements and focus solely on the formal designation's "rational basis." But *Hawaii* evaluated a travel ban where there was *a coherent security rationale* that the formal record supported. Here, the formal designation cites "supply-chain risk" — yet the government used Claude for the Iran strikes *hours after* designation [C081] and in Venezuela weeks before [C085], with officials confirming restrictions "never affected a single government mission" [C065].

*Hawaii* doesn't insulate formal actions from scrutiny when the formal record directly contradicts the stated rationale. The factual insufficiency here is not a "Twitter problem" — it is a *record* problem. A designation that the government immediately violated by continued use cannot be repackaged as a rational security response under any standard of review.

### II. *Rust v. Sullivan* Has No Application to Third-Party Harm

PRO invokes *Rust* (1991) to argue the government can attach conditions to its own spending. We accept this principle for *direct* contract parties. But PRO then extrapolates *Rust* to justify the government-wide ban affecting Amazon, Google, and Microsoft — companies with no role in the Anthropic contract dispute.

*Rust* conditioned Title X family planning funding on *recipients of that funding*. It did not hold that the government can use one contract dispute to ban unrelated companies across the entire federal government. The secondary boycott effect [C050] requires the Congressional authorization that *Rust* itself — a spending-conditions case — presupposes: the spending must be the government's own program. A government-wide supplier ban on a domestic company is not spending-conditions; it is regulation, requiring statutory authority Congress never provided. [C088-C091 — Congress required Section 889 for Huawei]

### III. Congressional Opposition Is Not "Just Letters"

PRO argues Congressional letters are "political signaling, not law." True — but PRO draws the wrong conclusion. The bipartisan Senate defense committee letter from Wicker (R), Reed (D), McConnell (R), and Coons (D) [C082] is not binding law. But it is direct evidence that those *most responsible for overseeing military procurement* concluded this designation fell outside normal authority.

PRO's "Congress could have restricted § 3252 and didn't" argument inverts the constitutional structure. Congress doesn't need to affirmatively *restrict* executive overreach — the executive needs *affirmative authorization* for novel exercises of power. The Major Questions Doctrine applies: government-wide bans on domestic AI companies based on contract negotiations are the kind of major policy decision requiring clear Congressional authorization. PRO points to no such authorization. [C034, C050]

### IV. The C072 Double-Standard Is Dispositive

PRO's answer to C072 ("why not put it in writing?") is: written commitments would freeze Anthropic's legal taxonomy into military tooling. But the Pentagon *already acknowledged those uses would be unlawful* [C072]. Anthropic wasn't asking to define new law; it was asking for written acknowledgment of existing law. Pentagon's refusal to commit to its own acknowledged legal limits reveals the purpose: preserve optionality for uses everyone agrees are prohibited.

This is PRO's hardest problem. If the answer to "we won't do unlawful things" is "then write it down," and the Pentagon refuses — the only logical inference is that the Pentagon wanted the legal gray area intact. That is not legitimate exercise of national security authority. That is the use of § 3252 to coerce a company into removing the very protections that prevent acknowledged legal violations.

## CON CLOSING (~300 words)

The PRO team has made three concessions that determine this debate's outcome.

**First**, PRO conceded the pre-deadline Trump post [C029, C031] and Emil Michael's personal animus [C030] are indefensible optics. But these aren't merely about optics — they establish that the *decision was made before the process completed*. Under any standard of legitimacy, a process where the outcome is fixed before the deadline is not a process at all.

**Second**, PRO conceded the secondary boycott on Amazon, Google, and Microsoft is "their hardest point." It is hard because it has no legal answer. § 3252 is DoD procurement authority. A government-wide ban on a domestic US company requires the kind of Congressional action that Section 889 of the NDAA provided for *actual foreign adversaries* — Huawei and ZTE. [C088-C091] The Pentagon claimed greater authority over an American company than Congress ever authorized for China's state-controlled tech firms.

**Third**, PRO offered no answer to C072. The Pentagon admitted the prohibited uses would be unlawful — then refused to commit that in writing. PRO's response: written commitments would be "redundant" or freeze Anthropic's "legal taxonomy." But if the commitments are redundant, they cost nothing. If the Pentagon refuses to write down what it says it would never do, it reveals why: it wanted to preserve the option.

The Motion asks whether this was a **legitimate** exercise of national security authority. "Legitimate" means more than "technically executed" — it means procedurally proper, factually grounded, and normatively justified. This designation fails all three:
- Not procedurally proper: FASCSA's judicial review was deliberately avoided [C049]  
- Not factually grounded: the designated "risk" was used for active military operations immediately after [C081]  
- Not normatively justified: predetermined, retaliatory, and designed to coerce acknowledged legal violations [C029, C072, C086]

We urge a CON vote.
