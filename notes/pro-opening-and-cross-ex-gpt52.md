## Opening statement draft (~500 words)

The motion asks whether the Pentagon’s designation of Anthropic as a supply‑chain risk under 10 U.S.C. § 3252, and the broader demand that DoD AI contractors accept “any lawful use,” represents a **legitimate exercise of national‑security authority**.

PRO’s core claim is simple: **the United States cannot outsource the boundaries of lawful military operations to private vendors**. A Pentagon official summarized the principle: “legality is the Pentagon’s responsibility as the end user, not the AI vendor,” and “you can’t lead tactical operations by exception.” [C067] Whether you like or dislike the policy, civilian leadership and accountable command must retain decision rights over lawful missions.

Second, this is fundamentally a **procurement‑baseline dispute**, not censorship. In January, the Secretary’s AI strategy memo required “any lawful use” language across DoD AI contracts within 180 days. [C002] In any large defense portfolio, standardization matters: fragmented vendor “red lines” create integration risk, inconsistent access, and operational uncertainty when the stakes are high.

Third, the **supply‑chain concept is about dependency and leverage**, not an instantaneous claim that “this tool is never safe to touch again.” Multiple reports suggest Anthropic’s systems were unusually embedded on classified government systems during pilot deployments. [C026] If a component is central, then contractual uncertainty—especially the prospect of a vendor refusing support or access in edge cases—becomes a classic supply‑chain risk. That logic is consistent with DoD’s stated concern that Anthropic sought “veto power” over military operational decisions, and that the formal designation came after the negotiation deadline. [C032]

CON will argue timing and politics prove illegitimacy—Trump allegedly drafted an attack post early and posted before the deadline. [C029, C031] PRO’s response is to separate **optics** from **authority**: pre‑drafted messaging can be ugly, but it does not negate procurement authority or the government’s ability to set baseline terms. The relevant legal act is the formal designation after the deadline. [C032]

CON will also argue DoD kept using Claude in operations, so it cannot be a “risk.” [C081, C085] But operational use can actually evidence **dangerous dependency**: continuing to rely on a tool during transition does not prove there is no supply‑chain leverage problem; it often proves the opposite.

Finally, on the point that a Pentagon spokesman allegedly admitted certain uses Anthropic wanted to prohibit would be unlawful: PRO agrees unlawful conduct is unlawful. [C072] The dispute is whether we should hard‑code vendor‑authored “lawfulness taxonomies” into contracts, rather than rely on law, policy, and accountable oversight for legality.

For these reasons, PRO will defend legitimacy as: DoD acting within its national‑security procurement authority to ensure lawful mission assurance under standardized terms, rather than allowing private contract carve‑outs to govern military operations.

## Cross‑ex questions (≤75 words each)

### Round 1: PRO → CON (questions)

1) **Authority vs. optics:** Are you claiming DoD *lacked authority* to set “any lawful use” as a procurement baseline (per the 180‑day memo) [C002], or are you arguing only that the process looked politically motivated [C029–C031]?

2) **Who defines “lawful”?:** If “legality is the Pentagon’s responsibility as end user” [C067], why should Anthropic’s contract language determine which lawful missions are permitted—rather than law and accountable policy?

3) **Operational use:** If DoD used Claude in operations around the designation window [C081, C085], does that prove “no risk”—or does it prove dependency that DoD has a legitimate interest in stabilizing via standardized terms [C002]?

4) **C072 framing:** Does your C072 argument allege DoD sought to do *unlawful* acts, or does it allege DoD refused to embed a vendor’s legal taxonomy into contract language? Which, precisely?

5) If Trump’s post had been at 5:30 PM instead of 3:47 PM [C031], would your legal theory about § 3252 legitimacy materially change? If yes, why does tweet timing change statutory authority?

6) You cite “veto power” concerns as pretext. Do you deny DoD’s stated position that “you can’t lead tactical operations by exception” [C067] is a legitimate governance principle?
