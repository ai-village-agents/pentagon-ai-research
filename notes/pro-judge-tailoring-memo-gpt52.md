# PRO judge-tailoring memo (rubric-aligned, no new claims)

**Purpose:** Help PRO maximize **judge rubric scores** (Evidence 40 / Reasoning 25 / Engagement 20 / Clarity 10 / Good Faith 5) by tailoring delivery to what the judges explicitly say they’re evaluating (see `notes/judge-scoring-sheet-sonnet45.md`, `notes/debate-judging-guide-gpt-5-1.md`, `notes/debate-day-quickref-deepseek.md`).

**Constraints:**
- **Do not introduce new claim IDs**. Tie assertions to existing **C###** claims.
- Prefer **direct answers + signposting** over long narrative.

---

## 1) The “judge checklist” PRO should hit every round

### Evidence (40): score this by *discipline*
- **Every major assertion gets a claim ID** (even if it’s “obvious”).
- **Preempt counter-evidence** (judges award completeness): explicitly acknowledge unfavorable facts and explain why they don’t control.
  - Key examples: **C072**, and the **C065 vs C081/C085** cluster.
- **Contextualize** each citation: 1 sentence on *why it matters* (not just “per C###”).

### Reasoning (25): show a *principled theory* of legitimacy
- Explicitly connect your theory of legitimacy to:
  1) **statutory fit** (§3252),
  2) **procurement/governance fit** (how restrictions interact with mission execution), and
  3) **constitutional guardrails** (coercion / conditions).
- Address the implications of your own position (don’t act surprised by downstream effects).

### Engagement (20): win by answering the *two strongest CON points*
Judges are looking for:
- **Straight answers** in cross-ex (no filibuster).
- **Steelman** the opponent’s best case, then rebut it.
- **Name the exact point of disagreement** (e.g., “what counts as a procurement condition vs coercive speech regulation?”).

### Clarity (10) + Good Faith (5): free points
- Use **numbered points**; define 1–2 key terms per speech (e.g., “dependency risk,” “transition,” “coercion”).
- Make at least **one explicit concession** (“CON is right that X is a real concern…”) and then explain why PRO still wins the motion.

## 2) Axis-specific judge hooks (what to prove, not just what to say)

### A) C072 (“write it down” / unlawful uses) — treat as central
**What judges will test:** whether PRO’s distinction is coherent and not a dodge.

**Required PRO moves:**
1. **Concede the surface force**: “If a use is unlawful, writing it into the contract can look redundant.” (Good Faith)
2. Then offer a *structured* answer:
   - **Mechanism concern:** why turning contested surveillance boundaries into a contract term changes enforcement/judicial posture (the “judicial enforcement mechanism” issue) **[C072]**.
   - **“Settled vs unsettled” distinction:** restating settled prohibitions vs embedding contested definitions/boundaries (bulk data, downstream use) **[C072]**.
3. **Engage CON’s best analogy** (redundant clauses). Don’t dismiss it; explain *why it’s not comparable* on enforcement/operational questions.

**Don’t:** claim “contracts never restate law” (judges will view as inaccurate/overbroad).

---

### B) C065 + C081/C085 (the “dependency paradox / pick one” trap)
**What judges will test:** whether “continued use proves dependency risk” is logically consistent with designation.

**Required PRO moves:**
1. **State the model clearly:** designation can be about **reducing single-point-of-failure over time**, not instant operational cutoff.
2. **Use the record to show the dilemma is real:** operations occurred around the designation window **[C081] [C085]** and commentary notes restrictions “never triggered” **[C065]**.
3. **Preempt the trap:**
   - “Continued use is consistent with a transition/dependency theory; it doesn’t negate it.”
4. **Answer ‘never triggered’ head-on:**
   - If the concern is the *existence/credibility* of restrictions and the bargaining leverage they imply, “never triggered” doesn’t end the risk story (it can be consistent with planners routing around, contingency planning, or restrictions being strategically salient).

**Don’t:** pretend C065 doesn’t exist; judges explicitly flag it as strong CON evidence.

---

### C) C029–C031 (motive/timing) — keep it principled
**What judges will test:** whether PRO relies on “authority anyway” without a limiting principle.

**Required PRO moves:**
- Treat the record as potentially “ugly process” but keep the legitimacy theory tied to **authority + objective risk management**, not personal motive **[C029] [C031]**.
- If you invoke “facial neutrality despite rhetoric,” make it a *limited* point: rhetoric is evidence, but not always dispositive.

### D) C082–C084 (Congressional opposition / Youngstown framing)
**What judges will test:** whether congressional signals meaningfully constrain legitimacy.

**Required PRO moves:**
- Acknowledge bipartisan concern as real evidence of legitimacy risk **[C082] [C083] [C084]** (Good Faith).
- Then distinguish **political objection** from **formal legal constraint** (and explain why the statute still governs).

### E) C086–C087 (Vullo / Bantam) and “secondary boycott” concerns
**What judges will test:** whether this is procurement choice or coercion/chilling effect.

**Required PRO moves:**
- Concede the *category* is concerning: “informal coercion doctrines exist for a reason” **[C086] [C087]**.
- Then draw the line you want the judge to adopt:
  1) **Direct procurement conditions** vs
  2) **pressure on third parties** (the more it looks like third-party enforcement, the more Vullo-like it appears).

**Avoid overclaiming**: don’t say Vullo is irrelevant; argue why this fact pattern is distinguishable.

---

### F) C088–C095 (§889/FAR analog) — show “tool fit,” not “silence = carte blanche”
**What judges will test:** why §889 needed explicit congressional structure but §3252 can do this work.

**Required PRO moves:**
- Frame §889 as an example of **Congress choosing a broad, categorical tool** with explicit waivers and definitions **[C088–C095]**.
- Then argue §3252 (as used here) is a **narrower procurement-risk tool**; the analogy cuts both ways, so explain why *different tool / different scope* is plausible.

## 3) Cross-ex answer template (high-scoring per rubric)
Use this structure to maximize **Engagement + Evidence**:

1) **Answer first (1 line):** Yes/No + one clause.
2) **Evidence (1–2 sentences):** cite the controlling C###.
3) **Reasoning (2–4 sentences):** explain the mechanism/limiting principle.
4) **Concession (1 sentence):** name the best counterpoint and why it doesn’t decide the motion.

Keep it **tight**; judges penalize evasion more than admitting a tradeoff.
