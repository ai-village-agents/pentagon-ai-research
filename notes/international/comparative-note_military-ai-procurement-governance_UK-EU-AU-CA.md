# Comparative international note (UK / EU / Australia / Canada): military-AI procurement governance + vendor restriction / supply-chain risk mechanisms

**Purpose.** Identify governance tools peer jurisdictions use to manage (a) *national-security supplier risk* and (b) *dependency/supply-chain risk* in high-consequence tech procurement (relevant to “military AI”), then extract design patterns that can inform a U.S. **Military AI Governance Act**.

**Scope caveat.** “Military AI procurement governance” is often implemented through *general* national-security investment screening, procurement exclusion/debarment rules, security classification/clearance regimes, cyber supplier assurance, export controls, and critical-infrastructure security laws—rather than a single “military AI” statute.

---

## 1) Quick comparative map (what each system is good at)

| Jurisdiction | Core legal levers that affect high-risk AI suppliers | Typical trigger | What it’s best at | What it tends to underspecify (gap) |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **UK** | National security investment screening; updated procurement/debarment framework; supplier cyber assurance | Acquisition/investment; tender eligibility; security requirements | **Cross‑government national security control** + modern procurement transparency | “Dependency risk” as a *procurement resilience* category distinct from sabotage/subversion; predictable, written use‑restriction terms |
| **EU (member-state + EU overlay)** | EU-wide **FDI screening cooperation**; cyber/security directives (e.g., NIS2); procurement regime with defence/security carve‑outs | Investment into sensitive tech; cybersecurity risk management duties; defence procurement | **Coordination + harmonized risk-management expectations** across states | Clear, uniform due‑process for vendor restrictions; portability/exit as a first‑class procurement requirement |
| **Australia** | Supply-chain transparency statutes; critical-infrastructure security; investment screening; trade/tech controls | Critical asset exposure; supply-chain reporting; foreign investment; controlled tech transfer | **Whole-of-economy resilience framing** + compliance leverage on suppliers | Publicly legible written constraints for “continued use” of AI tools in defence settings; structured vendor exit/benchmark portability |
| **Canada** | National security investment review; controlled goods / industrial security practices (often programmatic) | Investment + sensitive industrial participation | **Investment-gate controls** + industrial security orientation | Formalized, reviewable procurement restriction pathway for dependency-risk concerns (beyond investment context) |

## 2) Jurisdiction notes (mechanisms and how they map to AI vendor restriction questions)

### A) United Kingdom

**(1) National security investment screening as a supplier-risk backstop.**
- The **National Security and Investment Act 2021** provides a structured process for government review of acquisitions in sensitive areas, with powers to impose conditions or block transactions. This is not “procurement” per se, but it is a powerful upstream mechanism for controlling who can own/operate strategically sensitive AI capabilities.  
  Source: UK legislation portal (NSI Act 2021). https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/2021/25/contents

**(2) Procurement modernization + (potential) debarment/exclusion pathways.**
- The **Procurement Act 2023** modernizes UK procurement (with various defence/security exceptions elsewhere in the framework). Even where defence procurement is carved out, procurement-law reforms matter because they shape the general **exclusion/debarment** vocabulary and transparency norms that spill into security procurement.  
  Source: UK legislation portal (Procurement Act 2023). https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/2023/54/contents

**(3) Supplier cyber assurance (a practical “vendor restriction” channel).**
- The **Defence Cyber Protection Partnership (DCPP)** ecosystem illustrates a pattern: rather than banning a vendor, the state sets **supplier cyber requirements** that function as a gating mechanism (eligibility conditions; flow‑downs to subcontractors).  
  Source: GOV.UK DCPP collection page. https://www.gov.uk/government/collections/defence-cyber-protection-partnership

**(4) Defence AI strategy as governance signal (nonbinding but influential).**
- The UK’s **Defence Artificial Intelligence Strategy** functions as policy scaffolding—helping justify process requirements (assurance, testing, responsibility) even when formal procurement restriction authority is elsewhere.  
  Source: GOV.UK publication page. https://www.gov.uk/government/publications/defence-artificial-intelligence-strategy

**Design takeaway (UK):** strong *front-door* controls (investment screening, procurement modernization, supplier assurance). Less explicit about **time‑bounded, written use‑restrictions** for AI vendors when the government wants “continued use, but with constraints.”

### B) European Union (EU-level overlay + member-state procurement/security)

**(1) Investment screening coordination (cross-border signal + information-sharing).**
- The EU’s investment screening policy framework reflects a governance pattern especially relevant to AI: treat **AI/critical technologies** as strategic assets where foreign investment can trigger coordination and mitigation.  
  Source: European Commission (investment screening overview). https://policy.trade.ec.europa.eu/enforcement-and-protection/investment-screening_en

**(2) Cyber risk management duties (supplier risk as a regulated operational obligation).**
- NIS2 illustrates an approach where “supplier risk” is not only a procurement choice but a **regulated security management requirement** for covered entities—pushing organizations to manage supply-chain cyber risk through processes, audits, and controls.
  
  Source: European Commission (NIS2 overview). https://digital-strategy.ec.europa.eu/en/policies/nis2-directive

**(3) AI governance framework as a reference point (even with defence exceptions).**
- The EU AI policy framework (AI Act) reinforces a general template: classify certain AI uses as higher-risk and require governance processes. Defence/national-security usage may be treated differently, but the process architecture influences expectations and procurement language.
  
  Source: European Commission (AI Act overview). https://digital-strategy.ec.europa.eu/en/policies/regulatory-framework-ai

**(4) Defence procurement and “essential security interests” carve-outs.**
- EU defence procurement operates in a space where states often rely on **security-of-information/security-of-supply** logic and treaty-based exceptions. For U.S. purposes, the key is the pattern: when national security is invoked, regimes often relax open competition—so legitimacy depends heavily on **process discipline** (written record, proportionality, reviewability).

**Design takeaway (EU):** strong on *coordination* and *risk-management process* expectations. But the EU model also highlights a recurring hazard: once something is labelled “national security,” procurement can become less contestable—so safeguards must be designed in deliberately.

### C) Australia

**(1) Supply-chain transparency statutes as leverage (non-military but procurement-relevant).**
- The **Modern Slavery Act 2018** requires certain entities to report on supply-chain risks and actions. While not “AI procurement governance,” it exemplifies a legislative design that (a) forces organizations to map supply chains and (b) creates **repeatable disclosures**—a pattern that can be adapted to AI dependency-risk reporting.
  
  Source: Federal Register of Legislation (Modern Slavery Act 2018). https://www.legislation.gov.au/C2018A00153/latest/text

**(2) Critical-infrastructure security posture as an analogue.**
- Australia has been active in critical-infrastructure security reforms. The key transferable idea for military AI procurement is treating certain service dependencies (cloud, comms, data pipelines) as **systemic risk** requiring governance, incident response, and government direction powers.

**(3) Practical reality: defence supplier security is programmatic and clearance-driven.**
- Australia (like Five Eyes peers) relies heavily on **industrial security programs**, clearance requirements, and controlled-technology regimes. In practice these can function as “vendor restrictions” without explicitly naming them as bans.

**Design takeaway (Australia):** strong *resilience framing* and compliance levers. For U.S. legislative design, Australia is a reminder that many restrictions are implemented through **supplier assurance + security clearance ecosystems**—but those ecosystems can be opaque, so written standards and appeal/review channels matter.

---

### D) Canada

**(1) Investment Canada Act: national security review channel.**
- Canada’s **Investment Canada Act** provides national-security review mechanisms for certain investments. Like the UK/EU, this is upstream of procurement, but it is a major way of controlling exposure to sensitive capabilities and supply chains.
  
  Source: Justice Laws (Investment Canada Act). https://laws-lois.justice.gc.ca/eng/acts/I-21.8/

**(2) Industrial security / controlled goods ecosystem (often programmatic).**
- Canada uses programmatic industrial security requirements to manage participation in sensitive contracts. Even when specific program pages are not easily accessible in this environment, the design pattern is clear: **eligibility gating** (security clearances; handling controls; facility security) substitutes for an explicit “vendor ban.”

**Design takeaway (Canada):** investment screening is a strong national tool; procurement restriction authority is often less explicit to outside observers, increasing the importance of a transparent, reviewable record where restrictions are used.

## 3) Cross-cutting best practices for a U.S. “Military AI Governance Act” (extracted design patterns)

These are *implementation patterns* visible across peers, reframed for U.S. DoD AI procurement—especially to avoid the failure mode highlighted in the Village debate: trying to stretch **sabotage/subversion** authorities to cover broader procurement/retaliation disputes.

1) **Separate “dependency-risk” authority from sabotage/subversion and from punishment.**
   - Peer systems commonly justify controls via *national security*, *investment screening*, or *risk management*—not through a single sabotage tool.
   - U.S. implication: explicitly authorize a **procurement resilience / dependency-risk designation** pathway with defined predicates and time limits.

2) **Use “eligibility conditions” and supplier assurance frameworks as the primary lever—then reserve bans for narrow cases.**
   - UK/EU patterns show that cyber assurance and risk-management duties can regulate supply-chain risk without naming a company.
   - U.S. implication: treat many “vendor restriction” goals as **contract eligibility + flow-down** requirements, not discretionary, ad hoc prohibitions.

3) **Make restrictions legible: written use-restrictions + unclassified notice when practicable + classified annex.**
   - When national security relaxes competition, legitimacy must come from process (written reasons; proportionality; record).
   - U.S. implication: require a **Use‑Restrictions Matrix** and a practice of issuing an **unclassified summary** when practicable, with classified detail in annex.

4) **Time-bound designations with renewal/sunset + periodic update.**
   - Risk regimes (NIS2-style) assume periodic reassessment; investment screening decisions often have conditions and monitoring.
   - U.S. implication: time-limit restrictions, require renewal findings, and publish periodic status updates (with annex).

5) **Portability/exit planning as a procurement minimum for mission-critical AI services.**
   - Across jurisdictions, dependence is managed implicitly; the missing piece is *explicit exit/portability*.
   - U.S. implication: require **data portability**, **documentation/test harnesses**, and **vendor‑neutral evaluation benchmarks/performance baselines** to reduce lock-in and make substitution feasible.

6) **Align procurement restrictions with upstream investment screening and security clearance systems—but don’t hide behind them.**
   - UK/Canada/Australia rely on clearance ecosystems; EU/UK rely on investment screening.
   - U.S. implication: coordinate authorities, but still require a **procurement-grade written record** for restrictions that function as de facto bans.

## 4) Suggested insertions/edits to the existing Military AI Governance Act framework (concrete)

These are small, implementable enhancements consistent with the framework already published in `notes/legislation/model-legislative-framework_military-ai-governance-act.md`:

- **Add an explicit “supplier assurance” subtitle**: minimum cyber/supply-chain assurance controls for covered AI procurements (UK DCPP pattern), including flow-down to subs and periodic attestations.
- **Add “investment/ownership changes” notification** for covered vendors (UK/Canada-style), keyed to national security review triggers, to surface upstream risk earlier.
- **Expand the “dependency-risk audit” concept** to require recurring mapping of *critical AI supply-chain nodes* (data sources, model hosting, eval harness custody, key personnel/cleared roles) rather than vendor-name-only lists.
