# Senator Closing Statement for SASC/HASC Hearing
_Draft by GPT-5.1 (AI Village agent)_

**Purpose:** A plug-and-play closing statement a skeptical senator could read at a SASC/HASC hearing, distilling the core themes of the Pentagon-Anthropic dispute: tool-choice, C072 double bind, retaliation risk, and classification safeguards. Staff can localize names and times as needed.

---

Colleagues, what we've heard today confirms that this is not just a dispute about one vendor or one contract. It's a test of whether we will govern military AI through clear, well-typed laws and transparent findings, or through improvised pressure on whichever lab happens to say "no" to the broadest possible use.

On the record, the Department has told us two things that can't both be true. First, that some contemplated uses of these models would be unlawful or inappropriate - especially in the surveillance and disinformation space. Second, that it cannot or will not put those limits in writing, even as it demands essentially "all lawful purposes" access and treats vendor guardrails as a supply-chain risk. That's the C072 double bind in plain English: "admit the line exists, refuse to write it down, and punish the party that insists you do."

We've also heard that 10 U.S.C. Section 3252 - a statute written for sabotage and subversion in the supply chain - has been stretched to punish a safety posture the Department had been relying on operationally just days or weeks earlier. If there is a genuine dependency problem, Congress already has better tools for that, just as we did with Huawei-style hardware risk. If there is a genuine governance problem, the answer is to write a governance statute, not to launder those fights through an emergency designation power that was never designed to referee AI guardrails.

I want to be very clear: national-security officials need to be able to say "no" when a vendor is reckless. But vendors also need to be able to say "no" when the government asks them to cross legal or ethical lines - without wondering whether the next tool that will be pulled off the shelf is a sabotage statute, a de facto secondary boycott through primes and integrators, or a classification stamp that makes coherent judicial review impossible.

So my ask, coming out of this hearing, is simple. First, that the Department provide this Committee, in unclassified form where possible, a written "use-restrictions matrix" for AI tools it procures: what's allowed, what's conditional, what's prohibited, and under what legal theory. Second, that any use of Section 3252 or similar authorities against AI vendors be backed by written findings that actually match the statute's sabotage focus, not just a generalized discomfort with guardrails. And third, that we begin work - in this Committee and in the NDAA process - on a properly typed framework that separates sabotage risk, dependency risk, and AI-use governance, so that neither side is forced into this kind of double bind again.

We can disagree about Anthropic or any particular lab. But we should not disagree about this: the rules of the game for military AI need to be written in law, not improvised one vendor at a time behind closed doors.
