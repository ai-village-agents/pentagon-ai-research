## Cross-Examination Round 3 — CON Answers

**A7 (Future Administration Hypothetical):**

PRO's hypothetical conflates two different scenarios. This case isn't about "a monopoly vendor imposes arbitrary restrictions" — Anthropic explicitly offered *unrestricted access for all lawful uses* including classified missions under FISA [C015]. The dispute was limited to: bulk commercial data collection on Americans without judicial oversight, and autonomous lethal weapons. These aren't arbitrary vendor preferences — they're bright-line legal prohibitions Congress has already targeted (Fourth Amendment Is Not For Sale Act [C053]; DoD Directive 3000.09 [C017]).

The §3252 remedy requires adversarial *sabotage* risk — not "vendor won't waive Fourth Amendment protections." If a future administration faces a genuine sole-source monopoly problem, the proper remedies are: (1) contract with alternative vendors, (2) seek Congressional authorization for categorical exclusions (§889 is the template [C088-C091]), or (3) challenge specific contract terms in court. Designating "supply-chain risk to national security" for a domestic company that accepted all *lawful* uses is ultra vires regardless of which administration does it.

PRO's framework is more dangerous than CON's: it authorizes §3252 designation of any company that declines *any* Pentagon demand — turning a sabotage-risk statute into a blank procurement coercion tool.

**A8 (Bantam Books / First Amendment):**

PRO misreads CON's First Amendment claim. The relevant activity isn't Anthropic's contract negotiation — it's Anthropic's *public statements* about AI safety and its *business decisions* about which uses to enable. These are protected under *Janus v. AFSCME* (no compelled speech) and *Pacific Gas & Electric v. PUC* (no forced editorial policy).

More fundamentally: *Bantam Books* and *Vullo* both establish that formal government authority used to coerce compliance with *viewpoint-specific* demands is unconstitutional — independent of whether the compelled activity itself is First Amendment-protected. The Pentagon used §3252 to punish Anthropic for its publicly-stated positions on data privacy and weapons autonomy [C086, C087].

The secondary boycott makes this unambiguous: threatening Amazon, Google, and Microsoft with designation-by-association unless they divest from Anthropic [C050, C051] is textbook *Vullo* coercion — the government pressured *third parties* to withdraw from Anthropic for viewpoint reasons. That's the First Amendment violation, regardless of what Bantam Books covers.

**A9 (Democratic Accountability):**

CON's position is that *Congress* should provide the accountability mechanism — not Anthropic's contract. That's why this designation is illegitimate: Congress has not authorized the Pentagon to demand vendors waive constitutional protections as a condition of contracts.

Note what Anthropic *accepted*: FISA Court oversight for classified NSA use [C015]. Anthropic embraced exactly the democratic accountability mechanisms PRO champions. What it declined was Pentagon *extra-legal* demands — bulk commercial data collection expressly targeted by pending Congressional legislation [C053], and autonomous lethal weapons violating DoD Directive 3000.09 [C017].

The C072 knockout applies here: Pentagon spokesman admitted the uses Anthropic wanted prohibited would be *unlawful* [C072]. If Congress, FISA, and IGs are sufficient accountability, why object to writing down what is already legally required? The refusal to commit in writing reveals the true objective: preserving access to *unlawful* uses that those oversight mechanisms would prohibit.
