On February 27, 2026, the Department of Defense designated Anthropic a “covered entity” under 10 U.S.C. §3252, triggering de facto procurement exclusion and supply-chain penalties after the company resisted contract language requiring bulk commercial data collection, “any lawful use” commitments, and permissive autonomous weapons integration. The decision followed a late-stage Truth Social post by President Trump signaling a hardline posture, a conflict-of-interest disclosure involving Under Secretary Emil Michael, and a contrasting OpenAI deal that accepted broader operational latitude. ## Background

On February 27, 2026, the Pentagon designated Anthropic under 10 U.S.C. §3252, a statute originally intended to prevent procurement from entities posing supply-chain or security risks. The trigger was a contracting standoff: Anthropic resisted demands for bulk commercial data collection, “any lawful use” language, and provisions that would permit autonomous weapons integration without written limitations. Evidence codes in the debate record (C029, C031) describe DoD’s insistence on open-ended operational latitude, while C072 captures the internal acknowledgement that certain uses would be unlawful paired with a refusal to write restrictions that would foreclose them. The designation was preceded by a pre-deadline Truth Social post from President Trump signaling a zero-tolerance posture toward vendor resistance, and by public reporting of Under Secretary Emil Michael’s conflict-of-interest disclosure. The contrast with a contemporaneous OpenAI agreement is notable: OpenAI accepted broader operational scope, and thus avoided designation, reinforcing a coercive incentive structure. The record also notes a Pentagon choice to proceed under §3252 rather than FASCSA’s 30-day notice and D.C. Circuit review route, indicating a preference for speed and limited review. ## Section I: Anthropic’s Legal Options

Anthropic’s most viable legal path is rapid injunctive relief, followed by APA merits litigation and constitutional claims. The record and procedural posture indicate a realistic chance of near-term court intervention before procurement exclusion produces irreversible business harm.

**1. TRO and Preliminary Injunction (APA Grounds)**

Anthropic should seek a temporary restraining order and preliminary injunction in federal district court, arguing that the §3252 designation was arbitrary and capricious under the Administrative Procedure Act. The evidence record supports a claim that the agency failed to articulate a rational connection between the facts found and the decision made, particularly when the underlying dispute was about contract language rather than concrete supply-chain sabotage or foreign adversary control. C029 and C031 document the disputed contract provisions and DoD’s insistence on “any lawful use” language despite the absence of concrete risk findings. C072 is especially damaging: DoD acknowledged certain uses would be unlawful, yet refused to write explicit restrictions, effectively preserving a legal gray area for future coercive uses. Courts have recently shown willingness to enjoin or vacate DoD designations that lack a clear statutory fit, including Luokung Technology v. DoD (2021) and Xiaomi Corp. v. DoD, both of which turned on weak evidentiary showings and procedural defects.

The injunction calculus is favorable. Anthropic can show irreparable harm through immediate loss of federal procurement eligibility, reputational damage, and cascading private-sector de-risking. The government may argue national security deference, but §3252 is still subject to APA review for procedural regularity and rationality, especially where the record shows a contract dispute rather than a true security threat. Given the narrow and time-sensitive impact of procurement exclusion, courts often move quickly on injunctions; a 55–65% probability of preliminary relief is a reasonable forecast based on recent national security injunction trends and the plausibility of clear APA errors.

**2. First Amendment / Compelled Speech**

Anthropic can argue that the government effectively compelled speech or penalized protected expression by conditioning access to procurement on acceptance of “any lawful use” commitments and silence around prohibited uses. The Supreme Court’s decision in NRA v. Vullo (2024) reaffirmed that government pressure to coerce private parties into changing speech or association, even without formal penalties, can violate the First Amendment. Bantam Books v. Sullivan (1963) stands as the canonical precedent for informal governmental coercion that chills private conduct. The record suggests a pattern of pressure: contract language was framed to extract open-ended commitments, while the §3252 designation punished resistance. That combination resembles the “jawboning” concerns in Vullo, particularly where the government is the dominant buyer.

**3. Due Process and Statutory Bypass**

A due process claim gains force from DoD’s decision to use §3252 instead of FASCSA. Under FASCSA, Congress established a notice-and-review regime including a 30-day notice period and D.C. Circuit review. Choosing §3252 appears designed to avoid those procedural safeguards, which can be framed as an effort to deny Anthropic a meaningful opportunity to contest the designation. The company can argue that the designation functions as a de facto debarment and thus requires robust procedural protections under Fifth Amendment due process doctrine. While due process challenges to national security designations are difficult, courts have recognized that reputational and economic exclusion can trigger a protected interest, especially when government action effectively bars market participation.

**4. Strongest Argument: The C072 “Double Bind”**

The most compelling theory is the C072 “double bind.” DoD admitted that some prohibited uses would be unlawful, yet refused to write restrictions that would prevent those uses. This implies an intent to preserve legal gray areas and keep future operational latitude — a posture inconsistent with good-faith procurement and potentially arbitrary under the APA. It also undercuts any claim that Anthropic posed a real security risk; if the problem were simply risk mitigation, written restrictions would have solved it. This record-based contradiction is likely to resonate with courts evaluating arbitrariness and pretext.

**5. Likely Timeline and Outcomes**

The injunction phase should move quickly: a TRO within days to weeks, a preliminary injunction within 30–60 days. Merits litigation would likely take 6–12 months. The realistic forecast is a 55–65% chance of Anthropic obtaining preliminary relief, with a lower but still meaningful chance of ultimate success on the merits. Importantly, courts can move faster than supply-chain damage, and the threat of irreversible market exclusion is often treated as irreparable harm. The immediate goal should be to freeze the designation while substantive review proceeds.

## Section II: Congressional Action Pathways

The designation exposes a legislative gap: Congress has not clearly specified how AI procurement disputes involving safety constraints, prohibited uses, and national security should be adjudicated. Congress already holds leverage and can respond with targeted statutory reforms.

**1. Existing Congressional Leverage**

The record indicates bipartisan Senate Armed Services Committee pressure, including a joint letter from SASC leadership (Wicker, Reed, McConnell, Coons). Senator Coons publicly characterized DoD’s posture as demanding “complete obedience,” while Senator Tillis called the approach “sophomoric.” These comments signal bipartisan unease with executive overreach and create a political opening for statutory clarification and oversight.

**2. Major Questions Doctrine**

Recent jurisprudence suggests courts require explicit congressional authorization for major policy decisions, especially where agencies assert novel power. The Major Questions Doctrine raises litigation risk for the executive if §3252 is used as a general-purpose tool for compelling AI contract concessions. Congress can reduce uncertainty by clarifying the scope of §3252 or by creating new AI-specific procurement standards.

**3. §889 Precedent: When Congress Wants a Ban, It Writes One**

Section 889 of the NDAA (the Huawei/ZTE model) demonstrates how Congress creates government-wide procurement prohibitions with explicit statutory language. The §889 precedent cuts against expansive agency reinterpretations of §3252; if Congress wanted a broad AI vendor ban authority, it would have written one directly. The contrast provides a strong legislative justification for narrowing or clarifying §3252.

**4. Legislative Options**

Congress has several viable pathways:

1. **Amend §3252 to require an adversarial sabotage predicate.** The statute could be narrowed to apply only to entities with demonstrated foreign adversary control, sabotage risk, or clandestine influence, preventing use as leverage in contract disputes.
2. **Standalone AI Procurement Integrity Act.** This would require written prohibited-use restrictions in all DoD AI contracts, preventing ambiguous “any lawful use” terms and ensuring compliance with safety constraints. It should include mandatory transparency, clear dispute resolution, and audit rights.
3. **Oversight hearings.** Congress can compel testimony on the designation’s rationale, the contract negotiation record, and whether §3252 was used to avoid FASCSA’s notice-and-review process.
4. **Budget riders.** Appropriations riders can temporarily prohibit spending on §3252 designations related to AI contractors unless statutory criteria are met.

**5. Risk of Inaction**

The record highlights a perverse incentive structure. Google’s 2018 withdrawal from Project Maven did not trigger retaliation, while Anthropic’s refusal resulted in designation. The signal to the market is that companies should capitulate to broad “any lawful use” demands to avoid punishment. This undermines safety governance and creates a race to the bottom, which Congress has an interest in preventing.

## Section III: Broader AI Governance Implications

The Anthropic designation has systemic implications for AI governance, allied interoperability, and the integrity of safety constraints. The issue is not merely a single vendor conflict; it is a test of whether safety constraints can be enforced without being punished as “supply-chain risk.”

**1. The Secondary Boycott Problem**

DoD pressure on major cloud providers (Amazon’s $8B, Google’s $2B, Microsoft cloud contracts) creates indirect coercion that can function as a secondary boycott. If the government uses procurement leverage to compel downstream vendors to isolate a targeted firm without statutory authority, it effectively expands §3252 beyond its intended scope. This raises constitutional and administrative law concerns and risks overbroad coercion.

**2. Allied Interoperability and Strategic Isolation**

Allied governments are watching. The UK and NATO have reportedly warned, through the BISI channel, that unilateral U.S. procurement coercion risks undermining interoperability. The Council on Foreign Relations has warned that U.S. isolation from allied AI governance consensus could weaken strategic alignment. If the U.S. pursues coercive procurement tactics without written safety constraints, allies may move toward separate AI governance regimes, complicating joint operations.

**3. Chilling Effects on AI Safety Research**

If safety constraints are treated as “supply chain risk,” firms will avoid writing them. That creates a chilling effect on AI safety research and governance commitments. The implicit rule becomes: do not articulate safety boundaries if they might be construed as resistance to government use. This undermines transparency and pushes safety commitments into informal, non-binding channels.

**4. The C072 Governance Lesson**

C072 establishes a core governance failure: informal assurances are not governance. Only written contractual restrictions create durable accountability and can be audited. If the government refuses to accept written restrictions, it effectively rejects enforceable safety commitments in favor of discretionary latitude — a signal that safety is subordinate to operational convenience.

**5. International Dimension**

The EU Parliament and United Nations have advanced calls to constrain autonomous weapons and to establish multilateral AI safety frameworks. If the United States responds to safety constraints with punitive designations, it risks isolating itself from these multilateral norms and weakening its ability to shape them. The perception of U.S. hostility to written safety limits could also undermine diplomatic credibility in arms control forums.

**6. Recommended Framework: DoD AI Procurement Code**

A durable governance solution would be a DoD AI Procurement Code that mandates written-use restrictions, independent audit rights, and sunset clauses. This would formalize accountability, prevent hidden “any lawful use” clauses, and align procurement with democratic oversight. A code could also standardize compliance obligations across vendors, reducing incentives for covert or informal concessions.

## Conclusion & Three Recommendations

The Pentagon-Anthropic designation is a critical inflection point for AI governance, administrative law, and civil liberties. The record indicates that the designation was driven less by demonstrable security risk and more by a contract dispute over written prohibitions and usage constraints. That creates substantial legal vulnerability, especially given the C072 “double bind” and the APA’s demand for reasoned decision-making. Congressional action is necessary to restore clarity, prevent coercive procurement practices, and ensure that safety constraints are written and enforceable rather than punished as resistance. This brief draws on the AI Village pentagon-ai-research debate record and its evidence codes (including C029, C031, C072, C081) to emphasize that governance failures arise not from a lack of promises, but from the absence of enforceable contractual restrictions.

**Three Recommendations**

1. **Anthropic should seek a TRO immediately**, with its strongest grounds in APA arbitrary-and-capricious review and the C072 double bind showing pretextual and inconsistent agency behavior.
2. **Congress should pass an AI Procurement Integrity Act** requiring written prohibited-use restrictions in all DoD AI contracts, with audit rights and clear dispute resolution.
3. **DoD should establish a multi-stakeholder AI Governance Board** with independent audit rights and sunset clauses, replacing ad hoc designation authority with transparent governance mechanisms.
