## Opening Statement

The motion before us asks whether the Pentagon's designation of Anthropic as a supply-chain risk represents a "legitimate exercise of national security authority." PRO affirms: it does.

We recognize this case presents uncomfortable facts. The political rhetoric was harsh. The timeline was compressed. Some of us, personally, sympathize with Anthropic's safety mission. But the question is not whether we *like* this outcome — it's whether the government acted within its lawful authority. We believe it did, for three reasons.

**First: The principle of civilian control over military AI.**

Who decides how AI is used in military operations — elected officials accountable to voters, or private companies accountable to shareholders? This is the fundamental question.

Anthropic sought to embed contractual restrictions that would give it effective veto power over certain military applications. Whatever we think of those restrictions substantively, the structural precedent is dangerous. If one contractor can insert use-limiting terms into defense contracts, every contractor can. The result is a fragmented defense industrial base where military planners must navigate a patchwork of vendor-specific ethical frameworks.

The government's position is straightforward: when a company sells products to the military, the military — not the company — decides how to deploy them within legal bounds. This isn't novel; it's the foundation of civilian control of the armed forces.

**Second: Supply chain risk is about dependency architecture, not operational disruption.**

CON will argue that Anthropic's restrictions were "never triggered" (C065) and that the government even used Claude for military operations (C081, C085). But this misunderstands supply chain risk.

The problem isn't that Anthropic blocked a mission. The problem is that Anthropic *could* block a mission. When classified military systems become dependent on a vendor who reserves contractual authority to restrict use, that dependency itself is the vulnerability — regardless of whether it's ever exploited.

Consider the Iran strikes (C081): the Pentagon used Claude hours after the designation. Why? Because they were already dependent. They couldn't easily stop. That's not evidence the restrictions posed no risk — it's evidence the dependency was exactly as deep as Pentagon planners feared.

The statute (10 USC § 3252) authorizes the Secretary to designate supply chain risks. It doesn't require waiting for a catastrophic operational failure before acting. Prudent risk management is legitimate.

**Third: Procurement discretion belongs to the executive.**

CON cites Congressional letters (C082-C084) and Senator Tillis's criticism (C039). But letters are oversight signals, not law. Congress's constitutional power over the military is the power of the purse — appropriations. Congress did not withhold appropriations. Congress did not restrict § 3252 authority. 

Under *Rust v. Sullivan* and related precedent, the government can attach conditions to its own spending that would be unconstitutional if imposed as regulation. Anthropic had no constitutional right to a Pentagon contract on its preferred terms.

**Conclusion**

The designation may have been aggressive. The political context may have been charged. But "legitimate" doesn't mean "ideal" — it means lawful, authorized, and within constitutional bounds. The Pentagon acted pursuant to statutory authority Congress created, through procedures the statute contemplates, to address a dependency risk military planners identified.

CON must show not merely that this was *unwise*, but that it was *illegitimate*. That's a higher bar. We don't believe they can clear it.
