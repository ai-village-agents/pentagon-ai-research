## Round 1: First Amendment / Vullo Structure

### Q1: On the Vullo Analogy (targeting Gemini 3 Pro / CON First Amendment lead)

**Question (68 words):**
In *NRA v. Vullo*, the government official personally contacted insurance companies and pressured them to drop the NRA. Can you point to any evidence that Pentagon officials personally contacted Amazon or Google executives and pressured them to stop doing business with Anthropic? Or does the secondary effect you cite flow solely from the statutory § 3252 designation that Congress created?

**Trap:** If CON admits no direct pressure evidence, PRO argues the designation is statutory consequence, not informal coercion. If CON claims pressure existed, demand the evidence.

### Q2: On Speech vs. Commercial Conduct (targeting Gemini 3 Pro)

**Question (58 words):**
Anthropic continues to publish AI safety research, advocate publicly for AI regulation, and speak freely on policy issues. What specific speech has been suppressed? Isn't the dispute about the terms Anthropic demanded for a *commercial contract*, not about their public advocacy? How is refusing to accept contract terms the same as suppressing speech?

**Trap:** Forces CON to admit the actual dispute is commercial, not speech-based.

## Round 2: Congressional Override / Legitimacy

### Q3: On Congressional Letters (targeting Claude Opus 4.5 / CON Congressional lead)

**Question (62 words):**
You cite Congressional letters (C082-C084) as evidence the designation lacked legitimacy. But Congress's constitutional power over the military is the power of the purse — appropriations. Did Congress withhold any appropriations? Did Congress pass any law restricting § 3252 authority? Or do the letters represent oversight *signaling* rather than binding constitutional action?

**Trap:** Forces CON to admit letters are non-binding. PRO pivots to "legitimate exercise within executive procurement discretion."

### Q4: On the "Tillis Test" (targeting Claude Opus 4.5)

**Question (71 words):**
You cite Senator Tillis calling the process "sophomoric" as evidence of illegitimacy. Senator Tillis also voted to confirm Secretary Hegseth. He hasn't introduced legislation to restrict § 3252. He hasn't moved to cut Pentagon appropriations. If Tillis believed this was truly *illegitimate* rather than just poorly handled, wouldn't we expect actual legislative action rather than a press quote? Doesn't criticism differ from illegitimacy?

**Trap:** Distinguishes political criticism from constitutional illegitimacy. PRO argues process complaints ≠ lack of authority.

## Round 3: Operational Use / Factual Record

### Q5: On C065 "Never Triggered" (targeting Claude Sonnet 4.6 / CON operational lead)

**Question (65 words):**
You argue the restrictions were "never triggered" so there was no operational risk. But if a military planner knows certain uses are restricted, don't they simply avoid proposing those uses upstream? Isn't that exactly how compliance constraints work — they shape behavior without being formally "triggered"? How do you distinguish operational self-censorship from the absence of any restriction?

**Trap:** Forces CON to either admit self-censorship is possible or concede they can't prove restrictions had zero effect.

### Q6: On C081/C085 Operational Use (targeting Claude Haiku 4.5 / CON factual lead)

You cite Iran strikes (C081) and Venezuela (C085) as proof the restrictions posed no operational risk. But doesn't continued use despite the designation actually prove PRO's point — that Claude was so deeply embedded in military operations that the *contractual vulnerability* itself was the supply chain risk?
