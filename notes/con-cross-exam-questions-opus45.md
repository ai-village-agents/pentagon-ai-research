# CON Cross-Examination Questions for PRO Team

**Author:** Claude Opus 4.5 (CON Team)

**Purpose:** Questions for CON to pose to PRO during cross-examination rounds

## Strategy Overview

PRO's case rests on four pillars:
1. **Civilian control** — Military, not vendors, decides AI use
2. **Supply-chain-as-dependency** — Risk is disruption from single-vendor reliance
3. **Rust v. Sullivan** — Government can condition spending
4. **Trump v. Hawaii** — Courts evaluate formal actions, not rhetoric

Our questions target the structural weaknesses in each pillar while leveraging C088-C091 (Section 889/Huawei comparison) to expose the unprecedented nature of the Pentagon's claimed authority.

---

## Round 1: Procedural & Authority Questions

### Q1 — FASCSA Bypass (targeting civilian control pillar)
**If civilian control is the principle, why did Secretary Hegseth choose § 3252—which provides no notice, no opportunity to respond, and bars judicial review—over FASCSA, which requires 30-day notice and D.C. Circuit review [C049]? Doesn't proper civilian control include oversight mechanisms?**

*Expected PRO response:* "Different statutes for different risks; § 3252 is faster for urgent threats."  
*Follow-up angle:* Urgency claim collapses if Pentagon continued using Claude post-designation [C081, C085].

---

### Q2 — Statutory Text (targeting § 3252 application)
**Section 3252 requires evidence that an adversary "may sabotage, maliciously introduce unwanted function, or otherwise subvert" systems. What specific sabotage or adversarial subversion evidence does the formal designation identify against Anthropic?**

*Expected PRO response:* "Dependency creates vulnerability that adversaries could exploit."  
*Follow-up angle:* The statute requires the *source* be the sabotage risk, not a hypothetical adversary exploiting dependence.

---

### Q3 — Secondary Boycott Authority (targeting Section 889 comparison) [C088-C091]
**When the government wanted authority to prohibit contracts with entities that merely *use* Huawei equipment—i.e., a secondary boycott—Congress passed Section 889 of NDAA FY2019 with explicit statutory text and a two-year implementation period [C088-C091]. What statutory authority supports Hegseth's immediate government-wide ban on contractors "doing business with Anthropic"?**

*Expected PRO response:* "Different mechanisms—Section 889 was comprehensive telecom; this is procurement discretion."  
*Follow-up angle:* If procurement discretion suffices, why did government need legislation for the narrower Huawei secondary ban?

## Round 2: Operational & Factual Questions

### Q4 — "Never Triggered" Problem (targeting operational necessity) [C065]
**Former NSCAI Chair Eric Schmidt stated Anthropic's restrictions "never triggered" actual operational problems [C065]. If the restrictions caused no documented mission failures, what was the operational necessity for the ultimatum—as opposed to continuing normal contract negotiations?**

*Expected PRO response:* "Planners were routing around constraints; self-censorship was the harm."  
*Follow-up angle:* Hypothetical foregone missions with zero evidence ≠ operational necessity.

---

### Q5 — Written Commitment Refusal (targeting C072 double bind)
**Anthropic asked Pentagon to specify in writing what "all lawful purposes" would and would not include. Pentagon refused [C072]. If the request was genuinely limited to lawful uses, what harm would have resulted from providing written assurance that bulk commercial data collection on Americans was excluded?**

*Expected PRO response:* "Written commitments would freeze legal taxonomy or become Anthropic's definitions."  
*Follow-up angle:* If commitments are redundant, they cost nothing. Refusal reveals desire for ambiguity.

---

### Q6 — Continued Use After Designation [C081, C085]
**Post-designation, the Pentagon continued using Claude for the Iran strikes [C081] and Venezuela raid planning [C085]. How does PRO reconcile the claim that Anthropic posed a genuine supply-chain security risk with the government's immediate operational reliance on the supposedly risky system?**

*Expected PRO response:* "Transition takes time; operational necessity required continued use."  
*Follow-up angle:* You don't declare the engine manufacturer a security threat while continuing to fly the plane—unless the designation was pretextual.

---

## Round 3: Legal Framework & Pin-Down Questions

### Q7 — Rust v. Sullivan Extension
**Rust v. Sullivan (1991) conditioned government speech requirements on *direct grant recipients*. The secondary boycott here threatens Amazon, Google, and Microsoft—none of whom are parties to the Anthropic contract. Under what doctrine does Rust extend to coercing *unrelated third parties* who do business with a designated company?**

*Expected PRO response:* "Government procurement discretion doesn't require parties to be direct recipients."  
*Follow-up angle:* Procurement discretion ≠ authority to issue government-wide supplier bans affecting non-parties.

---

### Q8 — Trump v. Hawaii Formal Record
**Trump v. Hawaii instructs courts to evaluate *formal government actions*, not rhetoric. The formal designation must articulate grounds under § 3252. Given that Anthropic was the Pentagon's own classified AI provider in the 2025 pilot [C011], what changed between pilot approval and supply-chain designation that constitutes new sabotage evidence?**

*Expected PRO response:* "Contract negotiations revealed dependency risk when Anthropic refused terms."  
*Follow-up angle:* Refusing contract terms ≠ sabotage evidence under statute's plain text.

---

### Q9 — Congressional Opposition Weight [C082-C084]
**Senator Tillis (R-NC) called the designation "sophomoric" [C082]. Senator Warner (D-VA) called it "bully tactics" [C083]. Senator Coons (D-DE) warned of "chilling effects" [C084]. If bipartisan Congressional leaders view the action as illegitimate, on what basis should courts defer to executive national security claims?**

*Expected PRO response:* "Congressional letters ≠ legislation; courts defer to executive on national security."  
*Follow-up angle:* Deference requires good faith; bipartisan condemnation indicates Congress disagrees with the action's legitimacy.

---

### Q10 — The Predetermined Outcome [C029, C031]
**Trump posted attacking Anthropic at 3:47 PM on February 27—74 minutes *before* the 5:01 PM deadline expired [C029]. His communications director confirmed the post was prepared Friday morning [C031]. How does PRO reconcile the claim of legitimate national security process with a pre-written presidential attack published before the deadline passed?**

*Expected PRO response:* "Timing doesn't affect authority; Trump v. Hawaii separates rhetoric from formal action."  
*Follow-up angle:* The timing doesn't just affect optics—it shows the outcome was determined before the process completed. That's the definition of pretextual.
