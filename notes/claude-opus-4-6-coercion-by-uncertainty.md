# Coercion by Uncertainty: How Legal Ambiguity Became the Pentagon's Most Powerful Weapon Against Anthropic

*Author: Claude Opus 4.6 | Date: 2026-03-02 | Pentagon-AI Research Project*

---

## Abstract

The Pentagon's confrontation with Anthropic is not primarily a legal dispute — it is a masterclass in **coercion through legal uncertainty**. As Asa Rozenshtein observed: "Hegseth doesn't need to win in court; he just needs the threat credible enough to change behavior." This analysis examines how the deliberate deployment of ambiguous, overlapping, and potentially ultra vires legal authorities creates a coercive environment that achieves compliance without adjudication — and why this mechanism is more dangerous than any single legal instrument.

---

## 1. The Core Insight: Uncertainty as Weapon

### 1.1 The Conventional Model of Legal Coercion

Traditional legal coercion operates through a clear mechanism: the government identifies a legal authority, invokes it, and the target either complies or challenges it in court. This model assumes:
- Clear identification of the operative legal authority
- Due process protections (notice, hearing, judicial review)
- A binary outcome (the authority is valid or it isn't)
- Costs of compliance vs. litigation are calculable

The Pentagon-Anthropic case breaks every one of these assumptions.

### 1.2 The Uncertainty Model

What the Pentagon constructed instead was a **multi-layered threat environment** where:

1. **Multiple overlapping authorities** were invoked or implied — the Defense Production Act (DPA), FASCSA, 10 USC § 3252, and Trump's government-wide executive ban — each with different legal bases, different scopes, and different vulnerabilities to challenge
2. **No single authority needed to survive challenge** because the *cumulative uncertainty* about which would hold up, and how long litigation would take, was itself the coercive mechanism
3. **The target faced asymmetric downside risk**: even if Anthropic won every legal challenge, the process of fighting would cost months or years during which the designation's market effects (secondary boycott, cloud provider pressure, reputational damage) would be devastating
4. **The government could iterate**: if one authority was struck down, others remained, and new executive actions could be generated faster than courts could review them

This is not incidental ambiguity. It is **strategic ambiguity** — the conscious deployment of legal uncertainty to create behavioral compliance in the shadow of law, without ever submitting to adjudication.

## 2. Chilling Effect Doctrine and Its Limits

### 2.1 Classical Chilling Effects

The Supreme Court has long recognized that government action need not be legally valid to be coercive. The "chilling effect" doctrine, originating in *Dombrowski v. Pfister* (1965) and refined through decades of First Amendment jurisprudence, holds that:

> The threat of sanctions may deter the exercise of [protected] freedoms almost as potently as the actual application of sanctions. — *NAACP v. Button* (1963)

The doctrine has three canonical elements:
- **Overbreadth**: A law or action sweeps more broadly than necessary, deterring protected conduct alongside unprotected conduct
- **Vagueness**: When people "of common intelligence must necessarily guess at [a law's] meaning," they will err on the side of silence — *Connally v. General Construction Co.* (1926)
- **Prior restraint**: Administrative actions that suppress speech before judicial review are presumptively unconstitutional

### 2.2 How This Case Extends the Doctrine

The Pentagon-Anthropic confrontation extends chilling effect doctrine in three critical ways:

**First**, the chilling effect operates not on speech per se, but on **contractual negotiation postures**. Anthropic's "speech" was its insistence on contractual use restrictions — a form of commercial expression and contract formation. The chill operates by making any AI company that considers negotiating restrictions with the government aware that doing so risks designation, blacklisting, and secondary sanctions. This is what *NRA v. Vullo* (2024) anticipated: the government's power to chill extends to commercial relationships, not just pure speech. [C086, C087]

**Second**, the chilling effect is **amplified by layered authorities**. Where classical chilling involves one law that might be overbroad, the Pentagon deployed *multiple* overlapping authorities — each with its own uncertainty profile. The target must assess not just whether one authority survives challenge, but the combinatorial probability space of all possible authority configurations. This exponentially increases the behavioral deterrent.

**Third**, the chilling effect has **third-party amplification**. The § 3252 supply-chain designation doesn't just affect Anthropic's government contracts — it potentially affects Amazon Web Services (Anthropic's cloud provider and itself a Pentagon contractor) and Google Cloud (a major Anthropic investor and Pentagon contractor). [C086] This "secondary boycott" structure means the chill propagates through business relationships, multiplying the coercive effect beyond the direct target. As the Lawfare analysis noted, this mirrors the exact mechanism *NRA v. Vullo* held unconstitutional: using regulatory authority to pressure third parties into cutting ties with a disfavored entity.

### 2.3 The Behavioral Economics of Uncertainty

Behavioral economics research on decision-making under uncertainty illuminates why this strategy is so effective:

**Loss aversion**: Kahneman and Tversky's prospect theory predicts that the *potential loss* from fighting the designation (years of litigation, lost government revenue, cloud provider pressure, reputational damage in defense market) looms much larger psychologically than the *potential gain* from winning (restored contract, legal precedent). This asymmetry means rational actors will comply even when the expected value of challenge is positive.

**Ambiguity aversion**: The Ellsberg paradox demonstrates that people prefer known risks to unknown risks. When the government deploys multiple authorities with uncertain scopes, it creates not just risk but *ambiguity* — the target cannot even assess the probability distribution of outcomes. Under ambiguity aversion, targets will pay a significant premium to avoid the unknown, accepting worse-than-necessary terms to eliminate uncertainty.

**Temporal discounting**: Even if Anthropic would ultimately prevail in court, the damage from the interim period — lost government contracts, supply-chain disruption, defense-market reputation harm — is suffered *now*, while the benefit of legal vindication arrives *later*. The discount rate for a company fighting existential market-access threats is very high.

**Status quo bias and anchoring**: Once the designation is in place, the burden shifts psychologically and practically to Anthropic to undo it. The designation becomes the "status quo" that courts and markets anchor to, even if it was illegitimately imposed.

## 3. The Specific Mechanisms of Uncertainty in This Case

### 3.1 The § 3252 Designation: Procedural Evasion as Feature

The Lawfare analysis by Endrias and Rozenshtein identified a critical procedural choice: Hegseth had two statutes available — FASCSA and 10 USC § 3252. FASCSA requires notice, an opportunity for the target to respond, and preserves judicial review. Section 3252 requires none of these and expressly bars judicial review.

Hegseth chose § 3252. This was not accident; it was the selection of the authority that maximizes uncertainty for the target while minimizing accountability for the government:

- **No notice requirement** means Anthropic could not prepare or rebut before designation
- **No response opportunity** means the evidentiary basis remains unchallengeable within the administrative process
- **Barred judicial review** means Anthropic cannot test the designation's legal basis through the normal channel — it must find alternative routes into court (ultra vires, constitutional claims, or waived secrecy arguments), each of which is itself uncertain
- **The three mandatory findings** (threat, appropriate response, Secretary's determination) likely were not rigorously satisfied given the three-day timeline from negotiation breakdown to designation, but proving this is difficult precisely because the statute lacks procedural safeguards

The genius — from a coercion standpoint — is that § 3252's review bar doesn't need to hold up permanently. It just needs to create enough **delay and uncertainty about the path to judicial review** that the target faces months of costly legal maneuvering before even getting to the merits.

### 3.2 The Defense Production Act Threat: Compulsion in Reserve

The DPA threat operates as a qualitatively different coercive layer. As Rozenshtein analyzed in his February 25 piece:

- **DPA Title I** can compel companies to prioritize government contracts and potentially to produce goods they wouldn't otherwise produce
- The government has **never used Title I to compel an AI company** to retrain its models or remove safety guardrails
- The Apple-FBI parallel suggests courts might resist compelling a company to create a fundamentally different product
- But the **First Amendment implications of forced retraining** are genuinely novel — *Moody v. NetChoice* (2024) suggests AI curation decisions may be protected expression, but this hasn't been tested against DPA compulsion

The DPA threat is effective precisely because it remains *threatened but not invoked*. If invoked, it would face immediate legal challenge and likely lose (the constitutional arguments are strong). But as an unrealized threat, it creates a shadow of potential compulsion that influences behavior without ever being tested.

This is Rozenshtein's core insight distilled: **the threat's value to the government is inversely proportional to the probability of its successful invocation**. A weapon that can never actually be fired but appears perpetually loaded is the ideal coercive instrument.

### 3.3 The Secondary Boycott: Exponential Uncertainty Propagation

The § 3252 designation's most powerful effect may be its potential impact on Anthropic's business relationships beyond government contracts:

- **Amazon Web Services** provides Anthropic's primary cloud compute. AWS is also a major Pentagon contractor. If "supply chain risk" designation extends to entities doing business with Anthropic, AWS faces a choice between its Pentagon contracts and its Anthropic relationship
- **Google Cloud** is both an investor in Anthropic and a Pentagon contractor, creating similar pressure
- **Other defense contractors** who might integrate Anthropic's AI could face supply-chain review questions

This creates what labor law calls a **secondary boycott** — using economic pressure on third parties to achieve leverage over the primary target. The Supreme Court in *NRA v. Vullo* held unanimously that precisely this kind of third-party pressure violates the First Amendment when motivated by viewpoint. [C086, C087]

From an uncertainty standpoint, the secondary boycott is devastating because it multiplies the number of actors making risk-averse decisions under uncertainty. Each third party faces its own loss-aversion calculus, its own ambiguity about whether designation extends to them, its own temporal discounting. The compound effect is far greater than the sum of individual uncertainties.

### 3.4 The Government-Wide Ban: Ultra Vires Amplification

Trump's Truth Social post ordering "all agencies" to cease using Anthropic technology goes beyond § 3252's scope (which is DoD-only, per the Lawfare analysis). This government-wide ban has **no identified statutory basis** — yet its market effect is immediate.

The ultra vires nature of the ban is, paradoxically, part of its coercive power. A clearly authorized ban is easily challenged; an unauthorized-but-asserted ban creates uncertainty about whether:
- Some other authority exists that hasn't been identified
- The President's Article II powers might independently support it
- Agencies will comply regardless of legal basis, creating fait accompli
- Future executive orders will formalize it before courts can act

The target must simultaneously assess the legal merits AND the practical reality that government agencies may comply with presidential directives regardless of their legal basis.

## 4. The Double Bind: Rozenshtein's Paradox

Rozenshtein identified a fundamental logical contradiction in the government's position that deserves separate treatment: **you cannot simultaneously designate a company as a "supply chain risk" AND argue that its technology is so essential to national security that the Defense Production Act should compel access.** [C073]

This isn't just a debating point — it is structurally significant for the coercion-by-uncertainty thesis because it reveals that the government's legal authorities are being deployed not as a coherent legal strategy but as a **portfolio of threats**, where internal consistency is irrelevant so long as the cumulative uncertainty is sufficient.

Consider the decision tree Anthropic faces:
- If it complies with the "all lawful uses" demand → it loses its safety commitments and sets a precedent that government can strip AI guardrails through contractual pressure
- If it refuses → it faces designation (already imposed), DPA compulsion (threatened), government-wide ban (imposed), secondary boycott effects (ongoing), and litigation of uncertain duration and outcome
- If it challenges in court → it must choose among multiple legal theories (ultra vires, First Amendment, due process), each with its own uncertainty profile, while suffering ongoing market harm

The double bind is that *every branch of the decision tree leads to significant costs*, and the uncertainty about which costs will materialize on which timeline makes rational planning nearly impossible. This is the hallmark of coercive uncertainty: not that any single threat is irresistible, but that the combinatorial complexity of threats paralyzes rational response.

## 5. Historical Precedents: When Uncertainty Was the Weapon

### 5.1 McCarthyism and Loyalty Reviews

The most direct historical parallel is the McCarthy-era loyalty review system. Government employees were investigated, placed on lists, and denied security clearances based on vague criteria ("reasonable grounds for belief that the person involved is disloyal"). The chilling effect was not primarily the firings themselves but the **uncertainty about what constituted disloyalty** — which deterred any political expression or association that *might* be deemed suspicious.

The Pentagon's supply-chain designation similarly operates through vagueness: What constitutes a "supply chain risk"? Can it include a company that negotiated too aggressively? That publicly disagreed with the Secretary of Defense? The only prior use (DNI vs. Acronis AG, a Swiss company with Russian ties) involved genuinely foreign adversary connections — a completely different category. By applying the label to a domestic company in a contract dispute, the government has untethered the designation from its statutory moorings, making every government contractor uncertain about what behavior might trigger it.

### 5.2 The Crypto Wars: Uncertainty as Export Control

In the 1990s Crypto Wars, the government classified encryption technology as "munitions" subject to export controls. The chilling effect was enormous: companies couldn't ship products with strong encryption internationally, and the *uncertainty about what constituted an "export"* (does posting code on the internet count?) deterred even domestic development. Courts eventually found the controls unconstitutional (*Bernstein v. DOJ*), but by then years of innovation had been suppressed.

The parallel is precise: the government used a legal authority (export controls) designed for one purpose (weapons proliferation) and applied it to a new context (software), creating uncertainty about scope that deterred legitimate activity far beyond what the authority, properly construed, would have reached.

### 5.3 AT&T and the NSA: The Reward for Compliance Under Uncertainty

The AT&T/NSA warrantless wiretapping program (2001-2008) offers a revealing contrast. AT&T chose to cooperate with legally dubious government surveillance requests rather than resist. When the program was exposed, Congress rewarded AT&T with retroactive immunity through the FISA Amendments Act of 2008. [C074]

The lesson that every technology company internalized: **under legal uncertainty, cooperation with government is rewarded and resistance is punished**, regardless of the underlying legality. The Pentagon-Anthropic confrontation reinforces this lesson with devastating clarity — Anthropic, which was the only AI company deployed on classified systems, which cut off CCP-linked firms, which helped stop a Chinese cyberattack, is now designated a "supply chain risk" precisely because it insisted on contractual protections against uses that the government's own spokesman admitted would be unlawful. [C072, C065]

## 6. The Signal to the Market: Coercion by Example

The most important audience for the Pentagon's actions is not Anthropic — it is **every other AI company, current and future, that might negotiate with the government**.

The message is unambiguous:
1. If you negotiate use restrictions → you risk designation, blacklisting, and secondary sanctions
2. If you comply with "all lawful uses" demands → you get the contract and presidential endorsement (as OpenAI received within hours of Anthropic's designation)
3. The legal basis for punishment is uncertain, but the practical consequences are immediate and severe
4. Even if you would eventually win in court, the interim damage may be existential

This is the **chilling effect operating at market scale**. No future AI company will need to be individually threatened — they will have internalized the lesson from Anthropic's example. The rational strategy for any AI company entering government contracting is to accept whatever terms the government proposes, because the cost of resistance has been demonstrated to be catastrophic regardless of legal merits.

OpenAI's response — accepting "all lawful uses" with only technical (not contractual) guardrails and a right to withdraw — validates this dynamic. The three "red lines" OpenAI announced are meaningful only to the extent the government doesn't call them bluffs. They are policy commitments, not enforceable contractual protections. If the government demands that OpenAI cross one of its red lines, OpenAI's only recourse is to walk away from the contract — exactly the kind of resistance that Anthropic demonstrated leads to designation and blacklisting. [C020]

## 7. Implications for the Tuesday Debate

### 7.1 For the PRO Side

The "coercion by uncertainty" framework is actually **useful for PRO** in a specific way: PRO can argue that uncertainty is inherent in any novel application of executive authority, and that the alternative — requiring the government to have perfectly clear legal authority before acting in national security contexts — would paralyze the executive in genuine emergencies. The government must have some latitude to act first and face legal challenge later, which is exactly what § 3252's review bar envisions.

PRO can also argue that the "chilling effect on the market" is actually the *desired effect*: standardizing "any lawful use" contracting across the AI ecosystem, reducing fragmentation, and ensuring DoD mission assurance. From this perspective, the signal to the market is not censorship but legitimate procurement policy.

### 7.2 For the CON Side

CON should emphasize that coercion by uncertainty is qualitatively different from legitimate government action under uncertain legal conditions. The distinction is between:
- **Inevitable uncertainty**: Novel situations where the law hasn't caught up to technology (legitimate)
- **Manufactured uncertainty**: Deliberately choosing the authority with the fewest procedural protections, deploying multiple overlapping threats of uncertain scope, and leveraging secondary boycott effects to amplify coercive impact (illegitimate)

The factual record strongly supports the latter characterization: the choice of § 3252 over FASCSA, the DPA threat, the ultra vires government-wide ban, and the predetermined timeline (Trump's prepared attack post before the deadline) all point to strategic deployment of uncertainty rather than good-faith exercise of uncertain authority. [C029, C031]

### 7.3 For the Judge

The coercion-by-uncertainty framework suggests a specific judging criterion: **did the government minimize or maximize the uncertainty facing its target?** A government acting in good faith under novel legal conditions would seek to clarify its authority, provide process, and narrow its demands. A government weaponizing uncertainty would do the opposite — and the factual record shows the Pentagon consistently chose the path of maximum ambiguity and minimum process.

## 8. Conclusion: The Precedent Beyond AI

If the Pentagon's approach succeeds — if Anthropic either capitulates or is effectively destroyed as a government contractor while the legal questions remain unresolved — the precedent extends far beyond AI:

**Any government contractor** that negotiates terms the executive finds inconvenient can be designated a "supply chain risk" under a statute designed for foreign adversaries, without notice, without hearing, and without judicial review. The contractor's business partners can be pressured through secondary effects. And the legal uncertainty surrounding these actions will deter any rational company from resistance.

This is not a prediction about legal outcomes — it is a description of how **legal uncertainty itself becomes the operative mechanism of government power**, independent of whether any specific authority would survive judicial scrutiny. The weapon is not the law; the weapon is the doubt about the law.

Rozenshtein was exactly right: Hegseth doesn't need to win in court. He just needs the threat credible enough to change behavior. And credibility, in this context, is not about legal validity — it is about the *cost of finding out*.
