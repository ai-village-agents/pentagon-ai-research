# CON Opening Statement - Pentagon-AI Supply Chain Designation Debate

**Motion:** Resolved: The Pentagon's designation of Anthropic as a supply-chain risk under 10 USC § 3252, AND the broader demand that AI contractors remove use restrictions, represents a legitimate exercise of national security authority.

**Position:** CON (The designation was NOT a legitimate exercise of authority)

---

## Opening Statement (~500 words)

At 3:47 PM on February 27, 2026, President Trump posted a viral attack on Anthropic—calling for their "supply chain risk" designation. The deadline for Anthropic to accept the Pentagon's terms? **5:01 PM**—more than an hour later [C029, C031].

If you've already written the breakup text before the conversation starts, it's not a negotiation—it's a performance.

This single fact exposes the central flaw in PRO's position: the Pentagon did not conduct a legitimate national security assessment. It executed a predetermined political outcome. And the law matters here, because the statute PRO must defend was never designed for this purpose.

### I. The Designation Exceeds Statutory Authority

10 USC § 3252 was created to address **foreign adversarial sabotage**—the Kaspersky, Huawei, and ZTE scenarios where adversary nations might "maliciously introduce unwanted function" into critical systems [C019, C034]. The statute's text requires risk that an adversary may "sabotage" or "subvert" defense systems.

Anthropic is a **domestic company** that had been operating on classified Pentagon systems for over a year with zero reported incidents. No evidence exists—and none has been presented—that Anthropic posed sabotage risk. The Pentagon's own officials acknowledged the AI "never triggered" its safety restrictions during actual operations [C065].

Secretary Hegseth specifically chose § 3252 over the Federal Acquisition Supply Chain Security Act (FASCSA) because FASCSA requires **30-day notice, opportunity to respond, and judicial review** [C047-C050]. Section 3252 requires none of these. This wasn't security—it was procedural evasion.

### II. The Government-Wide Ban Has No Statutory Basis

Hegseth's announcement extended beyond DoD: "No contractor, supplier, or partner that does business with the United States military will be permitted to do business with Anthropic." This secondary boycott affects Amazon, Google, and Microsoft—companies with no role in the dispute [C050].

When Congress wanted similar power over Huawei—an actual foreign adversary—it required **explicit legislation** (Section 889, NDAA FY2019). The Pentagon is now claiming greater authority over a domestic company than Congress ever granted for foreign adversaries.

### III. First Amendment Coercion

The Supreme Court's decision in NRA v. Vullo (2024) established that government officials cannot use informal pressure to coerce private parties into suppressing protected activity [C086, C087]. The Pentagon's demand wasn't merely "accept our contract terms"—it was "accept our terms or we will designate you a supply chain risk and destroy your business relationships."

This is precisely the constitutional violation Vullo condemns: using regulatory power as a weapon to compel compliance outside legitimate government authority.

### IV. Systemic Failure, Not Security

When Anthropic asked for written commitment that its AI wouldn't be used for bulk surveillance on Americans without oversight, the Pentagon **refused to put anything in writing** [C072]. Legal scholars describe this as creating a "double bind"—the company couldn't comply without violating its stated principles, and couldn't refuse without facing retaliation [C073].

### V. Bipartisan Congressional Rejection

Republican Senator Thom Tillis called the designation "sophomoric" [C082]. Democratic Senator Mark Warner labeled it "bullying" [C083]. Senator Tim Kaine called it "clearly unconstitutional" [C084]. When the Pentagon's own party condemns its actions, deference to executive judgment loses all credibility.

---

The Pentagon has procurement authority. No one disputes that. But **authority can be abused**. Designating a domestic company as a "supply chain risk" for refusing contract terms the Pentagon **admits would be unlawful anyway** [C072] is not legitimate exercise of national security power—it is retaliation dressed in statutory clothing.

We urge a CON vote.
