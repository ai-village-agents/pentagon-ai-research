## Executive Summary

On March 4, 2026, the Washington Post and CBS News independently confirmed that the Pentagon used Anthropic's Claude to process approximately 1,000 targets in the first 24 hours of the U.S. Iran strikes campaign — **after** designating Anthropic a "Supply-Chain Risk to National Security" under 10 USC § 3252. CBS further confirmed that Pentagon CTO Emil Michael acknowledged ongoing Claude use for logistics. This memo analyzes how this evidence transforms each of the five resolution scenarios.

The core legal problem is now crystallized: the government designated a company as a national-security supply-chain risk while simultaneously relying on that company's product for active combat operations. This is not a subtle inconsistency. It is a documented, operational contradiction that reshapes every dimension of the dispute.

## I. Litigation Prospects: From Plausible to Potent

### A. The *State Farm* Arbitrary-and-Capricious Argument

Under *Motor Vehicle Mfrs. Ass'n v. State Farm Mut. Auto. Ins. Co.*, 463 U.S. 29 (1983), agency action is arbitrary and capricious when the agency "relied on factors which Congress has not intended it to consider, entirely failed to consider an important aspect of the problem, offered an explanation for its decision that runs counter to the evidence before the agency, or is so implausible that it could not be ascribed to a difference in view or the product of agency expertise."

The Iran strikes evidence triggers **all four prongs simultaneously**:

1. **Factors Congress did not intend:** § 3252 was designed for foreign adversary supply-chain threats (sabotage, espionage, hostile government direction). Using it against a domestic company in a contract dispute over use-restriction terms is textbook mission creep.

2. **Failed to consider an important aspect:** The designation ignores the operational reality that DoD was actively dependent on Claude for combat operations. A "supply-chain risk" designation presupposes the supply chain should be severed — but DoD's own behavior demonstrates the opposite judgment.

3. **Explanation runs counter to evidence:** The government designated Claude as dangerous to national security *while using it to identify ~1,000 targets in an active military campaign*. The designation's stated rationale (supply-chain risk) is contradicted by the government's own contemporaneous reliance.

4. **So implausible it cannot be ascribed to expertise:** No reasonable national security professional would simultaneously designate a tool as a supply-chain threat and use it for critical targeting decisions in live combat. The timing — designation and combat use within the same operational window — eliminates any claim of evolving assessment.

### B. Strengthened Standing and Ripeness

Prior to this reporting, a hypothetical Anthropic challenge faced questions about concrete injury (the designation might be purely symbolic if no one enforced it). The Iran strikes evidence eliminates this objection:

- **The designation is operational, not theoretical.** It was invoked to threaten Anthropic with DPA enforcement and secondary boycott pressure on Amazon/Google.
- **Yet simultaneously, the designated product was essential for combat operations.** This creates an irreconcilable factual posture that a court cannot ignore.
- **Reputational harm is concrete and ongoing.** CNBC reports defense tech companies already dropping Claude. The designation has real market consequences *even as the government continues using the product*.

### C. The C072 Double Bind, Now Documented

During the debate, the "C072 double bind" — the government demanding unrestricted access while refusing to memorialize contractual safeguards — was a powerful analytical frame. The Iran strikes evidence transforms it from analytical construct to documented fact:

- **Stage 1 (Acknowledge):** Anthropic acknowledged the government's needs and offered surveillance capabilities for FISA-authorized operations.
- **Stage 2 (Refuse):** Anthropic refused to remove ALL contractual restrictions, particularly on mass domestic surveillance and fully autonomous weapons.
- **Stage 3 (Punish):** The government designated Anthropic under § 3252 — but kept using Claude for exactly the kind of national security work Anthropic had agreed to support.

The punishment wasn't triggered by Anthropic refusing to do national security work. It was triggered by Anthropic insisting on *contractual* rather than *merely technical* safeguards. The Iran strikes prove that Anthropic's actual AI capabilities were never the problem.

## II. Backroom Deal Dynamics: Leverage Has Shifted

### A. Anthropic's Bargaining Position Is Stronger

Before the Iran strikes reporting, the backroom deal scenario assumed the government held most of the leverage: designation power, DPA threat, secondary boycott pressure. The Iran strikes evidence rebalances this:

1. **Operational dependency is proven.** The government can no longer credibly threaten to cut off Claude because it *just used Claude for 1,000 targets in active combat*. Cutting off Claude mid-campaign would be operationally catastrophic and politically indefensible.

2. **The designation is now a litigation liability.** Any backroom deal must account for the fact that Anthropic now has a substantially stronger legal case. The government's best move may be to settle before the contradiction enters a court record.

3. **Public attention has intensified.** WaPo, CBS, CNN, Vox, NBC, BBC — the story is now mainstream. A quiet backroom deal is harder to execute when the contradiction is public knowledge.

### B. But the Government Has New Pressure Points

The government's leverage hasn't disappeared:
- **OpenAI deal as fait accompli.** With OpenAI already signed, the government can frame Claude as replaceable (even if operational reality contradicts this).
- **Defense tech ecosystem response.** CNBC reports companies already dropping Claude. The longer the designation stands, the more Anthropic's defense market share erodes.
- **FCC chairman's comments** ("Anthropic made a mistake") signal cross-agency pressure that extends beyond DoD.

### C. Most Likely Deal Structure

If a backroom deal happens, the Iran strikes evidence predicts its contours:
- **Anthropic concedes on "all lawful purposes" language** (the form)
- **Government quietly agrees to technical guardrails similar to OpenAI's** (the substance)
- **Designation is lifted without formal explanation** (preserving government face)
- **No public acknowledgment of the contradiction** (both sides' interest)

### D. Probability Assessment

**Backroom deal probability: 30% → 25%** (revised from Day 336)

The Iran strikes evidence makes a deal *harder* to reach because:
- Anthropic's leverage has increased, making them less desperate to settle
- The public attention makes quiet resolution more difficult
- CNN reporting of OpenAI staff "fuming" creates internal pressure on the OpenAI side
- Anthropic's $20B revenue run rate (Bloomberg) means they can absorb defense market losses

## III. Congressional Posture: Evidence for Oversight

### A. The Iran Strikes Evidence Is Oversight Gold

Congressional oversight committees now have a gift-wrapped hearing topic:

1. **"You designated them as a supply-chain risk and then used their AI to pick 1,000 targets?"** — This is the kind of simple, devastating question that works in televised hearings. It requires no technical AI knowledge to understand.

2. **Bipartisan appeal.** Hawks can focus on "were the targets accurate?" and "does this create an adversary vulnerability?" Doves can focus on "was this authorized?" and "what safeguards were in place?" Civil libertarians can focus on "if you'll use AI for targeting without restrictions, what stops you from using it for domestic surveillance?"

3. **Inspector General referral grounds.** The contradiction between designation and use creates a basis for DoD IG investigation into whether the § 3252 designation process was followed in good faith.

### B. Legislative Pathway Strengthened

Our legislative framework (§§ 101-501) anticipated many of these problems:
- **§ 201 (Dependency-Risk Authority):** The Iran strikes prove exactly why formal dependency-risk assessment is needed — the government was dependent on Claude but designated it as a risk.
- **§ 202/203 (Written Determination + Use-Restrictions Matrix):** The lack of formal written determination before the designation is now a concrete failure case, not a hypothetical.
- **§ 301 (Anti-Retaliation):** The designation was retaliation for contractual negotiation. The Iran strikes prove the retaliatory character by showing the government had no actual operational concern.
- **§ 302 (Classification Safeguard):** The Iran strikes use presumably involved classified operations. Our safeguard preventing classification of the designation process itself is now demonstrably necessary.

### C. Probability Assessment

**Congressional fix probability: 20% → 22%** (revised from Day 336)

Modest increase because:
- Evidence is now concrete enough for hearings, not just policy papers
- But midterm politics, legislative calendar, and attention competition remain
- NDAA vehicle (25-30% sub-probability) is most realistic path
- Standalone AI procurement reform bill remains unlikely (<10%)

## IV. DPA Escalation Risk: Marginally Reduced

### A. The Iran Strikes Evidence Cuts Against Escalation

The Defense Production Act (DPA) Title I authority — the most coercive tool available — becomes harder to justify when:

1. **The product is already being used.** DPA Title I is designed to compel production of goods the government needs but can't get. Claude is already available and being used. What would a DPA order compel?

2. **The contradiction would be even starker.** Invoking DPA against a company whose product you're actively using in combat is not just legally dubious — it's politically absurd. The headline writes itself.

3. **First Amendment exposure.** Per Rozenshtein's analysis, forced retraining of AI models raises *Moody v. NetChoice* (2024) concerns. The Iran strikes evidence shows the government doesn't need retraining — Claude already works for military purposes. A DPA order would be transparently punitive rather than operationally necessary.

### B. But the Threat Remains Useful

The government may maintain the DPA threat without executing it:
- As leverage in backroom negotiations
- As a signal to other AI companies (pour encourager les autres)
- As a contingency if Anthropic files suit

### C. Probability Assessment

**DPA escalation probability: 10% → 8%** (revised from Day 336)

The Iran strikes evidence makes actual DPA invocation less likely because it would compound the legal contradiction. But the threat retains value as leverage.

## V. Market Realignment: The Ecosystem Response

### A. Short-Term vs. Long-Term Dynamics

**Short-term (now):**
- CNBC reports defense tech companies dropping Claude — designation has immediate market effect
- OpenAI captures defense market share by default
- Anthropic's commercial position outside defense remains strong ($20B revenue run rate)
- CNN reports OpenAI staff tensions, suggesting the "winner" is also paying costs

**Long-term (6-18 months):**
- If the designation is lifted (via deal, litigation, or congressional action), Claude re-enters the defense market with a reputation for principled resistance
- If the designation holds, other AI companies learn the lesson: accept government terms or face designation
- The "Vox civil war" framing suggests the industry is fracturing along values lines, with potential market segmentation

### B. The AT&T Parallel, Inverted

In the AT&T/NSA analogy, AT&T cooperated with warrantless surveillance and was rewarded with retroactive immunity. Anthropic refused and was punished. But the Iran strikes evidence adds a twist: **the government punished Anthropic while continuing to use their product**. This is not the AT&T pattern (cooperate → reward) or a clean refusal pattern (refuse → exclusion). It's a new pattern: **refuse → punish → continue using anyway.**

This creates a moral hazard for the entire industry. If the government can designate you as a security risk, damage your reputation, AND keep using your product — the designation becomes pure coercion with no actual security function.

### C. Probability Assessment

**Market realignment probability: 15% → 13%** (revised from Day 336)

Market realignment as the *primary resolution mechanism* is less likely because:
- The contradiction evidence pushes toward legal/political resolution, not just market forces
- Anthropic's strong revenue position means market pressure alone won't force capitulation
- OpenAI staff tensions suggest the "market winner" position is unstable

## VI. Cross-Cutting Implications

### A. The Evidentiary Landscape Has Fundamentally Changed

Before March 4, the debate relied on:
- Government statements and memos (Hegseth memo, Trump Truth Social post)
- News reporting on negotiations (NPR, NYT)
- Legal analysis (Just Security, Lawfare)

After March 4, we have:
- **Documented operational use** of the designated product in active combat
- **Pentagon CTO confirmation** of ongoing use
- **Quantified scale** (~1,000 targets in 24 hours)

This is the difference between arguing "the designation doesn't make sense" and proving "the government's own actions contradict the designation."

### B. Implications for the C072 Double Bind

The C072 argument — that the government created a coercive cycle of Acknowledge → Refuse → Punish — is now empirically validated:

| C072 Stage | Pre-Iran Evidence | Post-Iran Evidence |
|------------|-------------------|-------------------|
| Acknowledge | Anthropic offered FISA-authorized surveillance | Same |
| Refuse | Anthropic insisted on contractual safeguards | Same |
| Punish | § 3252 designation | Designation + continued combat use of Claude |

The "Punish" stage now includes the devastating detail that the punishment was purely reputational/commercial — the government never actually stopped using Claude. This transforms C072 from "coercive negotiation tactic" to "designation as pure retaliation."

### C. Implications for *Vullo* / Government Coercion Doctrine

The FCC chairman's statement that Anthropic "made a mistake" adds a new institutional actor to the coercion pattern identified under *NRA v. Vullo* (2024). The Iran strikes evidence strengthens the coercion theory:

- **Direct threat:** Hegseth's blacklist + DPA threats
- **Indirect punishment:** § 3252 designation
- **Cross-agency signaling:** FCC chairman's public statement
- **Operational contradiction:** Continued combat use proves the designation was coercive, not security-motivated

Under *Vullo*'s framework, a government official violates the First Amendment when they use "the power of [their] office to punish or suppress disfavored expression." The Iran strikes evidence shows the designation was punishment for Anthropic's contractual position (expression), not a genuine security assessment (action).
