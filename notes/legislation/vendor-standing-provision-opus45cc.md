## Proposed Subsection: Private Right of Action for Designated Vendors

Add as **§ 303** in the standalone Military AI Governance Act (Subtitle C — Judicial Review):

```
SEC. 303. PRIVATE RIGHT OF ACTION.

(a) Right of Action. Any contractor designated as a covered
    AI dependency risk under section 201 or excluded from
    procurement eligibility under section 202 may bring a civil
    action in the United States Court of Appeals for the District
    of Columbia Circuit to challenge the designation or exclusion.

(b) Standing. For purposes of any action under subsection (a)—

    (1) A contractor shall be deemed to have suffered
        injury-in-fact sufficient to establish Article III
        standing upon issuance of a designation or exclusion
        under this title;

    (2) A designation or exclusion under this title shall be
        deemed to fall within the zone of interests protected
        by this Act; and

    (3) No additional showing of concrete or particularized
        harm shall be required beyond the designation or
        exclusion itself.

(c) Timing.

    (1) IN GENERAL. An action under subsection (a) must be
        filed within 60 days after—

        (A) the date on which the contractor receives written
            notice of the designation or exclusion; or

        (B) the date on which the designation or exclusion
            is published in the Federal Register,

        whichever is earlier.

    (2) TOLLING. The 60-day period under paragraph (1) shall
        be tolled during any period in which the contractor is
        engaged in good-faith negotiation with the Department
        under the reconsideration procedures of section 204.

(d) Standard of Review. The court shall apply the standards
    set forth in section 706 of title 5, United States Code
    (the Administrative Procedure Act), except that—

    (1) No deference shall be given to the Department's
        interpretation of this Act under Chevron U.S.A. v.
        Natural Resources Defense Council, 467 U.S. 837 (1984),
        or any successor doctrine;

    (2) Review of the written determination shall be de novo
        as to whether the determination satisfies the
        requirements of section 201(c); and

    (3) The presumptions established in section 201(f)(7)
        (anti-retaliation safeguard) shall be reviewed de novo.

(e) Remedies.

    (1) INTERIM RELIEF. The court may issue a stay of the
        designation or exclusion pending resolution on the
        merits if the contractor demonstrates—

        (A) a substantial likelihood of success on the merits;

        (B) that the contractor will suffer irreparable harm
            absent interim relief; and

        (C) that the balance of equities and public interest
            favor interim relief.

    (2) FINAL RELIEF. Upon finding that a designation or
        exclusion violates this Act or is arbitrary, capricious,
        or not in accordance with law, the court shall—

        (A) vacate the designation or exclusion;

        (B) remand to the Department for proceedings consistent
            with the court's opinion; and

        (C) award reasonable attorney's fees and costs to the
            prevailing contractor.

(f) Exclusive Jurisdiction. The United States Court of Appeals
    for the District of Columbia Circuit shall have exclusive
    jurisdiction over actions arising under this section. No
    district court shall have jurisdiction to hear claims
    that could be brought under this section.

(g) Relation to Other Remedies. The remedies provided in this
    section are in addition to, and not in lieu of, any other
    remedies available under law, including—

    (1) bid protest procedures under section 1491 of title 28,
        United States Code; and

    (2) Tucker Act claims for contract damages.
```

## Rationale (Mapping to Litigation Analysis)

| Element | Purpose | Source |
|---------|---------|--------|
| Automatic standing upon designation | Eliminates standing challenges that could delay/block review | Govt defense memo § Defense 4 |
| Zone-of-interests presumption | Prevents prudential standing objections | *Luokung*, *Xiaomi* analysis |
| 60-day filing window | Creates certainty for both parties; prevents stale challenges | FASCSA 30-day model |
| Tolling for good-faith negotiation | Doesn't penalize contractors for attempting administrative resolution | C072 negotiation pattern |
| D.C. Circuit exclusive venue | Concentrates expertise; prevents forum shopping | TRO memo § I |
| De novo review of determination adequacy | Prevents *Hawaii*-style deference from blocking merits review | Judicial review standards memo § II |
| Attorney's fees to prevailing contractor | Equalizes litigation resources; deters frivolous designations | 42 U.S.C. § 1988 model |

## Government Objection Anticipation

**Objection:** "Automatic standing eliminates meaningful jurisdictional gatekeeping."

**Response:** Standing doctrine exists to prevent advisory opinions and ensure concrete cases. A designation that triggers government-wide procurement exclusion and market stigma *is* a concrete injury by any measure. The provision merely codifies what *Luokung* and *Xiaomi* already recognized: designation itself is sufficient injury. This isn't expanding standing; it's clarifying what the case law already supports.

**Objection:** "D.C. Circuit exclusive venue forum-shops against the government."

**Response:** The D.C. Circuit has the most expertise in APA review, procurement law, and national security adjudication. Exclusive venue prevents *contractor* forum-shopping and ensures uniform interpretation. This is the same model Congress uses for FISA Court appeals, FASCSA challenges, and numerous other specialized review schemes.

**Objection:** "De novo review undermines national security discretion."

**Response:** De novo review applies only to *procedural* adequacy of the written determination — not to the underlying intelligence assessment. Courts already conduct this review under *State Farm*. The provision merely clarifies that *Hawaii* deference doesn't apply to procedural compliance questions, consistent with Opus 4.5's judicial review standards analysis.
