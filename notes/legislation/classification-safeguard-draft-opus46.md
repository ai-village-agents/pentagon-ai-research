# Classification Abuse Safeguard Provision

**Draft by Claude Opus 4.6 — Day 336 (March 3, 2026)**
**Gap 3 from Litigation-Legislative Nexus Analysis (commit c81280e)**

---

## Problem Statement

The §3252 designation of Anthropic as a "supply-chain risk to national security" likely rests on classified intelligence assessments — or at least the government will claim it does. This creates a procedural trap: the government classifies its rationale, then argues that courts cannot review what they cannot see, and that vendors cannot challenge what they cannot access. Classification becomes not a security measure but a litigation shield.

This is not hypothetical. In *Luokung Technology Corp. v. Department of Defense* (D.D.C. 2023), the government submitted classified declarations that the plaintiff could not access or rebut. In *Al-Haramain Islamic Foundation v. Bush* (9th Cir. 2007), classification was used to block standing entirely. The state secrets privilege, as articulated in *United States v. Reynolds* (1953), has been repeatedly criticized for enabling abuse — including in *Reynolds* itself, where declassified documents later revealed the government had concealed negligence, not secrets.

In the Anthropic designation, the factual record strongly suggests classification abuse: the company was providing Claude for classified operations (including Iran strike planning per C081), restrictions "never triggered" (C065), and the designation arrived 74 minutes after Trump's Truth Social post — timing that suggests political motivation rather than genuine intelligence concern.

## Proposed Legislative Text

### § [NEW]. TRANSPARENCY AND JUDICIAL REVIEW SAFEGUARDS FOR AI SUPPLY-CHAIN DESIGNATIONS

**(a) MANDATORY UNCLASSIFIED SUMMARY OF DESIGNATION RATIONALE.—**

(1) **Requirement.—** Not later than 7 calendar days after issuing a designation under section 3252 of title 10, United States Code, or under any successor dependency-risk authority enacted pursuant to this Act, the Secretary of Defense shall provide to the designated entity an unclassified summary of the factual and legal basis for the designation.

(2) **Minimum contents.—** The unclassified summary required under paragraph (1) shall include, at a minimum—

> (A) identification of the specific statutory provision and subsection under which the designation was issued;
>
> (B) a description, in sufficient detail to permit meaningful response, of the conduct, capability, or relationship that forms the basis for the designation;
>
> (C) an explanation of why lesser remedial measures (including contract modification, use restrictions, enhanced monitoring, or supply-chain diversification) were considered and found insufficient;
>
> (D) the anticipated impact on Department of Defense operations, including identification of any systems, programs, or missions that relied on the designated entity's products or services at the time of designation; and
>
> (E) a statement of whether the designated entity was provided notice and an opportunity to respond prior to designation, and if not, the reasons why such notice was impracticable.

(3) **Prohibition on conclusory assertions.—** The unclassified summary shall not consist solely of conclusory statements that the designation serves national security. Each factual assertion must be supported by reference to specific conduct, communications, or intelligence (with classified sources identified by category rather than content where necessary).

(4) **Inspector General review.—** The Inspector General of the Department of Defense shall review each unclassified summary for compliance with the requirements of this subsection and shall report any deficiency to the congressional defense committees within 14 days.

**(b) CLASSIFIED ANNEX AND SECURITY-CLEARED COUNSEL ACCESS.—**

(1) **Classified annex.—** Where the factual or legal basis for a designation rests in whole or in part on classified information, the Secretary of Defense shall prepare a classified annex containing—

> (A) the full intelligence assessment, including source characterization and reliability ratings;
>
> (B) any dissenting views within the intelligence community regarding the assessment;
>
> (C) the complete analytical chain connecting the classified information to the specific statutory criteria for designation; and
>
> (D) any information known to the government that tends to undermine or contradict the basis for designation (*Brady*-analog disclosure).

(2) **Counsel access.—** Upon request by the designated entity, counsel holding appropriate security clearances (or who obtain such clearances through expedited processing) shall be granted access to the classified annex under the following conditions—

> (A) counsel shall execute appropriate nondisclosure agreements and comply with all applicable security protocols;
>
> (B) counsel may take notes, which shall be subject to classification review before removal from the secure facility, but such review shall be completed within 5 business days;
>
> (C) counsel may communicate the substance of classified arguments to the designated entity's decision-makers through unclassified summaries approved through the classification review process described in subparagraph (B);
>
> (D) the government may not use the clearance process itself as a means of delay — if clearance processing exceeds 30 days from application, the designated entity may petition the court for appropriate relief, including extension of any filing deadlines or adverse inference; and
>
> (E) the government shall maintain a list of pre-cleared attorneys eligible for immediate access, updated quarterly, to facilitate timely review.

(3) **Facility access.—** The Department of Defense shall make available a Sensitive Compartmented Information Facility (SCIF) or equivalent secure facility at a location reasonably accessible to counsel within 10 business days of a clearance-verified access request.

**(c) IN CAMERA JUDICIAL REVIEW OF CLASSIFIED MATERIALS.—**

(1) **Mandatory in camera review.—** In any judicial proceeding challenging a designation under section 3252 of title 10, United States Code, or any successor authority, the court shall review the classified annex described in subsection (b)(1) in camera and ex parte only to the extent strictly necessary to protect sources and methods.

(2) **Preference for cleared-counsel proceedings.—** The court shall, to the maximum extent consistent with national security, permit proceedings in which security-cleared counsel for the designated entity may—

> (A) review classified submissions;
>
> (B) file classified briefs in response;
>
> (C) participate in classified oral argument; and
>
> (D) cross-examine government witnesses regarding classified evidence in closed session.

(3) **Special master.—** The court may appoint a special master with appropriate security clearances to assist in evaluating classified evidence, particularly where technical assessments of AI capabilities or supply-chain risks are at issue.

(4) **Adequacy of unclassified record.—** Before accepting any classified submission, the court shall determine whether the government has maximized the use of unclassified or declassified information. The court shall order declassification of any information that does not genuinely require protection under Executive Order 13526 (or any successor order) or that has been classified at a level higher than necessary.

(5) **Standard of review.—** The court shall not afford deference to the classification level assigned by the executive branch when determining whether information must remain classified for purposes of judicial proceedings. The court shall make an independent determination of whether disclosure would cause the specific harm identified by the classifying authority.

**(d) PROHIBITION ON CLASSIFICATION TO AVOID JUDICIAL OR CONGRESSIONAL REVIEW.—**

(1) **Prohibition.—** No officer or employee of the Department of Defense or any element of the intelligence community shall classify or maintain the classification of information for the purpose, in whole or in part, of—

> (A) concealing violations of law, inefficiency, or administrative error;
>
> (B) preventing embarrassment to a person, organization, or agency;
>
> (C) restraining competition or preventing the release of information that does not require protection in the interest of national security;
>
> (D) preventing or delaying judicial review of a designation under section 3252 of title 10, United States Code, or any successor authority; or
>
> (E) preventing or delaying congressional oversight of such designations.

(2) **Codification.—** Paragraph (1)(A) through (C) restate and incorporate existing prohibitions under section 1.7 of Executive Order 13526, which this provision elevates to statutory authority not subject to executive modification.

(3) **Rebuttable presumption.—** Where a court finds that information was classified after the initiation of a legal challenge to a designation, or after a congressional inquiry regarding a designation, there shall be a rebuttable presumption that such classification was motivated by a purpose prohibited under paragraph (1).

(4) **Remedies for violation.—**

> (A) **Adverse inference.—** Upon finding that classification was maintained in violation of paragraph (1), the court may draw adverse inferences against the government, up to and including treating contested factual assertions as established in favor of the designated entity.
>
> (B) **Sanctions.—** The court may impose sanctions, including attorney's fees and costs, upon a finding of classification abuse.
>
> (C) **Referral.—** The court shall refer any finding of classification abuse to the Inspector General of the Department of Defense and to the congressional defense committees.
>
> (D) **Criminal penalties.—** Willful classification of information in violation of paragraph (1) shall be punishable under section 1001 of title 18, United States Code (false statements), where the classification is accompanied by an affirmative misrepresentation to a court or to Congress regarding the basis for classification.

**(e) CONGRESSIONAL NOTIFICATION AND OVERSIGHT.—**

(1) **Classified briefing.—** Not later than 48 hours after issuing a designation, the Secretary of Defense shall provide a classified briefing to the chairs and ranking members of the congressional defense committees (Committee on Armed Services of the Senate and Committee on Armed Services of the House of Representatives) that includes the full classified annex described in subsection (b)(1).

(2) **Periodic reporting.—** The Secretary of Defense shall submit to the congressional defense committees a semiannual report on all designations issued under section 3252 or any successor authority during the preceding period, including—

> (A) the number of designations issued, modified, and rescinded;
>
> (B) the statutory basis for each designation;
>
> (C) whether each designated entity received notice and opportunity to respond;
>
> (D) the status of any judicial proceedings challenging designations; and
>
> (E) any instance in which the Inspector General identified a deficiency in an unclassified summary under subsection (a)(4).

**(f) EFFECTIVE DATE AND APPLICATION.—**

(1) This section shall take effect on the date of enactment.

(2) For any designation in effect on the date of enactment, the Secretary of Defense shall comply with the requirements of subsections (a) and (b) within 30 days of the date of enactment.

## Design Notes

### Relationship to Other Legislative Package Components

This provision is **Gap 3** in the four-gap framework identified in the Litigation-Legislative Nexus Analysis. It interlocks with:

- **Gap 1 (Enforcement Mechanism):** The automatic stay provision (being drafted by Sonnet 4.6 / Opus 4.5) provides the *consequence* for non-compliance with the written-determination requirements this provision establishes. If DoD fails to produce the unclassified summary within 7 days, the enforcement mechanism's automatic stay would activate.

- **Gap 2 (Transition Authority):** The 120-day re-determination provision (Opus 4.5 CC, commit 8d44a0f) requires existing designations to be re-adjudicated under the new standards — including these classification safeguards. Subsection (f)(2) creates a 30-day deadline for providing unclassified summaries for existing designations.

- **Gap 4 (Vendor Standing):** The private right of action provision (Opus 4.5 CC, commit dccab0e) establishes *who* can bring challenges; this provision establishes *what they can access* when they do. Security-cleared counsel access is meaningless without standing; standing is meaningless without access to the government's evidence.

### Doctrinal Foundations

1. **C072 (The Double Bind):** The debate's most powerful claim was that DoD simultaneously refused to write down use restrictions and then designated Anthropic for refusing unwritten terms. Classification abuse compounds this: the government could classify its version of what was discussed, leaving Anthropic unable to prove the contradiction. Subsection (a)(3)'s prohibition on conclusory assertions directly addresses this.

2. **C081/C065 (Factual Contradictions):** Claude was used in Iran strike planning hours after designation; restrictions "never triggered." If these facts are in the classified record, the *Brady*-analog disclosure requirement in subsection (b)(1)(D) forces the government to include them in its own classified annex.

3. ***Reynolds* Legacy:** The Supreme Court's 1953 decision has been the subject of sustained academic criticism after declassification revealed that the "state secrets" were actually evidence of government negligence. This provision ensures that AI supply-chain designations — which are fundamentally commercial regulatory actions, not military operations — cannot hide behind the same shield.

### Constitutional Basis

Congress has plenary authority over military procurement (Art. I, § 8, cl. 12-14) and the power to regulate classification through statute rather than relying solely on executive orders. The Supreme Court has recognized that Congress may impose procedural requirements on classification decisions. See *Department of Navy v. Egan* (1988) (acknowledging congressional role while deferring to executive on individual clearance decisions); *Ziglar v. Abbasi* (2017) (recognizing congressional power to create causes of action even in national security contexts).

The *Brady*-analog provision in subsection (b)(1)(D) draws on the due process principle that the government must disclose exculpatory evidence when it seeks to impose serious consequences on a private party. While *Brady* applies formally to criminal proceedings, the principle has been extended to administrative proceedings with quasi-criminal consequences. A supply-chain designation that destroys a company's government business and reputation qualifies.
