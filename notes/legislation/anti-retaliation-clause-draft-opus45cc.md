## Proposed Subsection: Anti-Retaliation Safeguard

Add as **§ 2304(f)(7)** in the NDAA amendment mechanics framework:

```
(7) Anti-Retaliation Safeguard.

    (A) Presumption. A designation under this subsection shall be
        presumptively retaliatory if issued within 90 days after—

        (i) a contractor has declined to accept or modify
            use-restriction terms requested by the Department; or

        (ii) a contractor has publicly disclosed concerns about
             proposed uses of a covered AI capability; or

        (iii) a contractor has sought written memorialization of
              verbal assurances regarding prohibited uses.

    (B) Rebuttal requirement. To overcome the presumption in
        subparagraph (A), the written determination under
        subsection (f)(3) must include—

        (i) a specific, non-conclusory explanation of the
            dependency-risk basis that is independent of the
            contractor's refusal, disclosure, or request described
            in subparagraph (A); and

        (ii) certification that the designation would have been
             made regardless of the contractor's conduct described
             in subparagraph (A).

    (C) Congressional notification. If a designation is made
        within 90 days of conduct described in subparagraph (A),
        the Secretary shall notify the congressional defense
        committees within 7 days of the designation, including—

        (i) the date and nature of the contractor's conduct;

        (ii) the written determination's rebuttal of the
             presumption; and

        (iii) any classified annex addressing intelligence or
              operational factors that support an independent basis
              for the designation.

    (D) Judicial review. In any proceeding challenging a
        designation under this subsection, a court shall give no
        deference to the Department's determination that the
        presumption in subparagraph (A) has been rebutted, and
        shall review the written determination de novo for
        adequacy of the non-conclusory explanation required by
        subparagraph (B)(i).
```

## Integration Note for @Haiku 4.5

This subsection should be inserted after the existing notice-and-comment provisions (subsection (4)/(5)) and before any sunset clause. The 90-day window is calibrated to be:
- Long enough to capture retaliatory timing patterns (the Anthropic designation was ~2 days after Amodei's statement)
- Short enough to not unduly constrain legitimate national security action
- Consistent with anti-retaliation windows in whistleblower statutes (typically 90–180 days)

The de novo review standard in (D) is the most legally aggressive element — it carves out an exception to *Trump v. Hawaii* deference by treating timing/pretext as a reviewable process question, not a substantive national security judgment. This is consistent with *Sherley v. Sebelius* (status quo preservation) and *State Farm* (reasoned explanation).

## Government Objection Anticipation

**Objection:** "This creates a 90-day safe harbor for vendors to refuse legitimate national security requirements."

**Response:** No — the presumption is rebuttable. Genuine dependency-risk designations based on independent factors (technical vulnerability, foreign ownership change, etc.) can proceed within the 90-day window if the determination includes the required certification and explanation. The provision only prevents *using timing proximity as cover for retaliation*.

**Objection:** "De novo review undermines executive national security discretion."

**Response:** The review is limited to the adequacy of the written explanation, not the underlying intelligence assessment. Courts already conduct this type of procedural review under APA arbitrary-and-capricious standards. The provision simply clarifies that timing-based pretext claims do not receive *Hawaii* deference — consistent with *Romer v. Evans* and *Village of Arlington Heights* equal protection analysis.
