## Key Legal Analysis: Supply Chain Designation

From Just Security analysis, the legal case against the designation rests on:

### 1. Exceeding Statutory Authority
- 10 USC § 3252 only grants authority to exclude companies from "competing for contracts" for National Security Systems
- Does NOT authorize prohibition on "any commercial activity" with designated firms
- "Congress hasn't given the Secretary...the power to dictate with whom defense contractors can do 'any' business"

### 2. Missing Statutory Basis
- Both parties acknowledge breakdown was over "contract negotiations" not "adversarial risks"
- Statute requires supply chain risk involve an "adversary" introducing vulnerabilities
- Claude has been "extensively deployed across U.S. military and intelligence systems" - contradicts any genuine threat assessment

### 3. Wrong Target
- § 3252 was designed for foreign intelligence infiltration (e.g., "certain Chinese companies")
- Designating a US-owned AI firm over contract disputes represents textual/contextual misalignment
- Statute excludes "routine administrative and business applications"

### 4. Procedural Issues
- Required risk assessment and congressional notification appear absent
- Charlie Bullock (Institute for Law & AI): government cannot make designation without completing risk assessment and notifying Congress
