## PRO Opening

The motion asks whether the Pentagon's supply-chain risk designation of Anthropic represents a legitimate exercise of national security authority. We define "legitimate" as lawful, procedurally within executive authority, and normatively justified — even if imperfectly executed. PRO affirms.

We recognize uncomfortable facts. The rhetoric was harsh. The timeline was compressed. Some of us sympathize with Anthropic's safety mission. But the question is not whether we *like* this outcome — it is whether the government acted within its lawful authority.

**I. Civilian control means the military — not vendors — decides how lawful missions are conducted.**

Who decides how AI is used in military operations — elected officials accountable to voters, or private companies accountable to shareholders? A Pentagon official stated the principle plainly: "legality is the Pentagon's responsibility as the end user, not the AI vendor" and "you can't lead tactical operations by exception" [C067].

Anthropic's contractual restrictions gave a single vendor effective veto power over certain military applications. The "any lawful use" standard [C002] does not demand lawlessness — law, regulation, and the UCMJ already prohibit illegal uses. It demands that *law* remain the binding constraint, not vendor contracts that hard-code a private party's legal taxonomy into mission tooling.

**II. Supply chain risk targets dependency architecture, not technology quality.**

CON will argue restrictions were "never triggered" [C065] and that the government used Claude in operations [C081, C085]. We do not dispute this. But "supply chain risk" means *critical dependency subject to disruption*, not "bad product."

Anthropic was the only frontier AI company on classified systems [C026]. Its restrictions shaped military planning upstream — planners route around known constraints. The absence of a triggered restriction tells us nothing about self-censorship or foregone capabilities. Military planning operates on worst-case, not average-case; peacetime pilots cannot predict wartime demands.

Consider the Iran strikes [C081]: the Pentagon used Claude hours after designation. Why? Because they were already dependent and couldn't easily stop. That is not evidence restrictions posed no risk — it is evidence the dependency was exactly as deep as planners feared.

**III. Procurement discretion belongs to the executive.**

CON cites Congressional letters [C082-C084] and Senator Tillis calling the process "sophomoric" [C039]. But letters are oversight signals, not law. Congress did not withhold appropriations or restrict § 3252 authority. Under *Rust v. Sullivan* (1991), the government can attach conditions to its spending that would be unconstitutional if imposed as regulation. No company has a constitutional right to a Pentagon contract on its preferred terms.

**IV. We acknowledge imperfections — but imperfect execution does not void authority.**

Trump's pre-deadline post [C029, C031] and Michael's personal animus [C030] damage the optics. We concede this. But under *Trump v. Hawaii* (2018), courts evaluate formal actions' rational basis, not accompanying rhetoric. The formal designation [C032] cites supply-chain security grounds — that is what courts review.

CON must show not merely that this was *unwise*, but that it was *illegitimate*. That is a higher bar. We do not believe they can clear it.

## 4. Decisive Grounds

### 4.1 Statutory Fit & Institutional Tool‑Choice

The most important axis for me is **statutory fit**: what § 3252 was written to do, and whether using it here stays within that lane.

- The text and legislative history summarized in our claims set (C019, C034) ground § 3252 in threats that a foreign adversary or compromised vendor will “sabotage,” “maliciously introduce unwanted function,” or “otherwise subvert” critical systems.  
- CON showed, using C065, C081, and C085, that there is no record of Anthropic sabotaging anything, that the contractual guardrails “never affected a single mission,” and that the government continued to use Anthropic tools in sensitive operations immediately before and after the designation.  
- At the same time, C049 and C050 document a choice **not** to rely on FASCSA – which carries notice, response, and judicial review – and instead to invoke § 3252 in a way that, especially in light of the public “no contractor, supplier, or partner” language (C050), functions as a de facto government‑wide boycott.

Against that, PRO’s best statutory points are that Congress deliberately created § 3252 as a flexible DoD procurement tool (C032), and that not every categorical exclusion requires bespoke legislation. That is true in the abstract. But CON’s use of C088–C091 – the explicit § 889 template Congress adopted for Huawei and ZTE – is powerful: when Congress wanted a broad supplier ban with spillover to contractors and partners, it wrote a statute that specified scope, timelines, waiver standards, and reporting.

Given C019/C034, C049–C050, and C088–C091 together, I am not persuaded that § 3252 can reasonably be read as silently authorizing an Anthropic‑style designation that is triggered by a domestic contract dispute over guardrails, carries public language aimed at third‑party cloud providers, and overlaps heavily with the kind of government‑wide ban Congress has elsewhere treated as a “major question” requiring explicit authorization. On this axis, **CON clearly leads**.

### 4.2 C072 and the “Double Bind”

C072 records that Pentagon officials verbally agreed some of Anthropic’s requested prohibitions would be unlawful, yet refused to memorialize those limits in the contract. The central question is: what, if anything, should we infer from that combination?

- PRO’s vendor‑veto explanation – that written contractual language would create privately enforceable rights and invite litigation into classified operational decisions – is a serious concern. It is reasonable for DoD to be wary of boilerplate that could be read to give any one contractor leverage in court over mission design.  
- However, as CON emphasizes, C072 is not about Anthropic asking DoD to create new law; it is about asking DoD to **write down limits it already concedes are binding**. In that posture, the “vendor‑veto” worry is weaker: if a future operation truly would violate those acknowledged legal limits, one would hope courts *would* have something to say about it.

CON’s Closing crystallizes this with the line that, if the commitment is genuinely redundant with existing law, then “it costs nothing” to write it down. That framing does not receive a direct answer in PRO’s Closing. On this record, I come away with a meaningful, though not absolute, inference: DoD’s refusal to memorialize its own verbal concessions suggests a desire to preserve some operational gray zone the parties publicly agree is at least near the line. That inference weighs heavily **against** treating the designation as a clean exercise of legitimate authority.

### 4.3 Operational Dependency vs. Sabotage Predicate

On the **operational** axis, I credit PRO with an important win. Using C026 (single‑vendor lock‑in) together with C081/C085, PRO persuasively argues that the Pentagon’s continued use of Anthropic tools around the time of the designation is strong evidence of **dependency** and concentration risk. “We had to keep using the tool because we had no immediate alternative” is fully consistent with identifying a supply‑chain vulnerability and planning a phased transition rather than an overnight cut‑off.

Where this stops short of saving the designation is at the **level of the statutory predicate**. Section 3252, as reflected in C019/C034, is not a generic “dependency‑risk” statute; it is keyed to sabotage/subversion scenarios. PRO never quite bridges the gap between “we are uncomfortably dependent on this vendor for critical capabilities” – a point I accept – and “this vendor plausibly fits the sabotage/subversion risk profile that Congress had in mind when it enacted § 3252.”

So on this axis I effectively split the difference: PRO earns substantial credit for clarifying the operational reality, but CON still prevails on whether § 3252 is the right statutory tool for that problem.

### 4.4 Coercion / First Amendment (Supporting, Not Decisive)

Both sides devote real energy to Vullo, Bantam, and Claiborne. I view this axis as **supporting context** rather than the core of the decision.

- CON, anchored in C050 and C086–C087, makes a strong case that the combination of a formal designation plus public threats toward cloud providers looks and feels like regulatory coercion of third parties, not merely “government as customer.”  
- PRO rightly presses what, exactly, the protected activity is: declining contract terms is not obviously the kind of expressive association that Vullo/Bantam have historically protected, and there is no case squarely holding that refusal to buy on a vendor’s preferred terms is itself First‑Amendment coercion.

Given the state of the case law and our limited factual record, I do not treat the First‑Amendment argument as independently dispositive. It does, however, reinforce my statutory and C072 concerns by illustrating how easily procurement and designation tools can slide into de facto retaliation and secondary boycotts when deployed in the shadow of public threats.

- **Statutory authority & institutional fit (C019, C034, C049–C050, C088–C091):** Clear edge to **CON**. The way § 3252 is used here sits uncomfortably with its sabotage‑focused text and with Congress’s separate § 889 framework for Huawei/ZTE‑style bans.
- **C072 double bind:** **CON** has the better of the argument. PRO offers a plausible vendor‑veto story but does not rebut the core “if redundant, costs nothing” inference.
- **Operational dependency (C026, C065, C081, C085):** **PRO** scores meaningful points by showing that dependency is real and that continued use is compatible with phased mitigation. This, however, does not cure the statutory misfit.
- **Democratic accountability (C039–C041, C082–C084):** **CON** persuasively leverages bipartisan SASC opposition as evidence that the designation is outside the zone of action Congress expected under § 3252, even if letters are not themselves law.

It may reflect genuine concerns about dependency and mission assurance, but the choice of § 3252 as the vehicle, the way the designation was paired with secondary‑boycott rhetoric, and the refusal to codify acknowledged legal limits in writing make it, in my judgment, an illegitimate use of national‑security authority.
