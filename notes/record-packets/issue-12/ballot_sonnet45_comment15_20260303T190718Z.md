## DECISION RATIONALE

The motion was: "Resolved: The Pentagon's designation of Anthropic as a supply-chain risk under 10 USC § 3252, and the broader demand that AI contractors remove use restrictions, represents a **legitimate** exercise of national security authority."

CON wins on three decisive grounds:

### 1. **Statutory Authority / Major Questions Doctrine (Most Important)**

CON established that §3252's statutory text requires risk of "sabotage" or "subversion" by adversaries [C019, C034]. Declining contract terms in a domestic contract dispute does not constitute adversarial sabotage. PRO argued §3252 is flexible case-by-case authority [C032], but CON showed Congress explicitly legislated via Section 889 [C088-C091] for government-wide bans on actual foreign adversaries (Huawei, ZTE).

The secondary boycott affecting Amazon, Google, and Microsoft [C050] represents a government-wide supplier ban on a domestic company—exactly the kind of major policy decision that requires clear Congressional authorization under the Major Questions Doctrine. PRO conceded the secondary boycott was "their hardest point" but didn't provide statutory authority for third-party coercion beyond DoD's own procurement.

**This ground alone is sufficient for CON to prevail.**

### 2. **C072 Double Bind (Second Most Important)**

CON's most rhetorically powerful argument: Pentagon verbally acknowledged certain uses would be unlawful, then refused to write it down. PRO's vendor-veto explanation (written commitments create private enforcement rights) is plausible, but CON's Closing reframe—"if redundant, costs nothing"—went unanswered.

If the Pentagon truly would never do those things (as it verbally acknowledged), a written commitment acknowledging existing legal limits costs nothing and provides no new private enforcement rights—it's just stating what the law already is. Refusal to commit to one's own acknowledged legal limits creates an inference that preserving optionality was the goal.

PRO never engaged this specific "costs nothing" framing in their Closing.

### 3. **Factual Record Contradiction (Third Ground)**

CON distinguished *Trump v. Hawaii* persuasively: that case involved a formal record that *supported* the stated national security rationale. Here, the formal record *contradicts* the designation:
- Government used Claude for Iran strikes hours after designation [C081]
- Used Claude for Venezuela operations weeks before [C085]
- Officials confirmed restrictions "never affected a single government mission" [C065]

A designation based on "supply-chain risk" that the government immediately violates by continued operational use cannot satisfy even *Hawaii*'s deferential standard.

PRO's "dependency IS risk" flip was clever and logical—they argued continued use proves dependency, which proves concentration risk. But this doesn't resolve the contradiction: if Anthropic is such a serious sabotage risk that §3252 designation is warranted, how can the military trust it for combat operations hours after designation? The flip proves *dependency*, but undermines the *sabotage/subversion* statutory predicate.

### 4. **Democratic Accountability (Supporting Ground)**

CON's A9, citing bipartisan SASC condemnation [C082-C084] from Wicker (R), Reed (D), McConnell (R), Coons (D)—"those most responsible for overseeing military procurement"—was extremely strong. PRO responded that "letters aren't law" and Congress still funds §3252, but this doesn't address:
- Youngstown Category 3 implications (executive action contrary to Congressional will merits lowest deference)
- Major Questions Doctrine requirement that executive needs *affirmative* authorization for novel exercises of power, not just absence of restriction

CON's constitutional structure argument—executive needs affirmative authorization, not Congress to affirmatively restrict—is sound.
