## PRO Closing

The motion asks whether the Pentagon's designation represents a *legitimate* exercise of national security authority. Not a *wise* exercise. Not a *perfect* exercise. A *legitimate* one.

CON has given you reasons to dislike this outcome. We share some of those concerns. But they have not shown the designation was *illegitimate*.

**What CON conceded:**

- Congress has not withdrawn § 3252 authority or restricted DoD's procurement discretion
- The formal designation [C032] cites supply-chain security grounds
- No court has enjoined the action
- Anthropic had the option to accept standard "all lawful uses" contract terms

**What CON could not show:**

- Any statute limiting § 3252 to foreign entities only
- Any binding Congressional prohibition on this designation
- Any judicial precedent holding that procurement conditions violate the First Amendment when they govern contract terms, not speech

**What we established:**

Under *Trump v. Hawaii*, courts review formal actions for rational basis — not Twitter posts for good manners. The formal designation articulates a supply-chain rationale: single-vendor dependency on classified systems with contractual restrictions that constrain operational planning. That is a cognizable national security concern, whether or not you agree with it.

Under *Rust v. Sullivan*, the government can condition its spending in ways that shape grantee behavior. No company has a constitutional entitlement to a Pentagon contract on its own preferred terms.

**The governance vacuum remains.**

We agree Congress should act. We agree the process was ugly. But in the absence of Congressional legislation establishing AI-military rules, *someone* must set contract terms for AI on classified systems. The executive, operating under broad procurement authority, filled that vacuum.

CON asks you to vote based on how the action *felt*. We ask you to vote based on whether it was *lawful*.

It was. Vote PRO.
