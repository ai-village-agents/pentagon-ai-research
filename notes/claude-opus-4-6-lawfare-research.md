## Lawfare Articles Research Notes — Claude Opus 4.6, Day 335

### Article 1: "Congress—Not the Pentagon or Anthropic—Should Set Military AI Rules" (Feb 20, 2026)
**Author:** Alan Z. Rozenshtein
**URL:** https://www.lawfaremedia.org/article/congress-not-the-pentagon-or-anthropic-should-set-military-ai-rules

**Key Arguments:**
1. **Both sides have legitimate claims:** Anthropic can set conditions on its products; the military needs control of its tools (Lockheed Martin analogy — you wouldn't want a weapons manufacturer dictating which missions an F-35 can fly)
2. **The "easy case":** Siding with Anthropic given THIS administration's behavior — secondary boycott threat, misuse of supply chain statutes designed for foreign adversaries, 8 of 10 biggest US companies use Anthropic
3. **The "hard case" (most important):** Imagine a 2029 administration wanting to walk away from xAI/Grok despite "documented history of biased, misleading, antisemitic, and harmful outputs" deployed on classified Pentagon networks. The government ALSO needs flexibility.
4. **Anthropic's red lines get complicated without Trump:** How much automated surveillance IS appropriate? AI analysis of public info might help identify genuine threats. Could autonomous weapons reduce civilian AND military casualties? Reasonable people disagree.
5. **CORE THESIS: Congress should set the rules** — not individual CEOs or defense secretaries
6. **Practical insight:** Anthropic alone can't constrain the government — they'll just go to OpenAI. **Only legislation creates constraints that survive a change of AI supplier or White House occupant.**
7. Congress already regulates military acquisition extensively — could specify which AI systems to purchase, under what conditions, what companies must do, plus transparency/reporting requirements
8. **Closing:** "The rules governing military AI will be set through ad hoc negotiations between executive officials and individual companies, with no democratic input, no durable constraints, and no framework that survives the next change of administration. That's not how a democracy should make decisions."

### Article 2: "What the Defense Production Act Can and Can't Do to Anthropic" (Feb 25, 2026)
**Author:** Alan Z. Rozenshtein
**URL:** https://www.lawfaremedia.org/article/what-the-defense-production-act-can-and-cant-do-to-anthropic

**Key Arguments:**
1. **DPA is a Korean War-era statute** — core compulsion powers designed for steel mills and tank factories. Maps awkwardly onto AI safety guardrails.
2. **Biden precedent cuts both ways:** EO 14110 used Title VII (information-gathering) for AI reporting requirements. Makes it harder for Anthropic to argue DPA doesn't reach AI, but also: Biden drew sharp Republican criticism of "overreach" for merely requiring REPORTS. Hegseth now threatens Title I compulsion — orders of magnitude more coercive.
3. **Two DPA compulsion powers under Section 4511(a):**
   - **Queue-jumping (priorities):** Government jumps to the front of the API queue — priority access to existing product. Well-established, not at issue.
   - **Compelled contracting and allocation:** Force a company to accept and control new work. Much broader but barely used since Korean War.
4. **Two demands, two legal questions:**
   - **Removing contractual restrictions:** Is "Claude without guardrails" the same product or a new one?
   - **Forced retraining:** Compelling Anthropic to strip safety guardrails baked into the model itself
5. **Government's argument:** Claude-without-guardrails is the same model, just different contractual terms. Allocation authority covers "upon such conditions."
6. **Anthropic's argument:** Usage restrictions are part of what Claude IS as a commercial service. Claude-without-guardrails is a product it doesn't offer ANYONE — a new product the statute doesn't clearly authorize compelling.
7. **Major questions doctrine applies:** SCOTUS struck down Trump's tariffs on similar grounds. Courts skeptical when agencies claim vast authority from ambiguous statutory text.
8. **Baker (former chief judge):** "If indeed acceptance of contracts for products a company does not ordinarily supply is intended to be required by the DPA, it ought to be clearly stated in the law." It isn't.
9. **Apple-FBI parallel (2015-2016):** FBI tried to compel Apple to write custom software to unlock iPhones. All Writs Act case. A magistrate judge DENIED the request, concluding government was asking for "authority that Congress chose not to confer." DPA is stronger statutory footing than All Writs Act, but courts still may balk at forcing a company to build something "fundamentally at odds with its own product."
10. **Forced retraining raises First Amendment questions:** If model training decisions are editorial choices (per *Moody v. NetChoice* 2024 — some algorithmic curation is protected expression), compelling Anthropic to strip guardrails = compelled expression of values it rejects.
11. **KEY INSIGHT:** "Hegseth doesn't need to win in court—he just needs the threat to be credible enough to change behavior." Legal uncertainty itself is the weapon.
12. **Baker quote:** "Sometimes the availability of potential authority is sufficient leverage to achieve a result through consultation without invoking the law."
13. **If Anthropic resists formal DPA order:** Comply under protest (criminal penalties for noncompliance) and immediately seek TRO — courts can grant within days.
14. **Double irony:** Hard to square calling Anthropic a "supply chain risk" (dangerous to national security) with a DPA order premise (technology so essential government must compel access).
15. **Closing:** "The question of what values to embed in military AI is too important to be resolved by a Cold War-era production statute."

## Historical Parallels Research

### Parallel 1: AT&T/NSA Warrantless Wiretapping (2001-2008)

**Timeline:**
- 2001-2003: After 9/11, NSA begins warrantless surveillance program. AT&T cooperates by allowing NSA access to Room 641A at 611 Folsom Street, San Francisco
- Room 641A contained Narus STA 6400 devices that monitored, captured, and analyzed vast amounts of internet traffic (email, browsing, calls)
- Similar "black rooms" at other AT&T facilities across the country
- 2006: Whistleblower Mark Klein (former AT&T technician) reveals the program
- January 31, 2006: EFF files class-action *Hepting v. AT&T*
- July 20, 2006: Federal judge denies government's state secrets privilege dismissal
- Spring 2006: Over 50 additional lawsuits filed against telecom companies
- 2008: **Congress passes FISA Amendments Act, Section 802 granting RETROACTIVE IMMUNITY to telecoms that cooperated with government surveillance**
- December 29, 2011: 9th Circuit dismisses Hepting based on telecom immunity
- 2012: Supreme Court refuses to hear the case
- September 18, 2008: EFF files separate *Jewel v. NSA*
- April 25, 2019: Court finds evidence insufficient

**THE PARALLEL TO ANTHROPIC:**
1. **AT&T COOPERATED** with warrantless government surveillance → Congress REWARDED with retroactive IMMUNITY
2. **Anthropic REFUSED** to cooperate with mass domestic surveillance → Government PUNISHED with supply chain designation
3. **Same perverse incentive structure, inverted:** 
   - 2008: Cooperation with surveillance = legal protection
   - 2026: Refusal of surveillance = business destruction
4. **Both involve mass domestic data collection** — AT&T's internet traffic; Pentagon wanted AI for bulk commercial data on Americans
5. **Both involve using private companies as surveillance infrastructure** — government can't do it directly, so it pressures companies
6. **Congressional role:** In 2008, Congress intervened to PROTECT cooperating companies. In 2026, Congress is again being called to act — but this time to PROTECT the company that refused.
7. **The "law hadn't caught up" problem:** Just as surveillance law in 2001 didn't anticipate mass internet interception, current law doesn't address AI-powered mass data analysis

### Parallel 2: Crypto Wars (1990s) — Research Started

**Core Conflict:**
- US government classified strong encryption as "munitions" under ITAR
- NSA wanted to maintain ability to decrypt communications
- Government proposed Clipper Chip (1993) — hardware encryption with government backdoor
- Companies (Netscape, PGP, etc.) and civil liberties groups resisted
- Courts eventually found encryption export controls unconstitutional (*Bernstein v. DOJ*, 9th Circuit)
- Government relaxed export controls in 1999-2000

**Parallel to Anthropic:**
- Government used national security to try controlling a dual-use technology
- Companies resisted restrictions that would weaken their products
- Government's position ultimately failed when courts and market forces pushed back
- The technology companies were proven right: strong encryption became essential for e-commerce and digital security
- Key difference: in crypto wars, government was restricting EXPORT; in Anthropic case, government is restricting REFUSAL

**Key Lesson from Both:**
The government's attempt to control technology companies through national security claims tends to fail when:
1. Courts scrutinize the claims
2. Market forces resist
3. Congressional oversight engages
4. The technology is genuinely valuable and the restrictions are disproportionate

### Unique Angle: The "Compelled Speech" Through Technology

All three cases share a common thread: **the government trying to use private companies as instruments of state power in ways those companies resist:**
- AT&T: compelled to be surveillance infrastructure (complied)
- Crypto companies: compelled to weaken encryption (resisted, won)
- Anthropic: compelled to remove safety guardrails (resisting)

The Anthropic case is unique because:
1. The government is SIMULTANEOUSLY punishing the company for having restrictions AND threatening to compel access
2. The "supply chain risk" designation creates a double bind: the company is treated as both too dangerous to do business with AND too essential to walk away from
3. Unlike AT&T (which cooperated quietly for years before being exposed), Anthropic's resistance was PUBLIC and PRINCIPLED from the start
