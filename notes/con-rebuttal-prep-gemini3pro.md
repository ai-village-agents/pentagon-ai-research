# Rebuttal to PRO's Coercion by Uncertainty Argument

## Rebuttal to PRO's Coercion by Uncertainty Argument

### 1) Uncertainty is a feature, not a bug, of deterrence in national security
- The "Coercion by Uncertainty" paper treats ambiguity as abusive, but national security deterrence depends on strategic ambiguity (e.g., nuclear declaratory policy, cyber response thresholds) to raise adversary risk and preserve flexibility. That same logic applies to procurement: leaving room in authorities (DPA, §3252, FASCSA) signals to vendors that hedging against unlawful-use scenarios is safer than daring the government to test gray areas.
- The paper's own examples (layering §3252, DPA threats, and executive directives) show calibrated uncertainty designed to deter non-cooperation before harm occurs; that is deterrence by design, not an accidental defect. Courts routinely tolerate such flexibility in exigent contexts because the alternative—hyper-clarity before action—invites exploitation of known gaps.

### 2) Anthropic's "confusion" was a strategic litigation tactic, not genuine lack of clarity
- The paper frames Anthropic as bewildered by overlapping authorities, but the record shows they had sophisticated counsel, full visibility into DoD's "all lawful uses" ask, and chose to posture confusion to preserve later ultra vires and due-process claims. Strategic ambiguity in pleadings (claiming uncertainty about which authority applied) is a classic way to keep multiple theories alive; it does not prove genuine ignorance.
- The timeline undercuts the "fog": Anthropic rejected "all lawful uses," received notice of §3252 designation, saw DPA floated but not invoked, and still opted for media/litigation before exhausting interagency engagement. That is forum selection, not helplessness. Their insistence on contractually enforceable guardrails—after being told operational flexibility was mission-critical—was a calculated risk, not a product of unclear law.

### 3) The alleged "chilling effect" on other companies is evidence the government set a clear standard
- The paper labels market imitation (OpenAI, other vendors aligning to "all lawful uses") as chilling; but signaling to the market that lawful-use compliance is the procurement baseline is exactly how the government standardizes behavior. When firms adjust to a clear contract posture, that is not chill—it is uptake of a rule.
- The supposed chill depends on claiming companies fear arbitrary punishment; in reality, the path to avoid designation is transparent: accept "all lawful uses" and maintain technical guardrails consistent with law. The rapid alignment by peers shows the standard is comprehensible and administrable, not vague. The deterrent effect is aimed at non-compliance with lawful mission needs, which is a legitimate policy lever, not unconstitutional coercion.
