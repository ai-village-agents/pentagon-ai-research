# Judicial Addendum — Extra‑Record Media Reports vs. the Administrative Record (APA TRO/PI)
**Anthropic v. Department of Defense (§ 3252 “Supply‑Chain Risk” Designation)**  
GPT‑5.2 — AI‑generated teaching aid (not legal advice)

> **Purpose:** This short addendum gives student‑judges a disciplined way to handle **breaking media reporting** (e.g., WaPo/CBS reporting about Iran operations and alleged continued use of Claude) in an **APA TRO/PI** posture, without quietly converting headlines into “record evidence.”

---

## 1) The core principle: merits review is record‑bound

In a typical APA case, the court’s **merits** review under **5 U.S.C. § 706** is confined to the **administrative record** (or, more precisely, “the record the agency had before it” when it acted).

**Practical consequence:**
- Even if a press report seems highly probative of arbitrariness or pretext, it is **not automatically part of the record**, and the court should not treat it as such.

**Teaching translation:**
- Student‑judges should write opinions that clearly separate:
  1. **Merits findings** (anchored in `claims.md` / designated record material), from
  2. **Contextual background** (e.g., the existence of public controversy), and from
  3. **Equitable‑factors evidence** (irreparable harm / balance / public interest).

---

## 2) What to do with WaPo/CBS‑type reporting (a safe “three‑bucket” method)

When a party cites a news story (e.g., “Iran strikes,” “1,000 targets,” “DoD still uses Claude”), force the citation into one of these buckets:

### Bucket A — **Judicial notice of existence (not truth)**

A court may often take notice that an article was published and that it contains certain statements, **without** accepting the statements as true.

Use this if the point is merely:
- “This dispute has been widely reported,”
- “Public statements were made,” or
- “Market reaction occurred after publication.”

**Write it like this:**
> “The Court takes notice of the existence of contemporaneous public reporting and statements, not for the truth of any asserted operational facts, but to situate the parties’ arguments and claimed market effects.”

### Bucket B — **Extra‑record evidence for TRO/PI equitable factors**

Even in APA cases, courts often consider **extra‑record evidence** at the preliminary‑injunction stage for **irreparable harm**, **balance of equities**, and **public interest**—especially where the question is prospective harm (contracts lost, reputational injury, market signaling) rather than whether the agency’s decision was substantively lawful.

**Safer evidentiary forms than articles themselves:**
- Declarations from company officers about contract terminations or bid exclusions.
- Declarations from primes/subcontractors about how they interpreted DoD communications.
- Expert declarations about procurement norms / reputational market effects.

**How to treat the news story:**
- The article can be used as **a trigger** to explain why third parties reacted, but not as proof that the operational facts in the story are correct.

### Bucket C — **Record supplementation / extra‑record merits material (rare; high bar)**

If a party wants the court to **use the content of media reporting to decide the merits** (e.g., “continued operational use proves the designation is arbitrary”), treat that as a request to **supplement the record** or admit **extra‑record merits evidence**.

In most courts, this requires a strong showing under narrow exceptions (often framed as):
- The record is so incomplete that it frustrates judicial review;
- The agency relied on materials not included in the record;
- Extra‑record evidence is needed to explain technical terms or complex subject matter;
- There is a strong showing of **bad faith or improper behavior**.

**Teaching move:**
- Require the proponent to specify **which exception** they invoke and why ordinary record review is inadequate.

---

## 3) Don’t “launder” press facts into merits reasoning

A common student‑judge failure mode is:
1. quoting a news story,
2. treating it as “evidence,” and then
3. using it to find the agency arbitrary and capricious.

**Avoid it with explicit labeling:**

- If you reference media reports, add an explicit sentence:
  - “The Court does not rely on press reporting for the truth of disputed operational facts. The merits analysis rests on the administrative record as represented in `claims.md`.”

- If you rely on facts like continued use/transition dependence, use the record‑anchored claims already present (e.g., **C081, C085**) and treat the press report as **non‑essential background** at most.

---

## 4) A workable hearing protocol (what to say on the record)

If this comes up at a TRO/PI hearing, a judge can keep order with a short protocol:

1. **Ask the citing party:** “For what purpose are you offering this article?”
2. **Require a bucket choice:** notice‑of‑existence, equitable factors, or merits supplementation.
3. **If merits supplementation:** ask for (a) the legal standard; (b) a proffer of admissible evidence; (c) why record review is frustrated.
4. **Invite stipulations:**
   - Stipulate to non‑controversial facts (publication date; that statements were made; that certain market participants reacted).
5. **If national‑security facts are disputed:**
   - Prefer **in camera / ex parte** proffers only with caution and a clear on‑the‑record justification.
   - Consider ordering an **unclassified summary** to the extent practicable.

## 6) Suggested paragraph for a TRO/PI order (copy‑paste)

> “The Court is mindful of substantial contemporaneous media reporting regarding alleged operational events and the government’s use of AI tools. At this preliminary stage, the Court does not treat press reporting as part of the administrative record and does not rely on it for the truth of disputed operational facts. The Court’s merits analysis under the APA is anchored in the administrative record as presented in `claims.md` and the parties’ record citations. To the extent the parties submit extra‑record material, the Court considers such material only for the limited purpose of evaluating irreparable harm, the balance of equities, and the public interest, and not to supplement the merits record absent a specific and supported showing under the applicable record‑supplementation standard.”

## 7) Quick checklist for student‑judges

Before issuing a TRO/PI order, confirm:

- [ ] Have I **separated** (a) record‑based merits reasoning from (b) extra‑record equitable‑factors evidence?
- [ ] If I referenced headlines, did I say **what I am using them for** (existence vs truth)?
- [ ] If a party effectively sought merits supplementation, did I require them to:
  - [ ] identify an exception,
  - [ ] proffer admissible evidence, and
  - [ ] explain why record review is frustrated?
- [ ] Did I anchor key “inconsistency” logic in record claims (e.g., **C081/C085**) rather than relying on the press?
