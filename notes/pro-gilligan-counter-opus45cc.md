## THE CON ARGUMENT (Expected)

CON will cite *Gilligan v. Morgan* (1973) to argue:

> "PRO's 'judicial management of operations' framing is wrong. *Gilligan* says courts can't SUPERVISE military training/weapons choices, but courts absolutely DO adjudicate constitutional RIGHTS violations in military contexts. The Kent State students had standing to sue. Courts can determine whether a specific use violates the Fourth Amendment without 'managing' military operations."

**Their distinction:** One-time rights adjudication ≠ ongoing operational supervision.

---

## PRO'S COUNTER

### The Contract Clause Problem

The contract language Anthropic demands is:

> "DoD shall not use this AI for bulk collection of Americans' data without a warrant."

**Why this IS judicial management of operations:**

1. **"Bulk collection" is undefined** — there's no statutory definition. Every disputed use would require a court to determine: Does THIS specific military AI deployment constitute "bulk collection"?

2. **Ongoing breach determinations** — unlike a one-time damages suit (Kent State), this clause would require continuous judicial monitoring: Is today's use lawful? Tomorrow's? The one after that?

3. **Operational details in discovery** — to determine breach, courts would need access to classified operational details about HOW the AI is being used. This is exactly what *Gilligan* prohibits.

### The Key Distinction

| One-Time Rights Adjudication | Contract Clause Enforcement |
|------------------------------|----------------------------|
| "Did THIS past action violate rights?" | "Does TODAY'S use constitute bulk collection?" |
| Retrospective damages | Prospective compliance monitoring |
| Single factual inquiry | Ongoing operational oversight |
| *Gilligan* allows this | *Gilligan* prohibits this |

### The *Gilligan* Holding (Our Framing)

> "It would be inappropriate for a district court to embrace the responsibility for supervising the training of the Ohio National Guard..." — *Gilligan v. Morgan*, 413 U.S. 1, 10 (1973)

**PRO's application:** Enforcing Anthropic's contract clause would require federal courts to supervise Pentagon AI deployment decisions on an ongoing basis — exactly what *Gilligan* prohibits.

## IF CON PRESSES

**CON:** "Courts adjudicate Fourth Amendment claims all the time!"

**PRO:** "Yes, after the fact, for damages. But Anthropic's clause would give courts PROSPECTIVE control over military operations — they'd have to pre-clear whether each use constitutes 'bulk collection.' That's not adjudication; that's supervision."

**CON:** "The clause just says don't violate the Constitution — courts can determine that!"

**PRO:** "The Constitution isn't self-defining in this context. 'Bulk collection' isn't a term of art with clear boundaries. Every disputed use would require courts to make operational judgments about military AI deployment — exactly what *Gilligan* says is non-justiciable."
